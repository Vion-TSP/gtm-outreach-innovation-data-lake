Tactic 9.5 - Creative / Guerrilla

Office Lunch Drop

Cater lunch to a target office with a branded note. Gets your brand literally on their table and creates a memorable positive experience. Works especially well for teams of 10-50 people where you can create impact without overspending.

Effort: Low Cost: Medium ICP: Sales Leaders, Founders
01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Food creates instant positive associations. When you deliver lunch to an entire office, you're not just reaching one decision-maker - you're creating brand awareness across the whole team. People talk about the lunch ("Who sent this?"), share photos on Slack, and remember your name because you fed them.

The psychology behind lunch drops

  • Reciprocity at scale - Feeding a team creates collective obligation. Multiple people feel appreciative, increasing the chances someone takes action
  • Positive emotional anchoring - Food = happiness. Your brand gets associated with the positive feeling of a free meal, especially during busy workdays
  • Social proof internally - When 20 people eat lunch from your company, they talk about it. Your target sees colleagues discussing you, creating social proof before you even pitch
  • Multi-stakeholder reach - You reach decision-makers and influencers simultaneously. The VP might not respond to cold email, but their team definitely remembers who bought them lunch

Why this works for mid-sized teams

Lunch drops are most effective for offices of 10-50 people. Small enough that your budget can cover everyone (creating fairness and goodwill), large enough that you reach multiple stakeholders including the decision-maker. Bigger than 50 gets expensive fast, smaller than 10 feels less impactful.

The format advantage: Unlike one-to-one gifts (which can feel transactional), feeding a team feels generous and community-minded. It's harder to say no to someone who just fed your entire team.

Step-by-step process

StepActionDetailsTime Investment
1 Identify Target Office Choose a company where you have a warm lead or high-value prospect. Ideal office size: 10-50 people. Check LinkedIn for team size and office location 10 min (research)
2 Confirm Office Address and Size Verify office address (Google, LinkedIn company page). Confirm they're not fully remote. Estimate team size to order the right amount of food (call reception if unsure) 5-10 min
3 Order Catering Use Deliveroo for Business, Just Eat for Business, or UberEats for Business. Order enough for the full team plus some extra. Schedule delivery for lunch time (12-1 PM). Choose crowd-pleasers (pizza, sandwiches, salads with variety) 15-20 min (ordering)
4 Include Branded Note Add a printed note or card with delivery. Message: "Lunch is on us today. Hope you enjoy! - The team at Acme." Keep it light and non-salesy. No hard pitch 5 min (note creation)

Pro tip: Coordinate lunch drop with a relevant trigger event (new funding, product launch, team expansion). This makes the gesture feel timely and thoughtful rather than random. Example: "Congrats on the Series B! We wanted to help fuel your growth - lunch is on us."

Best Practices

▼

Food Selection (Variety, Dietary Restrictions, Crowd-Pleasers)

▼

Why food choice matters

Order the wrong food and people can't eat it (dietary restrictions), don't like it (too niche), or it arrives cold/stale (poor quality). The gesture falls flat. Order crowd-pleasers with variety and accommodate common dietary needs.

Safe food choices for office catering

Food TypeWhy It WorksWhat to Order
Pizza Universal appeal, easy to share, stays warm Mix of meat and veggie options. At least one vegan pizza. Avoid overly niche toppings. Estimate 2-3 slices per person
Sandwich platters Variety, accommodates dietary needs, individually wrapped Mix of meat, veggie, and vegan options. Label clearly. Include sides (crisps, fruit, dessert)
Salad boxes Health-conscious, customizable, travels well Mixed protein options (chicken, falafel, tofu). Dressing on the side. Include non-salad option for variety
Asian food (sushi, poke bowls, Chinese) Modern, shareable, Instagram-able Mix of vegetarian and meat options. Avoid anything too spicy or unusual. Include rice/noodle bases for variety

Dietary accommodation checklist

  • Vegetarian options - At least 30% of order should be vegetarian (higher in cities)
  • Vegan options - Include at least 2-3 clearly labeled vegan items
  • Gluten-free - One GF option if possible (salads, GF pizza base)
  • Allergen labeling - Include note listing common allergens if possible (nuts, dairy, gluten)
Rule of thumb: Order 10-15% more than estimated headcount. Better to have leftovers than run out - running out makes you look cheap and kills the goodwill. Leftovers can be taken home or eaten later, maintaining positive impression.

Timing and Logistics (Schedule for Lunchtime, Confirm Delivery)

▼

Optimal delivery timing

Deliver too early (10 AM) and it sits around getting cold. Too late (2 PM) and people already ate. Sweet spot is 12-12:30 PM when people are getting hungry but haven't ordered food yet.

Delivery coordination tips

  • Schedule in advance - Book delivery 24h ahead when possible. Day-of ordering risks delays or unavailable restaurants
  • Add delivery instructions - "Lunch delivery for Acme team. Please bring to reception and ask for Sarah." Helps driver navigate office buildings
  • Get phone contact - Provide recipient's phone number so driver can call if lost. Prevents food sitting outside or being sent back
  • Track delivery - Most platforms show live tracking. Monitor to ensure it arrives on time. Be ready to troubleshoot if delayed

Note and Messaging (Light Touch, No Hard Sell)

▼

What to include in the note

The note delivered with food should be simple, warm, and non-salesy. This is a goodwill gesture, not a pitch. Save the pitch for the follow-up email.

Good vs bad note examples

Bad (Too Salesy)Good (Light Touch)
"Lunch from Acme! We automate workflows for companies like yours. Book a demo: acme.com/demo" "Enjoy lunch on us! Hope your team has a great day. - The team at Acme"
"Fuel up and check out how we can save you 10 hours per week. Visit our website to learn more!" "Congrats on the recent funding! Lunch is on us to celebrate. Enjoy! - Acme"

Note format options

  • Printed card - Include a small branded card with the delivery. Simple message, logo, maybe QR code to website (no hard CTA)
  • Delivery note - Add note in delivery platform's "special instructions" field. Driver delivers it with food
  • Separate letter - If you can coordinate, have someone drop off a handwritten note separately (more personal, but higher effort)

Follow-up email same day

Subject: "Lunch should be arriving any minute"

Body: "Hi Sarah, we sent lunch for your team today - should arrive around 12:30. Hope you all enjoy it! I know you're busy scaling sales right now, and thought your team deserved a break. No strings attached, but I'd love to chat about how we're helping teams like yours with X. Let me know if you have 15 min this week. Enjoy the meal! - James"

Rule of thumb: The note on the food should be 100% warm and generous. The follow-up email can include a soft CTA (calendar link, request for meeting), but still keep it light. Hard selling after a lunch drop feels transactional and destroys goodwill.

When NOT to use this tactic

  • Fully remote teams - No office = nowhere to deliver. Waste of money
  • Teams over 50 people - Too expensive, impact gets diluted across too many people who won't remember who sent it
  • Low ACV accounts - If account is worth less than $10,000 annually, lunch drop ROI doesn't justify cost
  • No decision-maker in office - If your target contact doesn't work from that office, the gesture doesn't reach them effectively

How to measure success

  • Response rate - Track how many lunch drops lead to replies or meetings within 7 days
  • Internal sharing - Monitor if team posts about the lunch on LinkedIn or internal Slack (sign of positive reception and extended reach)
  • Deal conversion - Track long-term if lunch drop accounts convert at higher rates than cold outreach accounts
Rule of thumb: Spend no more than 2% of expected account value on lunch drop. A $20,000 ACV justifies $200-400 spend. A $100,000 enterprise deal could justify $1,000+ (but at that point, consider more targeted gifts vs team lunch).

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Wrong address (they work remotely or moved offices)

You send food to company HQ but the team works hybrid or from a different office. Food arrives to an empty office or wrong location. Money wasted, zero impact.
Verify office location and confirm team is actually there that day. Check LinkedIn for hybrid work mentions. For high-value accounts, call reception beforehand: "Is the sales team in office on Thursday?"

2. Dietary restrictions not considered (no veg/vegan options)

You order only meat options. Half the team can't eat it. Instead of goodwill, you create frustration and make people feel excluded.
Always include 30% vegetarian and 2-3 clearly labeled vegan options. Add gluten-free if possible. Order variety so everyone can eat something. When in doubt, pizza with veggie options is safest.

3. Note too salesy (kills the generous gesture)

Your note includes product pitch, demo links, or hard CTA. Recipients see it as a marketing stunt, not a genuine gift. Goodwill evaporates.
Keep note simple and warm: "Enjoy lunch on us! - The team at Acme." No pitch, no CTAs, no links. Save the ask for follow-up email. Let the food do the talking.

4. Food quality issues (arrives cold, wrong items, poor quality)

You order from unreliable restaurant or cheap options to save money. Food arrives cold, wrong items, or tastes bad. Creates negative brand association instead of positive.
Order from well-rated restaurants on platform. Check reviews. Don't cheap out - better to send great food to fewer people than mediocre food to more people. Quality over quantity.

5. No follow-up (send lunch and wait passively)

You send lunch but never email or call afterward. Team enjoys the food, but no one takes action because you didn't create a next step.
Email same day lunch arrives: "Hope your team enjoyed lunch! Would love to chat about X if you have 15 min this week." Follow up next day if no response. The lunch opens the door; follow-up walks through it.

6. Targeting too-large offices (50+ people, cost balloons, impact dilutes)

You send lunch to a 100-person office. Costs $800, most people don't know who sent it or why, decision-maker doesn't notice. Money wasted, minimal impact.
Target offices of 10-50 people max. Sweet spot is 20-30. Small enough to be memorable and create real goodwill, large enough to reach multiple stakeholders. Over 50 people, use targeted gifts instead.
