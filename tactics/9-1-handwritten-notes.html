Tactic 9.1 - Creative / Guerrilla

Handwritten Notes at Scale

Use robotic-pen services to send personalised handwritten notes that stand out in a digital-first world. Physical mail cuts through inbox noise and drives strong close rates with C-suite and founder ICPs.

Effort: Low Cost: Low-Medium ICP: C-suite, Founders
01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: C-suite executives receive hundreds of emails per day and zero handwritten notes per month. A physical, handwritten card creates a pattern interrupt that forces attention. Even though it's robotic pen technology, the tactile experience triggers reciprocity psychology in ways digital never can.

The psychology behind handwritten notes

  • Pattern interrupt - Physical mail is so rare in B2B that it stands out immediately. Executives open handwritten envelopes at near 100% rates vs 20-30% email open rates
  • Effort signal - A handwritten note signals you invested time and thought, even if robotic. This creates obligation to reciprocate (reply or take meeting)
  • Novelty factor - Most prospects have never received outbound mail from vendors. The novelty alone makes them remember you
  • Personal connection - Handwriting feels intimate and personal in a way typed text never does. It bypasses professional armour

Why robotic pen services matter

True handwriting doesn't scale past 5-10 notes per week. Robotic pen services use real pens controlled by robotics to write your message in realistic handwriting styles. The result passes the eye test and maintains the psychological impact while scaling to hundreds of notes per month.

The format advantage: Unlike gifts or packages (which can be rejected by gatekeepers or feel too salesy), a simple handwritten note on quality cardstock feels thoughtful and non-threatening. It's personal without being pushy.

04

Best Practices

▼

Message Structure (Short, Personal, Non-Salesy)

▼

The ideal note structure

Handwritten notes must be brief (under 100 words) because longer messages look robotic and feel impersonal. Structure: personal greeting, insight or value, soft CTA, signature.

Good vs bad examples

Bad (Too Salesy)Good (Value-First)
Hi Sarah, I work at Acme and we help companies like yours automate workflows. I'd love to schedule 15 minutes to show you our platform. Let me know when you're free. Hi Sarah, I noticed Datadog just raised Series C. Congrats. Scaling GTM post-raise is tough. I wrote a guide on avoiding common mistakes. Sending it separately. Happy to chat if useful.

Tone and language tips

  • Write like a human - Use contractions (I'd, you're, we've). Avoid corporate jargon. Sound like you're writing to a colleague, not a prospect
  • Lead with them, not you - Start with something about them (recent news, insight about their company), not about your product
  • No hard CTA - Don't ask for a meeting in the note. Soft CTAs work better: "Happy to share more if this is relevant" or "Let me know if you'd like the full report"
  • Signature matters - Sign with first name only, not title. Add a PS if you want to stand out further
Rule of thumb: If your note could be sent as a cold email and sound normal, it's too formal. Handwritten notes should feel more personal and conversational than any email you'd send.

Personalisation Points (Company News, Mutual Connections, Pain Points)

▼

Why generic notes fail

If your note could be sent to 100 people with no changes, it won't convert. Executives can spot templated outreach instantly. Personalisation is non-negotiable for handwritten notes to work.

High-impact personalisation sources

  • Recent company news - Funding rounds, product launches, executive hires, office expansions. Reference these in opening sentence: "Saw you raised Series B last month. Congrats!"
  • Mutual connections - LinkedIn shows shared connections. Mention them: "I work with James at Datadog and he mentioned you're scaling GTM." Always verify the connection is real and relevant
  • Content they've shared - If they post on LinkedIn, reference a recent post: "Loved your take on AI in sales. Totally agree on X."
  • Specific pain points - Use job postings, tech stack, or industry trends to infer challenges: "Noticed you're hiring SDRs. Scaling outbound is tough right now with deliverability changes."

Timing and Batching (Small Batches, Track Delivery, Follow Up Digitally)

▼

Why small batches matter

Sending 200 notes at once is a mistake. You can't track what's working, quality suffers, and follow-up becomes overwhelming. Start with batches of 25-50 to test messaging, track results, and iterate.

Batch size recommendations

Team SizeBatch SizeFrequencyRationale
Solo SDR or founder 10-15 notes Weekly Allows deep personalisation and manageable follow-up
Small team (2-3 SDRs) 25-30 notes Weekly Enough volume to test messaging, still trackable
Larger team (5+ SDRs) 50-75 notes Bi-weekly Divides across team members, maintains quality

Delivery tracking and follow-up timeline

  • Day 0 - Send batch of notes via service
  • Day 3-5 - Notes delivered (most services take 3-5 business days). Send follow-up email referencing the note
  • Day 7-10 - If no response to email, send LinkedIn connection request with note reference
  • Day 14 - Final touchpoint: short email or LinkedIn message checking if they received the note
Pro tip: Use tracking services (some platforms offer this) or include a unique URL/QR code on each note to see when recipients engage. This tells you when to follow up digitally for maximum impact.

Card Design and Quality (Professional, Not Gimmicky)

▼

Why card quality matters

A cheap-looking card undermines your message. Thin cardstock, garish designs, or overly branded cards scream "mass mail" and get discarded. Premium cardstock and simple design signal thoughtfulness.

Design best practices

  • Cardstock weight - Use 110lb or heavier cardstock. Anything thinner feels cheap
  • Colour choices - Stick to neutral tones (white, cream, light grey). Avoid bright colours or busy patterns
  • Branding - Minimise logos. Small logo on back of card is fine. Front should be clean and unbranded
  • Envelope style - Use quality envelopes that match card. Handwrite recipient address (robotic pen can do this too). No window envelopes

Good vs bad card designs

Bad Design ChoicesGood Design Choices

Large logo dominating front of cardSmall logo on back, or no logo at all

Rule of thumb: Your card should look like something you'd send to a friend or colleague, not a marketing piece. When in doubt, go simpler and more elegant.
05

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Generic message (no personalisation)

You send the same templated message to everyone. Recipients can tell it's mass outreach and discard it immediately.
Add 1-2 custom sentences per note based on company news, mutual connections, or specific pain points. Spend 3-5 min researching each prospect before writing.

2. Too salesy (pitching product in the note)

You use the note to pitch features or ask for a meeting. This feels transactional and defeats the personal touch.
Focus on providing value or insight, not selling. Soft CTAs only: "Happy to share more if relevant." Save the pitch for the follow-up email or call.

3. Bad handwriting font (looks obviously robotic)

You choose a handwriting style that's too perfect or too uniform. Recipients spot it as fake and the impact is lost.
Test multiple handwriting styles. Choose one that looks natural with slight imperfections. Scribeless and Bond have the most realistic options. Order samples before bulk sending.

4. No follow-up (sending note and hoping for reply)

You send the note and wait passively. Most recipients don't respond to mail alone, even if they liked it.
Follow up 3-5 days later with an email referencing the note: "Sent you a note last week about X. Easier to respond here." Include calendar link. Multi-touch is essential.

5. Sending to wrong address (recipient works remotely or moved offices)

You send to company HQ but the executive works remotely or from a different office. Note never reaches them.
Verify addresses before sending. Use LinkedIn to check if they mention remote work. For high-value prospects, send a quick email first: "I have something to send you. Still at [address]?" Or use home addresses (available via data enrichment services) for C-suite.

6. Sending too many at once (can't track or follow up effectively)

You send 200 notes in one batch. Follow-up becomes overwhelming, you can't tell what's working, and quality suffers.
Start with batches of 25-50 notes maximum. Track delivery dates, follow up systematically, measure response rates. Iterate messaging before scaling up.
