Tactic 4.2 - Content-Led Outreach

Co-Created Industry Research Report

Partner with complementary companies to produce original research about your shared ICP. Survey-driven insights create credible content that attracts organic traffic, earns backlinks, and positions both partners as thought leaders. Strong lead magnet and PR opportunity.

Co
Content (Primary)
Effort: High Cost: Medium-High ICP: C-suite, VP-level, Strategic Buyers
01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Original research cuts through noise. Everyone publishes opinions. Few publish data. When you survey hundreds of practitioners and publish findings, you create a credible asset that journalists cite, peers share, and prospects download. Co-creation splits costs and doubles distribution by leveraging both partners' audiences.

The psychology of research-driven content

  • Authority by data - Original research positions you as the source, not just a commentator. Media outlets prefer citing primary research over opinion pieces
  • Credibility through numbers - Survey data from real practitioners carries more weight than case studies or anecdotes. Buyers trust peer insights over vendor claims
  • Shareability of insights - Surprising or controversial findings get shared organically. People tag colleagues and post screenshots of compelling stats
  • Long-tail SEO value - Research reports rank for industry terms and attract backlinks. One report can drive organic traffic for years

Why co-creation amplifies impact

Partnering with a complementary company (non-competing but same ICP) splits the effort and cost while doubling reach. Each partner promotes to their audience, drives survey responses from their network, and shares the report with their channels. The combined credibility of two brands makes the research more trustworthy than a solo effort.

The compound effect: Research reports often get picked up by industry publications, referenced in other content, and cited in sales conversations. This creates a flywheel where the report continues generating leads and building authority long after publication.

Best Practices

▼

Topic Selection (Controversial or Novel Findings Get Coverage)

▼

What makes a research topic compelling

The best research topics challenge assumptions or reveal something unexpected. If your findings just confirm what everyone already believes, no one will share them. Pick topics where you expect to uncover surprises, contradictions, or data that challenges conventional wisdom.

Topic categories that work

  • Emerging trends - New behaviours or shifts that haven't been quantified yet. Example: "How AI adoption is actually changing sales workflows in 2026"
  • Common misconceptions - Topics where industry assumptions don't match reality. Example: "Why most outbound teams fail (it's not what you think)"
  • Underreported challenges - Problems everyone faces but no one talks about publicly. Example: "The hidden costs of GTM tool sprawl"
  • Peer benchmarking - Data showing what top performers do differently. Example: "What separates high-growth sales teams from the rest"

Vetting your topic

QuestionGood SignWarning Sign
Has this been researched before? Either never researched, or last study was 2+ years ago Multiple recent reports on same topic. Hard to differentiate
Will findings surprise people? You expect counterintuitive or controversial results Findings will likely confirm what everyone already thinks
Does your ICP care? Topic addresses a top-3 pain point for your target buyers Topic is interesting but not directly tied to buyer priorities

Pro tip: Run a LinkedIn poll testing 2-3 potential topics before committing. The topic with most engagement signals what your ICP cares about. Use poll comments to refine specific angles or questions to include.

Finding the Right Partner (Complementary Audience, Shared ICP)

▼

Why partner selection matters

The wrong partner creates misaligned incentives, uneven effort distribution, and diluted messaging. The right partner doubles your reach, splits costs, and makes the research more credible. Look for companies where collaboration benefits both sides equally.

Ideal partner criteria

  • Same ICP, different product - You both sell to the same buyers but solve different problems. Example: email deliverability tool partners with copywriting AI tool
  • Similar company stage - Roughly equal brand presence and audience size. Avoids power imbalance where one partner does most promotion
  • Aligned values and quality bar - Partner cares about producing high-quality research, not just quick lead gen. Check their existing content quality
  • Complementary networks - Partner has audience in channels you don't (different communities, geographies, or buyer personas within your broader ICP)

How to pitch potential partners

Reach out to companies you already have relationships with (customers, integration partners, community members). Explain the mutual benefit: split costs, doubled reach, shared credibility. Propose specific topic and rough timeline. Emphasise equal partnership (co-branded, equal promotion effort).

Partner pitch example Subject: Research partnership idea - GTM tool adoption study Hi Sarah, We're planning a research report on how GTM teams actually adopt and use new tools. Thought you'd be a great co-creation partner given we share similar audiences (Series A/B sales leaders) but complementary products. The idea: Survey 200+ sales leaders on tool evaluation, implementation challenges, and what drives adoption vs abandonment. Publish co-branded report with both our logos and distribution. Why partner: - Split survey design and analysis effort - Both promote to our networks (doubles reach) - More credible with two brands vs solo effort - Lead gen for both of us via gated landing page We handle survey platform and landing page. You handle report design. Equal promotion commitment from both sides. Interested in exploring? Happy to jump on a call. Best, James
Red flag partners: Avoid companies that want you to do all the work while they just slap their logo on it, partners in adjacent but slightly competitive spaces (creates tension over messaging), or partners with much larger audiences (they may not promote as hard because they get less value).

Survey Design (Keep Under 15 Questions)

▼

Why brevity matters

Survey completion rates drop dramatically after 10 minutes. Every additional question reduces your final response count. Better to get 300 responses to 12 focused questions than 100 responses to 30 questions. Quality over quantity.

Question design principles

  • Start with easy questions - Demographics and basic info first. Build momentum before harder questions. Don't start with open-ended essay questions
  • Mix question types - Multiple choice for quantitative data, rating scales for measuring intensity, open-ended for quotes and qualitative insights. Avoid too many open-ended (respondent fatigue)
  • Avoid leading questions - "Don't you think X is a problem?" biases responses. Neutral framing: "How significant is X in your workflow?" with scale
  • Include "Other" options - Multiple choice should always have "Other (please specify)" to catch unexpected responses and avoid forcing bad fits
  • Test for clarity - Send draft survey to 5-10 people. If they're confused by a question, rewrite it. Ambiguity kills data quality

Sample survey structure

SectionQuestionsPurpose
Demographics (3-4 questions) Company size, role, industry, revenue/stage Segmentation for analysis. Easy warm-up questions
Behaviour and practices (5-7 questions) What they currently do, tools they use, frequency, processes Core data. Multiple choice and scales. Easy to visualise
Challenges and pain points (2-3 questions) Biggest obstacles, what's not working, frustrations Reveals gaps and opportunities. Mix scales with open-ended
Future outlook (1-2 questions) Plans for next 6-12 months, interest in new approaches Forward-looking data. Shows where market is heading
Pro tip: Include one optional open-ended question at the end: "Any additional thoughts on this topic?" This captures passionate responses and often provides the best quotes for the report without forcing everyone to answer.

Sample Size (200+ for Credibility)

▼

Why sample size matters

Small sample sizes (under 100) look anecdotal. Media won't cover research based on 50 responses. Buyers question credibility. Aim for 200+ responses minimum for B2B research. 500+ is ideal but not always achievable without incentives or large audiences.

Strategies to hit 200+ responses

  • Email both partner lists - This is your primary distribution channel. Send to full list, not just engaged segments. Multiple reminders (3-4 emails over 2 weeks)
  • Offer incentive - Gift card raffle (10 winners of $50 cards), donation to charity per response, or early access to findings. Incentives can double response rates
  • Post in communities - LinkedIn groups, Slack communities, Reddit, industry forums where your ICP hangs out. Personalise asks rather than spamming links
  • Direct outreach to key contacts - Both partners DM 50-100 key connections personally asking them to complete and share. Personal asks convert at much higher rates
  • Partner with industry associations - Some associations will share surveys with their members if the research benefits the industry. Worth exploring

Response rate expectations

Pro tip: Display live response count on survey landing page: "Join 247 sales leaders who've already shared their insights." Social proof increases completion rates. Update the number weekly during distribution period.

Report Design (Executive Summary, Key Stats, Visual Charts)

▼

Why design quality matters

Data without good design doesn't get shared or read. Visual hierarchy, charts, and layout determine whether busy executives skim your report or ignore it. Invest in design. A beautifully designed 15-page report outperforms an ugly 50-page report every time.

Report structure

  • Cover page - Title, both partner logos, publication date, eye-catching visual
  • Executive summary (1 page) - 5-7 bullet points covering top findings. Busy people read only this page
  • Methodology (0.5 page) - Sample size, demographics breakdown, survey period. Builds credibility
  • Key findings (8-12 pages) - One finding per page or spread. Large stat callouts, supporting charts, brief analysis. Mix charts with pull quotes from open-ended responses
  • Conclusion and implications (1-2 pages) - What this means for the industry, recommendations, future outlook
  • About the partners (1 page) - Brief bios of both companies, no sales pitch

Visual design principles

  • Hierarchy matters - Large callout stats, medium charts, small body text. Guide reader's eye to most important data first
  • Limit colours - Use 2-3 brand colours plus neutrals. Too many colours look chaotic and unprofessional
  • Whitespace is your friend - Don't cram every inch with content. Breathing room makes reports easier to read and more premium feeling
  • Consistent chart styles - Pick bar charts, pie charts, or line graphs and stick with consistent styling throughout. Inconsistency looks amateurish
Pro tip: Create a 2-page ungated summary PDF with headline stats and methodology. Share this freely on social media. Full report requires email for download. This balances reach (shareable summary) with lead capture (gated full version).

Multi-channel launch approach

Launch week determines whether your report gets traction or dies quietly. Coordinate across PR, social, email, and events simultaneously. Both partners must commit to active promotion. One partner going silent kills momentum.

Gating Strategy (Ungated Summary + Gated Full Report)

▼

The gating dilemma

Fully ungated reports get maximum reach and shares but capture zero leads. Fully gated reports capture leads but get minimal organic distribution because no one can access and share them. The solution: hybrid approach with ungated summary and gated full report.

Hybrid gating model

  • Ungated 2-page summary - Key findings, top stats, methodology, partner logos. Freely shareable on social, in emails, in communities. Generates awareness and drives people to landing page
  • Gated full report - Complete 15-25 page report with all data, charts, quotes, and analysis. Requires name, email, company to download. Hosted on landing page with form
  • Progressive profiling - If using marketing automation, ask for basic info (name, email) on first download, then ask for more details (role, company size, challenges) on subsequent asset downloads

Landing page best practices

  • Above fold - Report title, 2-3 headline stats as teasers, download form (keep fields minimal - name, email, company only), both partner logos
  • Below fold - What's inside the report (table of contents), sample page previews, testimonials or quotes from early readers if available
  • Thank you page - After form submission, redirect to thank you page with immediate PDF download link. Don't make people wait for email. Also send email with download link as backup
  • Lead distribution - Agree upfront how leads are shared between partners. Common: both partners get all leads, or split by region/company size, or each partner gets leads that match their ICP criteria
Pro tip: On the landing page, include "As featured in..." logos for any media coverage you secured. Social proof increases download conversions. Update this section as you get media hits post-launch.

Ongoing Repurposing (Blog Series, Social Posts, Webinar, Infographics)

▼

Why repurposing compounds value

Launch week reaches maybe 10-20% of your potential audience. The other 80% missed it or need multiple exposures before they download. Repurposing spreads your research insights over months, reaching different audience segments through different formats and channels.

Repurposing content formats

  • Blog series (5-8 posts) - Each post deep-dives on one finding from the report. More detail than report provides. Good for SEO. Link to full report download in each post
  • LinkedIn carousel posts - Turn key stats into visual slide decks (8-12 slides). Carousels get high engagement on LinkedIn. Create 4-6 different carousels covering different findings
  • Infographics - One-page visual summary of top 5-7 stats. Highly shareable. Post on LinkedIn, Twitter, and embed in blog posts
  • Video explainer - 3-5 minute video walking through key findings. Use screen recording with voiceover showing report pages, or talking head format. Post on LinkedIn and YouTube
  • Podcast episode - If either partner has a podcast, dedicate an episode to discussing findings. Conversational format makes data more accessible
  • Quote graphics - Pull standout quotes from open-ended responses. Design simple quote cards. Post individually on social over several weeks

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Topic too broad or too niche

Too broad means findings are generic and unsurprising. Too niche means you can't get enough responses or no one cares about the results.
Pick topics specific enough to generate novel insights but broad enough to attract 200+ responses. Test topic appeal via LinkedIn poll before committing.

2. Too few survey responses

Publishing research based on 50-75 responses looks anecdotal and unscientific. Media won't cover it. Buyers question credibility.
Don't launch until you hit 200+ responses. Extend survey period, add incentives, or both partners do more promotion to hit threshold before publishing.

3. Boring or expected findings

If the data just confirms what everyone already thinks, there's no reason to share or discuss it. Report gets ignored.
Design survey to uncover surprises. Ask questions that might challenge conventional wisdom. If initial data looks boring, dig deeper into segments or outliers.

4. Bad report design

Ugly or dense layout means no one reads past page one. Data dumps without visual hierarchy lose readers immediately.
Invest in professional design or use Canva templates. Prioritise visual hierarchy, whitespace, and charts over dense text. Make it skimmable.

5. No launch plan

You finish the report and just email it to your list with no PR, no webinar, no social strategy. Report dies quietly without traction.
Plan launch at least 2 weeks before publishing. Coordinate PR pitches, both partners' social posts, email announcements, webinar, all timed together for maximum impact.

6. Gating everything (kills organic reach)

If the full report is gated from day one with no shareable summary, no one can read and share it organically. Reach stays minimal.
Create ungated 2-page summary for sharing. Gate full report for lead capture. Hybrid model balances reach and lead generation.

7. No repurposing (one-and-done)

You launch the report, promote for one week, then move on. Miss the opportunity to extract months of content value from the research.
Create content calendar for 8-12 weeks post-launch. Blog series, carousels, infographics, videos. Repurpose findings into multiple formats across channels.
