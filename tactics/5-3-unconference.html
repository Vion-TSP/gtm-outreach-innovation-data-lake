Tactic 5.3 - Event-Based Plays

Unconference / Open Space

Host participant-driven events where attendees set the agenda on the day. Create flexible structure for self-organising discussion groups around emergent topics. Attendees vote with their feet, moving between conversations that interest them most, creating organic peer learning environment.

Effort: Medium Cost: Low-Medium ICP: Technical Buyers, Founders, Ops Leaders
01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Traditional conferences impose agenda from on high that rarely matches what attendees actually want to discuss. Unconferences flip the model - attendees propose topics they care about and self-organise into discussions. This creates genuine engagement because every conversation is guaranteed relevant to people in the room. When you facilitate emergent learning, you become enabler of valuable connections rather than controller of content.

The psychology behind unconferences

  • Ownership and agency - When attendees set agenda, they invest more deeply. Their topics, their conversations, their outcomes. This creates psychological investment traditional events cannot match
  • Law of two feet - Core rule of unconferences: if you are not learning or contributing, move to different conversation. This permission to leave removes social pressure to sit through boring sessions
  • Emergent relevance - Agenda forms based on what people actually want to discuss in that moment, not what organiser predicted months ago. Real-time agenda means real-time relevance
  • Peer credibility - No staged speakers on pedestals. Everyone is peer. Knowledge sharing is horizontal not hierarchical. Technical practitioners especially value learning from equals over vendor presentations

Why unconference format specifically

Traditional conferences require massive upfront investment in speakers, content, and production. Unconferences work with minimal infrastructure because attendees bring the content. For B2B companies, this format attracts practitioners who want to solve problems collaboratively rather than passive consumers of vendor content.

The compounding effect: Attendees leave unconference with network of peers who share their specific challenges. When they need help in future, they contact peers from unconference. When you facilitated those connections, you remain associated with that valuable network.

04

Best Practices

▼

Opening and Principles - Set the Right Tone

▼

Explaining the unconference model

Most attendees have never experienced unconference format. First 15 minutes are critical for setting expectations and getting buy-in. Clear explanation prevents confusion and enables participation.

Core principles to communicate

  • Whoever comes are the right people - Small group deep discussion beats large group shallow listening. Do not wait for more people or wish for different people. Right people are the ones who showed up
  • Whatever happens is the only thing that could have - Release attachment to predetermined outcomes. Trust emergent process. Best conversations cannot be scripted in advance
  • Whenever it starts is the right time - Creativity and learning happen when they happen, not on schedule. Do not waste energy waiting for perfect moment
  • When it is over, it is over - If discussion reaches natural conclusion in 20 min instead of 45, finish early. Do not artificially extend. People appreciate efficiency
  • Law of two feet - If you are not learning or contributing, move to different session. No obligation to stay. This rule is essential for energy management

Opening sequence

TimeActivityPurpose
0-5 min Welcome and context Why we are here. What makes unconference different. What to expect today. Build excitement for format
5-10 min Explain principles and process Go through core principles above. Explain agenda-building process. Clarify mechanics of session participation. Answer questions
10-30 min Collect and organise topics Attendees propose discussion topics via Slido or shared doc. Group similar proposals. Vote on priorities if needed. Build time grid
30-35 min Review agenda and logistics Show final agenda. Explain breakout room mechanics. Confirm note-taking approach. Launch first session round
Managing uncertainty anxiety: Some participants feel uncomfortable without predetermined agenda. Acknowledge this explicitly: "Uncertainty is intentional. Best discussions emerge from real-time needs. Trust the process - it works." Give them permission to embrace ambiguity.

Topic Proposal Process - Democratic Agenda Formation

▼

How to collect topics

Make topic submission easy and low-friction. Remove barriers to participation. You want flood of proposals not trickle. Quantity generates options, voting creates prioritisation.

Topic submission methods

  • Slido Q&A mode - Attendees submit topics as questions. Others upvote topics they want to discuss. Democratic sorting. Visual feedback creates momentum
  • Shared Google Doc - Create simple bulleted list. Everyone adds topics. Lower tech barrier than Slido but less visual. Good for smaller groups
  • Miro board - Virtual sticky notes on board. Visual and collaborative. Can drag to group similar topics. More engaging but requires comfort with tool
  • Live verbal collection - Call on people to propose topics aloud. You capture on screen. More personal but slower. Best for groups under 20

Organising submitted topics

ChallengeSolutionExample
Too many similar proposals Cluster and combine Five people proposed AI tool topics - combine into "Evaluating and implementing AI tools in workflows"
Topics too broad Ask proposer to narrow "Sales enablement" becomes "Creating playbooks AEs actually use for technical products"
More topics than time slots Vote to prioritise Use Slido upvotes or quick poll. Top voted topics get slots. Rest go to parking lot for optional extra time
Topics not relevant to group Gentle redirect "That is interesting but might be too specific for this group. Happy to connect you with Sarah 1-on-1 after if relevant to both."
Seeding the agenda: If topic submission is slow, have 2-3 backup topics ready to propose yourself. Once first few topics are on board, others feel permission to contribute. Do not leave awkward silence during collection phase.

Session Facilitation - Light Touch Guidance

▼

Your role as organiser

You are not session leader or content expert. You are space-holder and facilitator of process. Create conditions for good conversation and get out of the way. Trust participants to facilitate their own sessions.

What you do during sessions

  • Float between breakout rooms - Pop into each room briefly to check energy. Stay if intervention needed, leave if flowing well. Presence shows you care but do not hover
  • Support struggling facilitators - If session proposer looks lost, offer quick guidance: "Try going around for quick takes from everyone" or "What is one specific question we can dig into?" Then leave again
  • Watch timing and transitions - Give 5-min warnings before sessions end. Bring everyone back to main room between rounds. Keep momentum high with clear transitions
  • Encourage room-switching - Remind people of law of two feet. "Do not stay in room that is not serving you. Check out other discussions." Give permission to move

What participants do in sessions

RoleResponsibilitySupport Needed
Topic proposer Set context and facilitate discussion. Not lecture or present. Ask questions and guide conversation Brief pre-event message with facilitation tips. Emphasise discussion over presentation
Note-taker Capture key points in shared doc. Themes, insights, questions, action items. Not transcript Provide note template. Clarify level of detail expected. Thank them publicly for volunteering

Handling empty rooms: If session gets zero or one attendee, do not panic. Proposer can join different session or have deep 1-on-1 if one person shows. Empty session is data - topic was not as relevant as expected. No shame in it.

Post-Event Synthesis - Creating Lasting Value

▼

Why synthesis matters

Unconference creates burst of valuable conversation but ephemeral nature means insights get lost. Your synthesis captures and amplifies value. This is where you earn long-term credibility - taking messy discussions and finding signal.

What to include in synthesis

  • Themes across sessions - What common threads emerged? What surprised you? What tensions or debates surfaced repeatedly? Show you were paying attention to patterns
  • Key insights per topic - Brief summary of each discussion topic. Main takeaways. Interesting approaches people shared. Do not attribute to individuals unless they gave permission
  • Unanswered questions - Capture questions that came up but were not resolved. This creates springboard for future conversations or content. Shows intellectual honesty
  • Resources and tools mentioned - If people recommended specific tools, frameworks, or articles, compile them. Useful reference for attendees and shows you listened

Synthesis timeline and format

TimingDeliverablePurpose
Within 24 hours Thank you email with link to session notes Immediate follow-up while energy is fresh. Provide raw notes so participants can review what was captured
Within 48 hours Synthesis document with themes and insights Your value-add as organiser. Show you extracted signal from noise. Create shareable artifact participants can reference and forward
Within 1 week Optional blog post or video recap Public-facing content that attracts future participants. Demonstrates thought leadership. Creates SEO asset. Only if you have bandwidth
Amplification strategy: Ask attendees to share synthesis on LinkedIn with their take on most valuable insight. User-generated social proof. Tag participants who contributed key ideas (with permission). Builds visibility for future events.
05

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Over-controlling the agenda

Defeating the purpose of unconference by pre-determining topics or steering away from emergent themes. Attendees feel manipulated and participation drops because you have removed their agency.
Trust the process. Let attendees genuinely set agenda even if topics diverge from what you expected. Your job is facilitation not control. Embrace unexpected directions - often most valuable.

2. Not explaining format clearly enough

Attendees arrive expecting traditional event structure. Confusion and frustration when reality does not match expectations. People disengage because they do not understand what they are supposed to do.
Over-communicate format in advance and again at start. Explicitly contrast with traditional events. Explain principles and mechanics thoroughly. Give examples. Answer questions before launching.

3. Too few or too many participants

Under 15 people and not enough diversity for multiple sessions. Over 50 and logistics become overwhelming. Sweet spot missed means format cannot work as designed.
Target 20-35 participants for first unconference. This supports 3-4 parallel sessions with meaningful groups. Can scale up with experience but start smaller. Quality over quantity.

4. Clunky tech causing friction

If moving between breakout rooms is confusing or Slido is not working, participants get frustrated and disengage. Technical friction kills energy that format depends on.
Test all tech thoroughly before event. Have backup plans. Keep tech stack simple - Zoom plus Google Doc is sufficient. Brief participants on mechanics before launching first round. Have tech support ready.

5. No note capture means lost insights

Amazing conversations happen but nothing documented. Ephemeral value only. Cannot create synthesis or share insights afterward. Opportunity for lasting impact wasted.
Assign volunteer note-takers to each session. Provide note template. Collect all session notes into shared folder. Use this raw material for your synthesis. Document process makes value visible and shareable.

6. Forgetting to reinforce law of two feet

People sit in dying conversations out of politeness. Energy drains from event. Sessions with few engaged participants limp along unproductively.
Explicitly give permission to move multiple times during event. "If this session is not serving you, check out another room. No offense taken." Model this yourself by moving between rooms.
