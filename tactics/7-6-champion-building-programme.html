Tactic 7.6 - ABM Plays

Champion Building Programme

Systematically identify and equip internal champions at target accounts with content, ROI data, presentations, and coaching to help them sell your solution internally. Turn engaged mid-level contacts into powerful advocates who navigate bureaucracy and build consensus on your behalf.

Effort: Medium Cost: Low ICP: Mid-level Managers, RevOps, Marketing Managers
01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Most enterprise deals have an internal champion whether you intentionally cultivate one or not. The champion either succeeds or fails at building internal consensus. By proactively equipping champions with tools and coaching, you dramatically increase their success rate, which directly increases your close rate.

The psychology behind champion programmes

  • Ownership and investment - When someone champions your solution internally, they become psychologically invested in its success. Helping them win makes them help you win. Mutual incentive alignment
  • Internal credibility - Champions are trusted colleagues not external salespeople. Their recommendations carry more weight than yours. Peer influence beats vendor pitches in enterprise decision-making
  • Political navigation - Champions understand internal politics, who has influence, where landmines exist, and how decisions really get made. You cannot navigate this from outside. Champions can
  • Effort reciprocity - When you invest significant effort helping someone succeed internally, they feel obligated to reciprocate with effort, advocacy, and information sharing. Relationship depth matters

Why champion building specifically

Natural champions exist but often lack resources to sell effectively internally. They believe in your solution but do not have ROI analysis, cannot answer technical objections, and struggle presenting to executives. Systematic champion building turns willing but under-equipped advocates into powerful internal salespeople.

The force multiplier: One equipped champion can reach 10 internal stakeholders you will never get meetings with. They attend internal meetings you cannot access. They continue selling when you are offline. Champion programmes scale your selling capacity beyond your direct reach.

Best Practices

▼

Identifying Right Champion - Look for Influence Not Title

▼

What makes an effective champion

Not everyone who likes your solution can champion it effectively. Good champions combine personal motivation with organisational influence and political skills. Wrong champion wastes your investment and leads to stalled deals even when they try hard.

Champion characteristics to look for

  • Personal motivation - Do they have skin in the game? Will successful implementation make their job easier or advance their career? Champions driven by personal benefit work harder than those doing favours
  • Political capital - Are they respected internally? Do people listen when they speak? New employees or those with low credibility struggle to champion regardless of enthusiasm
  • Access to decision makers - Can they get meetings with executives? Do they have regular touchpoints with budget holders? Champions without access to power cannot drive decisions
  • Communication skills - Can they articulate value proposition clearly? Present to executives? Handle objections? Strong communicators are more effective champions
  • Persistence and follow-through - Do they follow up on commitments? Building internal consensus requires consistent effort over months. Champions who go quiet after initial enthusiasm cannot close deals

Champion screening questions

QuestionWhat It RevealsGood Answer
"How are purchase decisions like this typically made here?" Understanding of internal process and their role in it Detailed explanation showing they have navigated this before and understand the landscape
"Who else needs to be involved in evaluating this?" Awareness of buying committee and willingness to multi-thread Names specific stakeholders and explains their concerns. Offers to facilitate introductions
"What would successful implementation mean for you personally?" Personal motivation beyond business benefits Specific answer about career goals, performance metrics, or job satisfaction improvements

Warning sign: If potential champion says "just send me information and I will share it around" without asking questions about how to position it or who to approach, they likely lack political skills or motivation to truly champion. Real champions want coaching not just materials.

Champion Toolkit - Give Them Everything They Need

▼

Why toolkits matter

Champions want to help but lack materials to sell effectively internally. They cannot build ROI analysis from scratch. They do not know how to handle technical objections. Comprehensive toolkit removes friction and increases champion effectiveness dramatically.

Essential champion toolkit components

  • Executive presentation deck - 10-15 slide deck champion can present to their leadership. Include problem statement, solution overview, ROI, case studies, implementation timeline. Make it fully editable so they can customise
  • ROI calculator or business case - Pre-built financial analysis showing expected return. Ideally customised with their company data. Champions struggle building financial business cases from scratch. Do it for them
  • Objection handling guide - Document addressing common objections with recommended responses. "What if someone asks about security?" Here is how to answer. Anticipate concerns and prep champions
  • Technical FAQ - Answers to technical questions that will come up from IT or Engineering stakeholders. Integration details, security certifications, architecture diagrams. Champion cannot answer technical questions alone. Give them resources
  • Comparison sheet - How you compare to alternatives they will evaluate. Be honest about trade-offs. Help champion position you accurately not just positively
  • Case studies from similar companies - Stories from companies in their industry or similar size. Champions need social proof to show peers having success

Toolkit delivery format

Create shared Notion workspace, Google Drive folder, or simple webpage containing all materials. Make it easy to navigate and find specific resources. Update as new materials become available or objections arise. Give champion one URL to access everything.

Customisation matters: Generic toolkit is better than nothing, but customised toolkit for their specific company is 10x more effective. Use their company name in presentations. Reference their specific pain points. Include data relevant to their situation. Customisation shows investment and increases champion confidence.

Coaching Strategy - Teach Them How to Sell Internally

▼

Why coaching beats just providing materials

Champions are not professional salespeople. Giving them great materials without teaching them how to use those materials leads to mediocre internal selling. Coaching transforms willing advocates into effective internal salespeople who navigate politics and build consensus.

Key coaching areas

  • Stakeholder mapping - Help champion identify all stakeholders who need to approve. Discuss each person's priorities, potential objections, and how to approach them. Build strategy for building coalition of support
  • Pitching to executives - Role-play presenting to their C-suite. Practice concise elevator pitch. Teach them to lead with business outcomes not features. Help them anticipate and handle executive-level questions
  • Handling objections - Go through likely objections and practice responses. Not just what to say but how to say it confidently. Build champion's capability to think on their feet in internal meetings
  • Building consensus - Teach political skills for building internal support. Who should they approach first? How to get early wins to build momentum? When to escalate versus resolve conflicts directly?
  • Timeline and milestones - Create realistic internal project plan with milestones. When should they present to executives? When to involve procurement? Having plan reduces champion anxiety and increases follow-through

Coaching session structure

Session 1: Stakeholder strategy - Map buying committee, identify champion's relationship with each, plan approach for building support across stakeholders

Session 2: Executive pitch practice - Champion presents using your materials. Give feedback on messaging, pacing, and objection handling. Iterate until confident

Ongoing: Office hours - Offer standing availability for quick questions as champion navigates process. "Text me before your CFO meeting and we can prep for 10 minutes."

Coaching mindset: Position yourself as their advisor not someone extracting work from them. "I want to help you succeed in getting this approved internally. What obstacles do you foresee?" Frame coaching as helping them win not pushing your sale.

Staying Connected - Support Through Long Buying Cycle

▼

Why continuous support matters

Enterprise buying cycles last 3-9 months. Champions lose momentum when left alone. Obstacles arise that discourage them. Continuous support keeps champion engaged and helps them overcome hurdles that would otherwise kill the deal.

Support cadence

  • Weekly check-ins - Short 15 minute calls or Slack messages. "What happened this week? Any obstacles? Who should we talk to next?" Maintain momentum and show ongoing investment
  • Before key meetings - Prep sessions before champion presents to executives or key stakeholders. Review talking points, anticipate questions, boost confidence
  • After setbacks - When deals hit obstacles such as budget freezes or internal politics, reach out immediately. Help champion regroup and adjust strategy. Many deals die here if champion feels unsupported
  • Celebrations of milestones - When champion gets approval from key stakeholder or clears major hurdle, acknowledge and celebrate. Recognition reinforces their effort and maintains motivation

Support do's and don'ts

Do ThisNot This
Ask "How can I support you this week?" Demand "What is your timeline for getting this approved?"
Provide additional resources when objections arise Say "We already gave you everything you need"
Offer to join internal meetings when helpful Insist on attending every meeting because you do not trust champion
Check in when champion goes quiet to understand obstacles Assume champion ghosting means lack of interest and deprioritise
Persistence pays: Many enterprise deals close in months 6-9 of evaluation after multiple setbacks. Champions who feel supported through difficult periods stick with it. Champions who feel abandoned move on to easier wins. Your sustained support directly impacts deal closure rates.

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Choosing wrong champion - picking based on enthusiasm not influence

You pick junior person who loves your product but has no political capital or access to decision makers. Despite their enthusiasm, they cannot move the deal forward.
Assess political influence and access to power not just enthusiasm. Look for mid-level managers with credibility, communication skills, and track record navigating internal approvals. Influence matters more than title.

2. Not giving them tools to sell internally

You expect champion to build business case, create presentations, and answer technical questions on their own. They lack resources and struggle to sell effectively internally.
Create comprehensive champion toolkit with exec presentation, ROI calculator, objection guides, technical FAQ, and case studies. Make it easy for champion to sell without you. Remove all friction.

3. Losing touch during long buying cycle

You engage champion initially then go quiet for months. Champion loses momentum, encounters obstacles without support, and deal stalls or dies from neglect.
Weekly check-ins throughout buying cycle. Stay engaged, provide ongoing support, help overcome obstacles, celebrate milestones. Sustained relationship drives deals to closure. Do not go dark.

4. Not understanding their internal politics

You do not invest time learning about champion's political landscape, who has power, where landmines exist. Champion navigates blindly without your strategic guidance.
Deep dive on internal politics early. Who are allies? Who are skeptics? What is history of similar initiatives? How do decisions really get made? Use this intelligence to coach champion's internal strategy.

5. Pushing too hard - making champion uncomfortable

You pressure champion for constant updates, demand they move faster, or criticise their internal selling approach. Champion feels used not supported. Relationship deteriorates.
Frame relationship as helping them win not extracting work from them. Ask "How can I support you?" not "Why is this taking so long?" Be patient with internal processes. Make champion feel like you are on their team.

6. Not coaching them - just dumping materials

You send champion pile of documents and presentations expecting they know how to use them effectively. No coaching on how to pitch, handle objections, or navigate politics. Materials alone are not enough.
Role-play executive presentations. Practise objection handling. Coach on stakeholder strategy and consensus building. Teach champion how to sell internally. Coaching multiplies material effectiveness by 10x.
