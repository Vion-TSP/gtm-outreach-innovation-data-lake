Tactic 5.4 - Event-Based Plays

Conference Piggyback Events

Host side events during major industry conferences to capture attendees already in town. Create intimate breakfast, lunch, or evening gatherings that offer more value than conference content. Leverage existing travel investment while avoiding massive conference sponsorship costs.

Effort: Medium Cost: Medium ICP: All personas
01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Industry conferences concentrate your target buyers in one location but official conference content is often disappointing and expensive sponsorships deliver poor ROI. Side events capture attendees when they are already in town, in learning mode, and looking for valuable conversations beyond crowded expo halls. You get conference access without conference costs.

The psychology behind conference piggybacks

  • Sunk cost leverage - Attendees already invested in flights and hotels. Adding breakfast or dinner requires minimal additional commitment. They are present and available
  • Conference fatigue relief - After hours of expo hall noise and vendor pitches, intimate side event feels like oasis. Quality over quantity positioning
  • FOMO and exclusivity - Limited-capacity side event feels more valuable than open-access conference session. Invitation signals status and importance
  • Network efficiency - Conference attracts right people but networking in crowds is inefficient. Your event creates structured connection opportunity they crave

Why piggyback specifically

Running standalone event requires convincing people to travel. Piggyback eliminates that barrier completely. Conference marketing machine has already done work of assembling your target audience in one place. You simply provide better experience at fraction of cost.

The compounding effect: Regular piggyback presence at key conferences builds recognition. "Oh yes, they always host that breakfast at this conference" becomes brand association. You own a moment in conference calendar without owning the conference.

04

Best Practices

▼

Conference Selection - Choose Strategically

▼

Not all conferences are created equal

Piggyback strategy only works if conference attracts genuine concentration of target buyers. Vendor-heavy conferences with few practitioners waste your investment. Do research before committing.

Selection criteria

  • Attendee composition - What percentage are potential buyers vs vendors and press? Look for practitioner-heavy events not vendor expos. Check past attendee lists or speaker rosters
  • Decision-maker presence - Will people with budget authority attend or mostly junior staff? C-suite and VP-heavy conferences justify investment in side event
  • Geographic accessibility - Is conference in city where you have team presence or customers you can invite? Local element adds value and reduces your costs
  • Timing in buying cycle - Events in Q4 when budgets are set are less valuable than Q1-Q2 when buyers are actively evaluating. Consider fiscal year timing

Conference types and fit

Conference TypePiggyback FitWhy
Vertical industry event Excellent Concentrated target audience. Easy to identify attendees. Your side event provides cross-company peer learning they cannot get on expo floor
Functional role summit Very good Sales leaders summit or CFO forum attracts exact personas. Homogenous audience makes content relevant to everyone. High decision-maker concentration
Mega vendor event Mixed Huge attendance but diffuse audience. Hard to stand out among hundreds of side events. Can work if you tightly filter invites to specific subset
Academic or research conference Poor Attendees rarely have buying authority. More interested in theory than practice. Side event unlikely to generate pipeline
Cost-benefit calculation: Budget £2000-5000 for venue, food, and logistics. If conference has 50+ qualified prospects attending, cost per attendee at your event drops to £100-250. Far cheaper than booth sponsorship or paid speaking slot.

Venue and Logistics - Proximity and Quality

▼

Location is critical

Attendees will not trek across city for your event. Walking distance from conference venue or hotel cluster is non-negotiable. Anything requiring taxi or long walk kills attendance.

Venue requirements

  • Within 10-min walk - Ideally same building or adjacent. Maximum 10-min walk from main conference venue or convention center. Check actual walking route not just map distance
  • Private or semi-private space - Separate room or sectioned area. Needs to feel distinct from public dining. Creates intimacy and enables networking
  • Appropriate capacity - Space should feel full not empty. If expecting 20 people, book for 25 not 50. Packed room has better energy than sparse one
  • Tech infrastructure - If doing presentation or panel, ensure AV works. Test beforehand. Have backup plan. Many restaurants have terrible wifi and no screens

Timing considerations

Time SlotProsCons
Breakfast (7-8:30am) Less competition from other events. Attendees appreciate being fed before conference. High energy. Can attend and still make 9am sessions Early start after potential late networking night. Some no-shows. Shorter format limits depth
Lunch (12-1:30pm) Middle of day when people need break. Good energy. Conference lunch is usually bad so you are upgrade. Easy to attend Competes with official conference programming. People feel guilty missing sessions. Rushing to get back limits conversation depth

Booking timeline: Reserve venue 2-3 months before major conferences as good spaces near venue book quickly. Smaller conferences allow 4-6 week lead time. Always have cancellation terms clarified in case conference itself gets cancelled.

Invitation Strategy - Target the Right Attendees

▼

Finding conference attendees

Official attendee lists are rarely available. You must identify prospects who plan to attend through research and social signals. Start this work 6-8 weeks before conference.

Attendee identification methods

  • LinkedIn hashtag search - Search conference hashtag and conference name. Look for posts saying "Looking forward to ConferenceName" or "See you at ConferenceName". Build list of people announcing attendance
  • Speaker and panelist lists - Conference websites list speakers. Their companies often send teams. Connect with speakers and ask if colleagues attending. Speakers appreciate side event invites
  • Your existing database - Query CRM for contacts at companies in relevant industries or roles. Send targeted email: "Will you be at ConferenceName? We are hosting breakfast."
  • Past attendee data - If conference is annual, people who attended last year likely attending again. Check conference social media for last year photos and tags

Invitation messaging

ElementWhat to IncludeWhy It Matters
Subject line Mention conference name and breakfast/dinner "Breakfast during ConferenceName - join us?" immediately signals relevance. They know you know they are attending
Value proposition Peer conversation or expert panel, not networking Generic networking event is noise. Promise specific value like "discussion on implementing AI in sales workflows" attracts right people
Convenience angle Location proximity and timing logic "Walking distance from convention center" and "Before sessions start" removes friction. Show you thought about their schedule
Social proof Who else is coming - roles not names "Sales leaders from fintech and SaaS companies" signals peer group. Creates FOMO. People want to network with peers
Invitation cadence: First invite 4-6 weeks out. Reminder 2 weeks before conference. Final reminder 3 days before with "limited spots" message. Day-before reminder with logistics. Expect 40-50% no-show rate even with confirmed RSVPs - over-invite accordingly.

Event Execution - Deliver Memorable Experience

▼

Day-of logistics

Your side event competes with everything else happening at conference. Execution must be flawless because attendees are judging whether it was worth leaving conference floor or skipping session.

Setup checklist

  • Arrive 30 min early - Test AV if using. Confirm food and drink. Set up name tags or place cards. Ensure signage is visible if needed. Brief venue staff on flow
  • Personal greeting - You or team member should personally greet every attendee at door. Make them feel welcomed and expected. Facilitate introductions immediately
  • Name tags with company - Make networking easy. Include name and company. Avoid job titles which can create hierarchy. Simple and clear
  • Managed agenda - Start exactly on time even if people still arriving. Respect early arrivers. End on time or early. Never run over - people have conference commitments

Content formats that work

FormatBest ForExecution Tips
Peer roundtable Groups of 15-25 Facilitate structured discussion around specific challenge. Everyone contributes. No presentations. Pure peer learning
Expert panel Groups of 25-50 2-3 practitioners share approaches to common problem. Q&A focused. Keep panelists brief - 5 min each max. Audience wants discussion not speeches
Workshop or masterclass Groups of 15-30 Teach tactical skill. Provide templates or frameworks they can use immediately. Interactive not lecture. High perceived value
Pure networking Groups of 20-40 Only works if attendees are pre-qualified peers. Structured activities like speed networking prevent clustering. Needs facilitation
The no-pitch rule: Attendees are in conference mode and hyper-sensitive to vendor pitches. Keep your company talk to 2-min intro max. Focus entire event on delivering value. Your restraint builds more credibility than any pitch. Sales comes later in follow-up.
05

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Venue too far from conference

Attendees will not travel 20 minutes for your event when conference is full of options. Distance kills attendance no matter how good your content. They choose proximity over quality.
Book venue within 10-minute walk maximum. Ideally same building or adjacent. Check actual walking route including any construction or obstacles. Proximity is non-negotiable for success.

2. Generic networking reception with no value

Another vendor cocktail party is noise. Attendees go to be polite then leave quickly. No differentiation means no memorable impact and no follow-up opportunities.
Create specific value proposition - expert panel, peer roundtable, workshop. Give them reason to attend beyond free drinks. Structured content creates talking points and relationship hooks for follow-up.

3. Pitching your product during event

Attendees feel bait-and-switched. They came for promised peer learning and got sales presentation. Trust destroyed. They avoid your booth for rest of conference.
Save all selling for post-event follow-up. Keep company intro to 2 minutes maximum. Entire event should deliver value without asking for anything. Your restraint differentiates you from every other vendor.

4. No-show contingency plan

Conference environments have high no-show rates. If you invite exact number you need, you end up with half-empty room and awkward energy. Looks like failure.
Over-invite by 50-60%. If expecting 20 attendees, invite 30-35 with confirmed RSVPs. Some will no-show. Better to be at capacity than sparse. Can always add chairs.

5. Booking too late

Good venues near major conferences book 2-3 months in advance. Last-minute booking forces suboptimal location or time slot. Reduces attendance and impact.
Reserve venue as soon as you decide to piggyback conference - ideally 3 months ahead for major events. Build venue booking into annual conference planning calendar. Early booking gets best options.

6. Weak follow-up after event

Event creates warm connections but momentum dies if you do not follow up quickly. Attendees meet dozens of vendors during conference. You become forgettable without reinforcement.
Send personalised thank you within 24 hours with photos and promised resources. Reference specific conversation points. Offer to continue discussion or meet again at conference. Strike while memory is fresh.
