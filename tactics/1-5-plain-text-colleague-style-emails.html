Tactic 1.5 - Reactive Outbound

Plain Text Colleague-Style Emails

Strip all HTML formatting, logos, and fancy signatures. Write like an internal colleague reaching out with a quick question. Bypasses Gmail Promotions filters and reads as genuine, conversational outreach. Under 5 sentences always.

E
Email
Effort: Low Cost: Free ICP: All ICPs, Technical Buyers
01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Gmail Promotions filters and spam detection algorithms look for marketing signals: HTML formatting, tracking pixels, logos, multiple links, marketing copy patterns. Plain text emails with no formatting signals pass through as personal correspondence. They land in Primary inbox, not Promotions.

The psychology behind plain text colleague-style emails

  • Pattern interruption - Decision-makers receive 50-100 marketing emails per day with designed templates. A plain text email stands out by blending in as genuine correspondence
  • Trust through informality - Fancy signatures and company branding trigger sales defence mechanisms. Plain text signals peer-to-peer communication, not vendor pitch
  • Cognitive ease - No visual clutter. No images loading. Just text. Easier to process, faster to read, lower friction to respond
  • The colleague test - Would a real colleague at your company write this exact email? If yes, it passes the authenticity test. If no, it gets flagged as sales outreach

Why this bypasses spam filters

Gmail Promotions filtering and spam detection systems analyse multiple signals: sender reputation, HTML complexity, link density, tracking pixels, image-to-text ratio, marketing language patterns. Plain text emails eliminate most of these signals. No HTML means no tracking pixels. No images means no image-to-text ratio analysis. Short copy with contractions and casual tone doesn't match marketing copy patterns.

Technical note: Even sending from email marketing platforms like Instantly or Lemlist, plain text emails have 60-80% Primary inbox placement vs 10-20% for designed emails. The delivery mechanism matters less than the content format.

Best Practices

▼

The Colleague Test (Would a Real Colleague Write This?)

▼

Before sending any plain text email, apply the colleague test: if a real colleague at your company sent you this exact email, would it feel normal or would it feel like a sales pitch?

What passes the colleague test

  • Casual tone with contractions: "I'm", "you're", "we've", "can't"
  • Short sentences. 10-15 words max per sentence
  • Lowercase subject lines: "quick question", "following up"
  • No marketing jargon: no "solutions", "leverage", "synergy", "best-in-class"
  • Direct questions: "are you the right person?", "worth a quick chat?"
  • Signed with just first name, no title or company

What fails the colleague test

  • Formal language: "I am reaching out to discuss...", "I would like to explore..."
  • Company pitch: "We help companies like yours...", "Our platform enables..."
  • Long sentences with sub-clauses and formal punctuation
  • Marketing CTAs: "Schedule a demo", "Download our whitepaper"
  • Signature blocks with logo, title, phone, LinkedIn, company address
Test: Show the email to a colleague and ask: "Does this read as if I'm emailing someone I already know?" If they say no, rewrite it.

Formatting Rules (What to Remove)

▼

Remove all of the following

  • No images: No company logo, no headshot, no embedded graphics, no GIFs
  • No links in first email: No calendar links, no website links, no case study links. Exception: if they explicitly requested a link
  • No logo signatures: No company logo in signature block. No branded email signature graphics
  • No HTML formatting: No bold, italics, colours, font changes, bullet points rendered as HTML
  • No tracking pixels: Disable open tracking and click tracking in your email tool settings
  • No marketing footers: Remove "Sent via [Tool Name]", "Unsubscribe", "This email was sent to..."

What you can keep

  • Plain text line breaks for paragraph separation
  • Simple punctuation: periods, commas, question marks
  • Your first name as signature
  • Optionally: mobile phone number on second line (no label, just the number)
Technical note: Even if using an email marketing tool, force plain text mode. Most tools have a "plain text" toggle or "remove HTML" option. Use it. If your tool doesn't support true plain text, consider switching tools or sending manually via Gmail.

Tone and Language (Write Like a Human)

▼

Short sentences rule

Colleagues don't write essay paragraphs. They write 1-2 sentence emails. Break long sentences into multiple short ones. Aim for 10-15 words per sentence. Never exceed 20 words in a single sentence.

Use contractions

Don't write: "I am reaching out because I believe there is potential value."

Write: "I'm reaching out because I think there's value here."

Contractions signal informal, peer-to-peer communication. Formal language signals marketing.

Conversational patterns

Formal (Marketing)Conversational (Colleague)
I wanted to reach out to discuss... Quick question for you
I believe there is an opportunity... Thought this might be relevant
Would you be available for a call? Worth a quick chat?
I look forward to hearing from you Let me know
Our solution enables companies to... We help with [specific problem]
Rule of thumb: If you wouldn't say it out loud to a colleague in the hallway, don't write it in the email.

Subject Line Patterns (2-4 Words, Lowercase)

▼

Why lowercase subjects work

Marketing emails use title case or sentence case with proper punctuation. Internal colleague emails often use lowercase, no punctuation. Gmail Promotions filters flag title case subjects combined with marketing sender patterns. Lowercase subjects bypass this filter.

High-performing plain text subject patterns

  • "quick question" - Generic, non-threatening, low friction
  • "following up" - Implies prior context (even if there wasn't any)
  • "saw your post" - Social proof, personalisation signal
  • "thought of you" - Personal, warm, peer-to-peer
  • "idea for [Company]" - Specific, value-forward, relevant
  • "are you the right person" - Humble, helpful framing

What NOT to do

  • Title case subjects: "Quick Question For You" - signals marketing
  • Punctuation: "Quick question?" or "Following up!" - signals marketing
  • Long subjects: "Quick question about improving your outbound sales process" - too long, too specific, too salesy
  • Emojis: "Quick question 👋" - fine for some audiences, risky for B2B cold outreach
A/B test: Try sending the same email body with two subject variants: "Quick Question For Sarah" (title case) vs "quick question" (lowercase). The lowercase version typically gets 20-30% higher open rates in cold outreach.

When Plain Text Beats Designed Emails

▼

Use plain text for

ScenarioWhy Plain Text Wins
Cold outreach to technical buyers (CTOs, VPs Engineering, DevOps) Technical audiences are allergic to marketing. Plain text signals peer-to-peer technical conversation, not vendor pitch
First touchpoint in a multi-touch sequence Break through as authentic personal outreach. Follow-ups can introduce more structure, links, case studies
Re-engagement or breakup emails Last-ditch effort to revive dead leads. Designed emails already failed. Plain text signals genuine care, not automated sequence
High-value target accounts (enterprise, C-suite) Decision-makers have executive assistants filtering obvious sales emails. Plain text bypasses the gatekeeper filter
When your sender reputation is weak or domain is new Designed emails from new domains trigger spam filters aggressively. Plain text gets benefit of doubt

Use designed emails for

ScenarioWhy Designed Wins
Product demos or feature announcements Visual content (screenshots, diagrams) requires design. Plain text can't show the product
Event invitations or webinars Branding and visual hierarchy help with credibility and registration conversion
Nurture sequences for opted-in leads They've already expressed interest. Design reinforces brand and professionalism
Customer onboarding or support emails Step-by-step instructions benefit from formatting, images, links
Hybrid approach: Send first email plain text to break through. If they engage, send follow-up with light design (simple logo, one link, one CTA). Earned the right to look professional after proving you're human first.
05

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Adding tool footer branding

"Sent via Instantly" or "Powered by Lemlist" at the bottom instantly signals bulk email, not personal outreach. Kills credibility.
Go into tool settings and remove all footer branding. Most tools allow custom signature blocks. Leave it completely blank or just your first name.

2. Tracking pixels triggering spam filters

Most email tools add invisible tracking pixels by default to track opens. Gmail detects these and flags the email as marketing.
Disable open tracking and click tracking in campaign settings. Accept that you won't have open rate data. Focus on reply rate instead.

3. Writing too formally despite plain text format

No HTML doesn't matter if the language is still corporate-speak. "I am writing to inquire..." in plain text still reads as marketing.
Use contractions, short sentences, casual language. Read it aloud. Does it sound like how you'd talk to a colleague? If not, rewrite.

4. Including links in the first email

Links trigger spam filters, especially in cold emails from unknown senders. Even plain text emails with multiple links get flagged.
No links in the first email. Not even your calendar link. Save links for the reply or follow-up email after they've engaged.

5. Using a company signature block

Company name, job title, phone, email, LinkedIn, company address in signature = obvious sales email, even in plain text.
Sign with just your first name. Optionally add your mobile number on a second line (no label, just the number). Nothing else.

6. Subject line too long or too salesy

"I have an idea that could help Acme improve outbound sales by 40%" - length and specificity signal marketing blast.
2-4 lowercase words: "quick question", "following up", "thought of you". That's it. No punctuation. No capitals.

7. Email body over 5 sentences

Real colleagues send 2-3 sentence emails. 7-10 sentence emails signal prepared sales pitch, even in plain text.
Absolute max 5 sentences. Ideally 3-4. If you can't explain the ask in 5 sentences, it's not clear enough. Simplify.

8. Sending from generic company addresses

hello@, info@, sales@ addresses have poor sender reputation and high spam complaint rates. Get filtered aggressively.
Use personal address: firstname@company.com or your personal Gmail. Personal addresses have better deliverability and higher trust.
