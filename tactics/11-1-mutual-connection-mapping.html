Tactic 11.1 - Warm Intro / Network

Mutual Connection Mapping

Map your team's collective network against target account decision-makers to identify warm introduction paths. Warm intros convert significantly better than cold outreach.

Effort: Medium Cost: Low-Medium ICP: All ICPs
01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Your team collectively knows hundreds or thousands of people. Most companies never systematically map these connections against their target accounts. Warm introductions leverage existing trust, dramatically increasing response rates and meeting conversion compared to cold outreach.

The psychology of warm introductions

  • Trust transfer - When someone you trust introduces you to another person, their credibility transfers. The prospect starts with positive sentiment rather than skepticism
  • Social proof - A mutual connection serves as implicit endorsement. "If Sarah trusts this person enough to introduce us, they're probably worth my time"
  • Reciprocity - People feel obligated to respond to introductions. Ignoring a warm intro feels rude in a way that ignoring cold email doesn't
  • Context and relevance - Mutual connections can provide specific context about why this introduction makes sense, making your pitch immediately relevant

Why most teams miss these opportunities

Individual reps only see their own networks. Your SDR might be cold-emailing someone that your CTO went to university with. Your AE might be struggling to reach a VP who your CEO worked with five years ago. Without systematic mapping, these connections stay invisible.

The compounding effect: Each team member adds their unique network. A 5-person team might have 200 mutual connections across target accounts. A 20-person team might have 800-1000. The larger your team, the more powerful this tactic becomes.

StepActionDetailsTime Investment

2 Map Team LinkedIn Networks Use LinkedIn Sales Navigator TeamLink feature or tools like The Swarm or Apollo.io. Each team member connects their LinkedIn account. The tool aggregates all first-degree connections across your team 30 min setup, 5 min per team member
3 Identify Mutual Connections Cross-reference target contacts against aggregated team network. The tool highlights who on your team knows who at target accounts. Pro tip: Run this mapping exercise quarterly. Your team's networks grow over time. New hires bring new connections. Previous contacts move to new companies that might be target accounts. Regular mapping ensures you never miss emerging opportunities.

Best Practices

▼

Connection Strength Scoring (How Well Do They Actually Know Each Other)

▼

Not all LinkedIn connections are equal

Two people can be LinkedIn connections but have wildly different relationship strength. Someone you worked closely with for three years is a much stronger connection than someone you met once at a conference. Quality beats quantity.

Connection strength scoring framework

ScoreRelationship TypeCharacteristicsIntro Success Rate
10/10 Strong personal relationship Former colleague (worked together 1+ years), close friend, family, current co-investor, active mentor/mentee relationship Very high - they'll make the intro enthusiastically
7-9/10 Professional relationship Former colleague (worked together under 1 year), current customer, active partner, regular industry contact (met multiple times) High - they'll likely make the intro if you provide good context
4-6/10 Acquaintance Met at conference/event, occasional LinkedIn interaction, mutual connection introduced you once, same alumni network but don't know each other well Medium - they might make the intro if you make it very easy, but don't expect enthusiasm
1-3/10 Weak connection LinkedIn connection but never interacted, accepted connection request from stranger, connection from years ago with no recent contact Low - often not worth asking unless no other path exists

How to assess connection strength

  • Ask your team member directly - "How well do you know James? Would you feel comfortable introducing me?" This is the most reliable signal
  • Check LinkedIn activity - Do they comment on each other's posts? Tag each other? This indicates an active relationship
  • Look for shared history - Same company at overlapping times? Same university in same years? Geographic proximity?
  • Recency matters - A strong connection from 10 years ago with no recent contact is weaker than a moderate connection from 6 months ago
Rule of thumb: Only ask for intros on connections scored 7/10 or higher. Lower scores waste time and risk damaging the relationship by asking for favours that feel uncomfortable.

Forwardable Blurb Template (Make It Easy for the Introducer)

▼

Why forwardable blurbs matter

Most people want to help with introductions but procrastinate because crafting the intro email feels like work. If you give them a complete, forwardable blurb, they can make the introduction in 30 seconds with minimal editing. Remove friction and you increase intro completion rate dramatically.

Forwardable blurb structure

TemplateHi [Mutual Connection Name], Hope you're well. I'm looking to connect with [Target Person Name] at [Target Company] and noticed you two know each other. Would you be comfortable making an introduction? Here's a blurb you can forward if so: --- [Target Person Name], meet [Your Name]. [Your Name] is [Your Role] at [Your Company]. They help [ICP Description] with [Specific Problem You Solve]. I thought you two should connect because [Specific Reason - e.g., "you mentioned struggling with X last time we spoke" or "given [Target Company]'s focus on Y, this could be relevant"]. [Your Name], [Target Person Name] is [Their Role] at [Target Company] and [One Relevant Detail About Them - shows you've done research]. I'll let you two take it from here. --- Let me know if you're comfortable making this intro. No pressure if the timing isn't right. Thanks, [Your Name]

Key elements of effective blurbs

  • Pre-written and ready to forward - The introducer should be able to copy-paste with zero or minimal editing
  • Context for the mutual connection - Explain why you want the intro and what's in it for the target person
  • Specific and relevant - Generic intros ("I think you two should meet") get ignored. Specific relevance ("You mentioned struggling with X") gets responses
  • Shows you've done research - Include one detail about the target person that proves you're not mass-requesting intros
  • No pressure opt-out - "No pressure if timing isn't right" gives them permission to decline without awkwardness
Pro tip: Personalise the reason for each intro. "I thought you two should connect" is lazy. "I noticed [Target Company] just launched X, and we help companies in similar situations with Y" shows genuine relevance and increases intro likelihood.

Timing and Frequency (Don't Burn Out Your Network)

▼

The danger of over-asking

Your network is a finite resource. If you constantly ask the same people for introductions without providing value in return, you'll quickly burn out your relationships. People will start avoiding your requests or declining intros because you've become "that person" who only reaches out when you need something.

Healthy intro request frequency

Relationship StrengthMax Intro RequestsMinimum Gap Between Requests
Strong (10/10) 3-4 per year 3 months minimum between requests
Professional (7-9/10) 2-3 per year 4-6 months minimum between requests
Acquaintance (4-6/10) 1 per year maximum 12 months minimum between requests

How to maintain relationship equity

  • Provide value between asks - Share relevant articles, make introductions for them, comment on their LinkedIn posts, offer help with their challenges. Build deposits in the relationship bank
  • Report back on outcomes - After every intro, tell the introducer what happened. "Thanks for the intro to James. We had a great call and he's now a customer." This creates positive reinforcement
  • Reciprocate introductions - When someone in your network needs an intro, make it enthusiastically. What goes around comes around
  • Thank them publicly - When an intro leads to something good, thank the introducer publicly (LinkedIn post, team meeting mention, etc.). Recognition builds goodwill
Warning sign: If you're asking the same 2-3 people for most of your intros, you're probably over-relying on them. Spread intro requests across your team's network to avoid burning out individual relationships.

Tracking and Attribution (Measure Which Intros Convert)

▼

Why tracking matters

Without tracking, you don't know which types of introductions work best. Are customer intros better than investor intros? Do intros from former colleagues convert better than conference connections? Tracking reveals patterns that improve your future prioritisation.

Key metrics to monitor

  • Intro completion rate - What percentage of requested intros actually get made? Low rate suggests you're asking wrong people or making it too difficult
  • Response rate - What percentage of intros get a response? Should be much higher than cold outreach
  • Meeting conversion rate - What percentage lead to actual meetings?
  • Opportunity creation rate - What percentage create pipeline?
  • Best connection types - Which types of mutual connections convert best? Double down on those
Benchmark targets: Intro completion rate above 70%, response rate above 60%, meeting conversion above 40%. If you're below these, revisit your connection strength scoring and forwardable blurb quality.

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Asking for intros to people you barely know

You ask a team member to introduce you to someone they're loosely connected with. The introducer feels awkward making an intro when they don't know the person well. The intro either doesn't happen or feels forced.
Only ask for intros on connections scored 7/10 or higher. Weak connections waste everyone's time and damage credibility.

2. Not providing a forwardable blurb

You ask for an intro but leave it to the introducer to figure out what to say. They procrastinate because writing the intro email feels like work. The intro never happens.
Always provide a complete, forwardable blurb. Make it so easy they can copy-paste with zero editing. Remove all friction.

3. Shotgun approach (too many asks at once)

You discover 20 mutual connections and immediately ask for all 20 intros. The introducer feels overwhelmed and either ignores your request or makes low-quality intros.
Prioritise top 3-5 highest-value intros. Ask in small batches. Space out requests over time. Quality over quantity.

4. Not following up after the intro is made

Someone makes an intro for you, but you respond slowly or not at all. This makes the introducer look bad and ensures they won't make future intros for you.
Respond within 24 hours of any intro. Thank the introducer publicly (cc them). Move the conversation forward immediately. Report back on the outcome.

5. Not reciprocating introductions

You constantly ask for intros but never offer to make intros for others. Your relationship equity depletes. People start avoiding your requests.
Actively look for opportunities to make intros for others. When someone in your network needs help, connect them enthusiastically. Build deposits in the relationship bank.

6. Generic intro requests lacking context

You ask for an intro with no explanation of why it's relevant. "Do you know anyone at Acme Corp?" The introducer doesn't understand the value and doesn't make the intro.
Provide specific context for every intro request. Explain why you want to meet this person, what problem you solve, and what value you can provide. Specificity increases intro likelihood.
