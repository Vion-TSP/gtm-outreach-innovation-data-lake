Tactic 5.1 - Event-Based Plays

Executive Dinner (8-12 People)

Host intimate dinners for C-suite and VP-level buyers featuring facilitated peer conversation without vendor pitches. Create high-trust environment where executives share challenges and learn from peers, positioning your company as valuable convener rather than pushy vendor.

Effort: High Cost: High ICP: C-suite, VP-level, Founders
01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Senior executives are bombarded with transactional pitches but starved for authentic peer connection. An intimate dinner where they can discuss real challenges with others facing similar issues creates genuine value. When you facilitate these conversations without selling, you become a trusted advisor rather than another vendor.

The psychology behind executive dinners

  • Scarcity and status - Limited invites signal exclusivity. Executives value access to peer circles they cannot easily reach. Being selected creates reciprocity obligation
  • Safe vulnerability - Small group size and no-pitch rule allow authentic conversation about real challenges. Executives rarely get to speak candidly about what keeps them up at night
  • Peer learning over vendor selling - People trust peer insights far more than vendor claims. Hearing how another CFO solved a problem carries weight no sales pitch can match
  • Relationship depth - Three hours of facilitated conversation builds more trust than six months of cold emails. You become a real person, not a logo

Why dinners specifically

Dinner provides natural structure for extended conversation. Breaking bread together is ancient relationship-building ritual. The setting is relaxed, not transactional. Alcohol loosens tongues. Multiple courses pace the evening and allow topics to evolve organically.

The compounding effect: Attendees leave with valuable peer connections they did not have before. When you facilitate those connections, they associate you with the value of their new network. Future outreach from you carries weight of that positive experience.

04

Best Practices

▼

Guest Selection - The Right Mix Matters

▼

Ideal guest profile

The success of an executive dinner lives or dies on who attends. Wrong mix kills conversation. Right mix creates magic that attendees talk about for months.

Selection criteria

  • Similar seniority level - Mix VPs with C-suite and conversation fragments. Directors feel intimidated. Keep within one level maximum
  • Shared challenges - Guests should face similar problems even if different industries. CFOs discussing scaling finance operations, Sales leaders tackling team growth
  • Non-competitive - Do not invite direct competitors unless you explicitly want competitive tension. Usually better to keep industries diverse but complementary
  • Strong opinions - Wallflowers kill dinner energy. Research attendees' thought leadership and look for people who engage, write, speak publicly

Prospect to customer ratio

MixWhen to UseEffect
All prospects New market or early stage Pure peer learning environment. Harder to facilitate without existing customer stories. More pressure on you to guide conversation
70% prospects, 30% customers Most common mix Customers provide authentic proof points without feeling like plants. Prospects still drive majority of agenda
50-50 split When you want customer community building Risks feeling too vendor-centric. Works if customers are advocates who enjoy the peer connection itself
Invitation strategy: Over-invite by 30%. Expect 20-30% drop-out rate even with confirmed RSVPs. Have waitlist of backup guests. Better to cancel 48 hours out than host dinner with 5 people.

Venue and Logistics - Details Matter

▼

Venue selection criteria

Venue sends a signal about how much you value attendees. Too cheap feels insulting. Too flashy feels like you are showing off. Find the balance that says "we invested in making this special."

Key venue requirements

  • Private room - Non-negotiable. Open dining room kills candid conversation. Need to discuss challenges without being overheard
  • Round or oval table - Everyone can see everyone else. No head of table creating hierarchy. Encourages equal participation
  • Good acoustics - Low ceilings, soft surfaces, reasonable background music. If people must shout to be heard, intimacy dies
  • Attentive but not intrusive service - Staff who can read the room. Food appears when needed but servers do not interrupt key moments

Logistics checklist

ElementBest PracticeWhy It Matters
Timing Start at 7pm on Tuesday, Wednesday, or Thursday Monday too soon after weekend. Friday people want to go home. Early enough that dinner finishes by 10pm
Duration Plan for 2.5-3 hours including arrival drinks Long enough for depth. Short enough people do not check out. Build in buffer time

Budget planning: Plan £150-250 per person for quality venue with wine. For 10 people expect £1500-2500 total. Do not cheap out - you only get one chance to make the impression.

Facilitation Approach - How to Guide Without Dominating

▼

The facilitation mindset

Your job is to make great conversation happen, not to be the star of the conversation. Best dinners are where attendees do 80% of talking and you guide with strategic questions and transitions.

Opening the dinner

  • Set ground rules upfront - "Everything shared tonight stays in the room. No pitching - this is peer learning not vendor selling. Please share honestly."
  • Go around for introductions - Name, role, company, and one current challenge or goal. Establishes vulnerability and gives everyone a voice early
  • Acknowledge the format - "I have some topics I thought would be interesting but let's go where the energy is. Interrupt me if something else is more valuable."

During the conversation

ScenarioFacilitation TechniqueExample
Someone dominating Politely redirect "James, that is really interesting. Sarah, I know you have worked on something similar - what was your experience?"
Quiet participant Directly invite "Sarah, you mentioned during intros you are working on X. How does this relate to what James just described?"
Conversation stalls Introduce new angle "Shifting gears slightly - how are you thinking about this challenge in context of AI emerging as a factor?"
Someone pitching Gently deflect "That sounds interesting - happy to connect you two after dinner. For now, let's stay on the theme of how everyone is approaching this."
The power of silence: When you ask a question, let silence hang for 5-10 seconds. Resist urge to fill it. Someone will start talking and often it is the person who most needed to contribute.

Follow-Up Strategy - Converting Good Experience to Business

▼

Immediate follow-up

Strike while memory is fresh but do not be transactional. The dinner built relationship capital - spend it wisely.

24-hour follow-up

  • Personal thank you email - Reference specific thing they said or moment from conversation. Make it clear you were paying attention to them specifically
  • Make promised connections - If you offered to introduce attendees to each other or connect them with resources, do it immediately. Your reliability matters
  • Share relevant content - If conversation touched on topic where you have useful content, share it with note: "This relates to what we discussed about X."

Sales follow-up timing

TimingApproachWhy
Within 24 hours Avoid Feels transactional and destroys trust you built. They will feel used. Wait longer
7-10 days after Soft outreach "Really enjoyed our conversation at dinner. Would love to continue discussing how we might help with the challenge you mentioned." Still warm but not pushy
2-3 weeks after Value-first offer "Been thinking about what you shared at dinner. Would you find it useful if I pulled together analysis of X for Acme specifically?" Lead with value not ask

Long-term nurture

Continue providing value without asking for anything. Share relevant articles. Make useful introductions. Invite to future events. Build relationship over months not days. When they are ready to buy, you will be first call.

Measurement approach: Track meetings booked within 90 days of dinner, opportunities created, and deals influenced. Executive dinners rarely create immediate pipeline but often shorten sales cycles dramatically by pre-building trust.
05

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Pitching your product during dinner

Violates the no-pitch promise and makes attendees feel tricked. They came for peer learning and you hijacked it into a sales presentation. Trust destroyed instantly.
Save all selling for follow-up at least a week later. If attendees ask about your product, answer briefly and redirect to peer conversation. Your restraint builds more trust than any pitch could.

2. Inviting wrong seniority mix

Directors feel intimidated around C-suite and stay quiet. C-suite finds director-level concerns less relevant. Conversation fragments into parallel discussions that exclude people.
Keep within one level maximum. If targeting VPs, invite only VPs and C-suite. If targeting C-suite, do not include directors or managers. Shared seniority creates peer safety.

3. Choosing loud crowded venue

Background noise forces shouting. Lack of privacy kills candid sharing. People cannot hear each other and give up trying to engage. Dinner becomes endurance test not enjoyable experience.
Always book private room. Visit venue beforehand to test acoustics. Specify music volume limits. Ensure space is sized appropriately - too large feels empty, too small feels cramped.

4. Not facilitating conversation actively

Without facilitation, loudest person dominates and quiet attendees tune out. Conversation stays superficial or devolves into side chats. Value promised does not materialise.
Come prepared with discussion guide. Actively direct conversation. Draw out quiet participants. Politely redirect dominators. Introduce new angles when energy drops. Your facilitation is key value delivered.

5. Immediate transactional follow-up

Sending sales pitch within 24 hours makes entire dinner feel like elaborate lead generation scheme. Attendees feel manipulated. Relationship capital squandered.
Wait at least 7-10 days for sales follow-up. Immediate follow-up should be thank you and delivering promised value. Let relationship breathe before making asks.

6. Skimping on venue quality

Cheap venue signals you do not value their time. Mid-tier restaurant with decent food in non-private space feels like you are cutting corners. Attendees question whether they should have come.
Budget £150-250 per person for quality venue, pre-selected menu, and good wine. This is not place to cut costs. Investment in experience pays back many times in relationship quality.
