Tactic 9.3 - Creative / Guerrilla

We Built This For You Demo

Pre-configure your product with the prospect's data, logo, and use case. Record a personalised walkthrough showing exactly how they would use it. Creates immediate value visualisation and dramatically shortens sales cycles with technical buyers and founders.

Effort: High Cost: Low ICP: C-suite, Founders
01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Prospects don't want to imagine how your product could work for them - they want to see it already working for them. Pre-configuring your product with their actual data removes all friction from visualisation. They open the link and immediately see their company name, their logo, their use case already built out.

The psychology behind custom demos

  • Ownership effect - Seeing their company name and logo in your product creates psychological ownership before they've bought anything. It feels like it's already theirs
  • Reduced cognitive load - Generic demos force prospects to mentally translate "how this demo applies to my situation." Custom demos eliminate that work. The value is immediately obvious
  • Signal of effort - Building something custom for them signals serious intent and investment. This creates reciprocity obligation to at least take the meeting
  • Social proof internally - Recipients forward the demo to colleagues: "Look what this vendor built for us." This expands your reach inside the account

Why this works for technical buyers

Technical buyers and founders are sceptical of sales pitches. They want proof, not promises. A custom demo is proof. You're not telling them it works - you're showing them it already works with their data. This bypasses scepticism entirely.

The format advantage: Unlike scheduled demos (which require calendar coordination and gatekeeping), a recorded custom demo can be watched anytime, forwarded to stakeholders, and rewatched for clarity. It's asynchronous sales at its best.

Best Practices

▼

Data Configuration (Realistic Data That Matches Their Use Case)

▼

Why data quality matters

Generic demo data (names like John Doe, companies like Acme Corp) screams lazy outreach. The power of this tactic is in the specificity. The data must feel like it could actually be their data.

Data sourcing strategies

  • Public website scraping - Pull their customer testimonials, case study names, product categories from their website. Use these as sample data points
  • Industry benchmarks - If you're showing analytics or metrics, use realistic numbers for their industry and company size. Research typical KPIs for their sector
  • Tech stack inference - Use BuiltWith or job postings to see what tools they use. Configure integrations or data imports to match their stack
  • LinkedIn research - Pull employee names and titles from LinkedIn. Use realistic role names in your demo data (if you're a CRM, create contacts with titles that match their team structure)

Configuration checklist

ElementWhat to CustomiseWhere to Find Data
Account nameTheir actual company nameObvious (website, LinkedIn)
LogoTheir company logoWebsite, LinkedIn, Clearbit
User namesRealistic names/roles for their teamLinkedIn (check their team page)
Data pointsSample data reflecting their businessWebsite (customer types, product categories), industry benchmarks
Metrics/KPIsRealistic numbers for their stage/industryIndustry reports, competitor analysis, Crunchbase
Rule of thumb: Spend 70% of your time on data configuration, 30% on recording. A well-configured demo with mediocre narration beats a perfectly narrated demo with generic data every time.

Video Recording Tips (Keep Under 5 Min, Address Specific Pain Points)

▼

Why shorter is better

Busy executives will watch a 3 min video. They will not watch a 15 min video. You have one chance to hold attention. Use it wisely. Focus on one core use case, not a comprehensive product tour.

Video structure

SectionDurationWhat to Cover
Intro 30 sec "Hi Sarah, I built this quick demo for Acme. I noticed you're hiring SDRs and scaling outbound. Here's how we'd help you manage that."
Context 30 sec "I configured this with your company name and created sample data based on your customer profile. What you're seeing is how it would look for your team."
Walkthrough 2-3 min Show 2-3 key features that solve their specific pain point. Point out where their data appears. Click through realistic workflows. Narrate what you're doing and why it matters to them
Close 30 sec "That's a quick look. I can give you access to this demo environment if you want to click around yourself. Happy to discuss how this would work for your specific setup. Calendar link below."

Narration best practices

  • Speak to them directly - Use "you" and "your team," not "users" or "customers." Make it personal
  • Explain the why, not just the what - Don't just say "here's the dashboard." Say "this dashboard shows your SDR activity in real-time, so you can spot issues before they become problems"
  • Point out their data - Explicitly call out when you're showing their company name, logo, or customised data: "See, I used your actual customer segments here"
  • Don't script it - Conversational tone beats polished script. It's okay to pause or say "um" occasionally. Sounds more authentic
Pro tip: Do a quick test recording first. Watch it back. If you would skip forward or zone out, your prospect will too. Redo it. Aim for energy and specificity, not perfection.

Follow-Up Based on Viewing Data (Track Engagement, Time Follow-Up)

▼

Why viewing analytics matter

Loom and Vidyard show who watched, how long, and whether they rewatched or shared. This data is gold for follow-up timing and messaging. Someone who watched all 5 min gets a different follow-up than someone who bailed after 30 sec.

Follow-up strategy by engagement level

Viewing BehaviorWhat It MeansFollow-Up Action
Watched 100%, multiple views Highly interested, likely shared internally Call immediately or send urgent email: "Saw you watched the demo a few times. Would love to give you live access to explore. When can we chat?"
Watched 70-100%, single view Interested, evaluating Follow up within 24h: "Thanks for checking out the demo. What did you think? Happy to walk through any questions or give you access to the environment."
Watched 30-70%, single view Curious but not hooked, or got interrupted Follow up in 2-3 days: "Noticed you started the demo. Curious if it was relevant or if I missed the mark on your use case. Happy to build a different version if needed."
Watched less than 30% Not relevant, or timing is bad Follow up in 5-7 days: "Saw you didn't get through the demo. Totally fine if timing's not right. Let me know if you want me to try again in a few months."
No view after 3 days Missed the email or deprioritised Send reminder: "Following up on the custom demo I built for Acme. Did you get a chance to check it out? Only takes 3 min."

Advanced follow-up tactics

  • Reference specific sections - If Loom shows they rewatched a particular section, mention it: "Noticed you rewatched the reporting section. Want to dive deeper on that?"
  • Offer live access - If engagement is high, give them login credentials to the configured demo so they can explore themselves
  • Multi-stakeholder tracking - If video was shared to multiple people (Loom shows this), follow up acknowledging the team interest: "Saw a few people on your team watched this. Happy to do a live walkthrough for everyone."
Pro tip: Set up Loom notifications so you're alerted the moment someone watches. If a high-priority prospect watches at 3pm, you can follow up by 4pm while you're still top of mind. Speed wins.

Scaling Considerations (Timebox Setup, Reuse Components)

▼

The scaling challenge

Custom demos are high-effort. If you spend 60 min per demo, you can only do 5-6 per week. To scale this tactic, you need to reduce setup time without sacrificing quality.

Efficiency strategies

  • Template configurations - Create 3-4 base configurations for common ICP segments (e.g., SaaS companies, e-commerce, agencies). Start with template and customise rather than building from scratch each time
  • Reusable data sets - Build libraries of realistic sample data for common industries. Instead of creating new contacts/products/metrics every time, swap in pre-built data sets
  • Narration scripts - Write 2-3 narration scripts for common pain points. Personalise the intro and close, but reuse the middle walkthrough sections
  • Time limits - Cap setup at 30 min max per demo. If you can't configure it in 30 min, your product is too complex for this tactic (use interactive demo platforms instead)

When NOT to use this tactic

SituationWhy It Won't WorkAlternative
Product requires 60+ min setupToo time-intensive, ROI doesn't justify effortUse interactive demo platforms (Navattic, Storylane) that let you build once and personalise variables
No clear visual differentiationIf demo looks the same for everyone regardless of data, customisation isn't impactfulStick to standard demos or focus personalisation on use case narrative, not data
Accounts worth less than $10,000 ACVEffort doesn't match potential valueUse this for enterprise only. For SMB, use standard recorded demos or live demos
Rule of thumb: Only use this tactic for accounts worth at least $10,000 ACV and where you can configure a realistic demo in under 30 min. Otherwise, the ROI doesn't justify the effort.
05

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Demo doesn't work properly (broken features or bugs)

You send a demo link but when they click in, something is broken or doesn't load. This destroys credibility instantly.
Always test the demo environment yourself before sending. Click through the full workflow. Make sure all features work. Have a colleague test it as a sanity check. If your product is buggy in demos, fix that before using this tactic.

2. Too much data (overwhelming, not helpful)

You load the demo with hundreds of data points trying to be thorough. Prospect opens it and is overwhelmed. Can't figure out what to focus on.
Less is more. Show just enough data to illustrate the use case. If it's a CRM, create 10-15 sample contacts, not 500. If it's analytics, show 2-3 key metrics, not 20 dashboards. Clarity beats comprehensiveness.

3. Not explaining what they're seeing

You send the demo link with no context. Prospect opens it, sees their company name, but doesn't understand what they're looking at or why it matters.
Narrate the demo. Record a Loom walkthrough explaining what you built and why. Point out the customised elements. Connect features to their specific pain points. Context is everything.

4. No clear CTA (they watch, then nothing happens)

You send an impressive demo but don't tell them what to do next. They watch it, think "cool," then move on with their day.
End the video with a clear CTA: "I can give you live access to this environment to explore, or we can jump on a call to discuss your specific setup. Calendar link is below." Make the next step obvious and easy.

5. Spending too long per demo (not sustainable)

You spend 90 min building each custom demo. You can only do 3-4 per week, and it burns you out quickly.
Timebox to 30 min max per demo. Create template configurations for common ICP segments. Reuse data sets and narration scripts. If you can't configure a realistic demo in 30 min, use interactive demo platforms or stick to live demos.

6. Generic data that doesn't match their business

You put their logo on a generic demo with placeholder data (John Doe, Acme Corp). Feels lazy and templated, defeating the purpose.
Research their business and use realistic data. Pull customer types from their website, use industry-appropriate metrics, create contacts with job titles that match their team structure. Data quality is what makes this tactic work.
