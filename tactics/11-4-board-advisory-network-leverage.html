Tactic 11.4 - Warm Intro / Network

Board / Advisory Network Leverage

Map your board members' and advisors' networks. Ask for specific introductions with forwardable blurbs.

Effort: Low Cost: Free ICP: Founders, C-suite
01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Board members and advisors are typically senior operators with decades of relationships across industries. Their networks skew toward decision-makers. An intro from your board member to another executive carries significant weight because it comes from a peer, not a vendor.

The psychology of board-level introductions

  • Peer-to-peer credibility - Executives trust other executives. When a board member introduces you, the recipient knows you've been vetted at the highest level
  • Reputation staking - Board members only make intros when confident you'll perform. They protect their reputation. This implicit endorsement is powerful
  • Network reciprocity at scale - Board members routinely make intros for each other's portfolio companies. It's part of the value they provide. Asking for intros is expected
  • Access to closed networks - Board members have relationships that aren't findable on LinkedIn. Private networks, golf buddies, dinner circles. These relationships unlock doors impossible to reach via cold outreach

Why most companies underutilize their board

Founders treat board members as governance and strategic advisors but forget they're also massive sales assets. Board members want portfolio companies to succeed. Making strategic introductions is one of the highest-leverage ways they can help. Most board members are underasked.

The quality advantage: Board intros convert at significantly higher rates than even warm employee intros. Board members connect you directly to economic buyers, not mid-level managers. You skip layers of gatekeepers and get straight to decision-makers.

04

Best Practices

▼

Specific vs Vague Asks (Name the Person, Not the Company)

▼

Why vague asks fail

When you ask "Do you know anyone at Acme Corp?" the board member has to do work. They have to think through their network, figure out who's relevant, assess relationship strength, craft the intro. This is friction. Most vague asks die from inertia.

Specific vs vague comparison

Vague Ask (Bad)Specific Ask (Good)
"Do you know anyone at Acme Corp?" "Would you be comfortable introducing me to Sarah Chen, VP of Sales at Acme Corp?"
"We're trying to reach enterprise retailers. Any connections?" "I saw you were on the board at RetailCo with James. Would you introduce me?"
"Looking for intros in the fintech space." "Can you introduce me to the CEO of FinanceApp? I noticed you invested in their Series B."

How to make asks specific

  • Research first - Use LinkedIn, Crunchbase, company websites to identify the exact person you want to meet
  • Confirm the connection - Verify your board member actually knows this person (shared board, past employment, investment, mutual connection)
  • Name the person and their role - "Sarah Chen, VP of Sales" not "someone in sales at Acme"
  • Explain why this specific person - "Sarah owns the sales tech stack decision" not just "we want to sell to Acme"
  • Make it yes/no - "Would you be comfortable making this intro?" forces simple decision, no ambiguity
Response rate impact: Specific asks get responded to and actioned at significantly higher rates than vague asks. Do your homework before asking. Make it a simple yes/no decision for the board member.

Forwardable Blurb Best Practices (Write It For Them)

▼

Why forwardable blurbs are critical

Board members are extremely busy. If you make them write the intro email from scratch, it goes to the bottom of their to-do list. If you provide a complete, well-written blurb they can forward as-is, the intro happens within 24 hours.

Forwardable blurb template

Template for Board MemberHi [Board Member Name], Quick request: Would you be comfortable introducing me to [Target Name], [Target Title] at [Target Company]? I noticed you [specific connection: sit on board together, worked together at X, co-invested in Y]. If you're open to it, here's a blurb you can forward: --- [Target Name], I wanted to introduce you to [Your Name], [Your Title] at [Your Company]. [Your Company] helps [specific ICP] with [specific problem]. Given [Target Company]'s focus on [relevant initiative you've researched], thought this could be timely. [Your Name], [Target Name] is [something complimentary and specific about them showing you've done research]. Happy to let you two take it from here. --- No pressure if the timing isn't right. Let me know if you need any other context. Thanks, [Your Name]

Key elements of effective blurbs

  • Short and scannable - Board members get hundreds of emails daily. Keep the full request under 150 words
  • Explain the connection - "I noticed you sit on the board together" - shows you've done research and this isn't a random ask
  • Make blurb copy-paste ready - Clearly marked with --- separators so board member knows exactly what to forward
  • Include specific context - Reference something about target company that proves this intro is relevant, not generic
  • Compliment the target - Show you've researched them specifically. "Saw your recent post on sales transformation" makes it personal
  • Give easy out - "No pressure if timing isn't right" respects their judgment on relationship strength and timing
Pro tip: Send the forwardable blurb in plain text, not formatted HTML. Board members often forward from mobile. Plain text ensures it renders correctly on any device.

Frequency and Relationship Management (Don't Overask)

▼

The risk of burning out board members

Board members make intros as a favor. Their currency is their reputation. If you ask too frequently or for low-quality intros, you deplete their willingness to help. They'll start declining or ignoring requests.

Healthy board intro frequency

Board Member Engagement LevelMax Intro RequestsSpacing
Highly engaged (active board member) 6-8 per year At least 6 weeks between requests
Moderately engaged (advisor, occasional involvement) 3-4 per year At least 3 months between requests
Lightly engaged (strategic advisor, infrequent contact) 1-2 per year At least 6 months between requests

How to maintain board member goodwill

  • Prioritise ruthlessly - Only ask for intros to truly high-value targets. Don't ask for intros to accounts that aren't ICP fit
  • Always report back - After every intro, update the board member on outcome. "Intro to Sarah led to demo scheduled for next week. Thanks!" This positive reinforcement encourages future intros
  • Acknowledge publicly when appropriate - If an intro leads to a deal, mention it in board meeting. "James's intro to Acme resulted in our biggest deal this quarter." Recognition matters
  • Offer reciprocal value - When possible, make intros for your board members to people in your network. Help them recruit, connect them to potential investors, share relevant content
  • Batch requests strategically - Instead of trickling requests, ask for 2-3 intros at once quarterly. This feels more strategic and less needy than constant one-off asks
Warning sign: If a board member starts declining intro requests or taking weeks to respond, you've probably overasked. Pull back for 3-6 months and focus on other channels. Rebuild relationship equity before asking again.

Post-Intro Follow-Up (Make the Board Member Look Good)

▼

Why follow-up matters for future intros

Board members pay attention to what happens after they make intros. If you respond quickly and professionally, they'll keep making intros. If you're slow to respond or waste the intro, they stop helping. Your follow-up behavior determines whether they'll introduce you to others.

Post-intro follow-up timeline

TimelineActionPurpose
Within 4 hours Reply-all thanking board member and engaging with target Show responsiveness. Move conversation forward immediately. Board member sees you valued the intro
After first meeting Email board member with brief update "Had a great call with Sarah. She's interested and we're following up with proposal." Closes the loop
Monthly until outcome Brief status update if deal is progressing Keep board member informed. Particularly important if it's a strategic account. No news makes them wonder if intro was wasted
When deal closes (or dies) Final outcome update "Sarah became a customer!" or "Sarah went with competitor because of budget." Either way, close the loop completely

How to make board members look good

  • Be exceptionally professional - Your behavior reflects on the board member. Show up prepared, deliver on promises, communicate clearly
  • Acknowledge them publicly in reply - "Thanks James for the intro." This credits them in front of the target
  • Move fast - Respond to intro within hours, not days. Schedule meeting within a week. Speed signals respect for everyone's time
  • Reference them during conversation - "James mentioned you two worked together at TechCo. What was that experience like?" Honors the relationship
  • Report wins - When intro leads to something good, make sure board member knows. They get satisfaction from helping
Career tip: Board members have long memories. Treat every intro as an audition for future intros. Founders who consistently convert board intros into outcomes get more intros. Those who waste them get cut off.

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Vague asks ("know anyone at...")

You ask "Do you know anyone at Acme Corp?" Board member has to do work to figure out who's relevant. Request sits in inbox unanswered.
Name the specific person you want to meet. Make it a simple yes/no decision. Do the research yourself.

2. Not preparing forwardable email

You ask for intro but leave it to board member to write it. They're busy. Writing intro emails from scratch is work. Request dies from friction.
Provide complete, copy-paste ready blurb. Make the intro take 30 seconds, not 30 minutes.

3. Asking board members too frequently

You ask for intros every week. Board member feels used. Their network becomes depleted. They start declining or ghosting requests.
Max 6-8 requests per year even for highly engaged board members. Space requests 6+ weeks apart. Prioritise quality over quantity.

4. Not reporting back on outcome

Board member makes intro and never hears what happened. They wonder if you followed up. Feels like their effort was unappreciated. Won't make future intros.
Update board member after first meeting and again when outcome is known. Close the loop every time. Show appreciation.

5. Not making the connection easy for the introducer

Your forwardable blurb is three paragraphs of dense text. Board member edits it down before forwarding. Or worse, doesn't forward because it's too long.
Keep forwardable blurb under 100 words. Three short paragraphs max. Make it scannable and easy to forward as-is.

6. Slow response after intro is made

Board member makes intro. You take 3 days to respond. Board member looks bad. Target loses interest. Board member won't make future intros for you.
Reply within 4 hours of intro email. Thank board member publicly. Engage with target immediately. Speed shows respect.
