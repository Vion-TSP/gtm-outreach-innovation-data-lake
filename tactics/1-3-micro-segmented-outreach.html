Tactic 1.3 - Reactive Outbound

Micro-Segmented Outreach (Under 50)

Build tiny lists of fewer than 50 prospects sharing hyper-specific characteristics. Deep research each prospect, write segment-specific copy that feels 1-to-1, and outperform large volume campaigns through extreme relevance.

01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Relevance beats volume. A message crafted for 30 prospects who all share the same industry, company size, tech stack, and trigger event will always outperform a generic message sent to 3,000. Micro-segments enable hyper-relevant copy that feels hand-written, not templated.

Why small lists outperform large campaigns

  • Context depth - With 30-50 prospects, you can actually research each person individually. With 3,000, you cannot. Individual research enables context that feels personal
  • Message precision - When everyone in your list shares the same characteristics, you can write copy that speaks directly to their exact situation without hedging or generalising
  • Manual touchpoints - Small lists make manual outreach feasible. You can actually personalise the first line of each email, send LinkedIn voice notes, or leave voicemails. These tactics don't scale to thousands
  • Quality filtering - Building a list under 50 forces you to be ruthless about fit. You exclude marginal prospects. Higher bar means better conversion rates

What makes a true micro-segment

A micro-segment is NOT just a title filter or industry filter. True micro-segments combine multiple dimensions:

  • Industry + Company size + Tech stack + Trigger event
  • Example: Series A fintech companies (50-200 employees) using Stripe who hired a Head of Growth in the last 90 days
  • The more dimensions you stack, the smaller the list and the more relevant your message becomes
Anti-pattern: A list of 500 "VP Sales at SaaS companies" is not a micro-segment. It's too broad. A list of 35 "VP Sales at Series B marketing automation platforms using Salesforce who posted a job for an SDR Manager this month" is a micro-segment.

What Makes a Micro-Segment (Examples)

▼

Characteristics of strong micro-segments

Strong micro-segments combine at least 3 of these dimensions:

  • Industry vertical - Not just "SaaS", but "marketing automation SaaS" or "vertical SaaS for healthcare"
  • Company stage/size - Series A, 50-200 employees, $5M-$20M ARR
  • Tech stack - Using specific tools: Salesforce + Outreach + Gong, or HubSpot + Clay + Apollo
  • Trigger event - Recent funding, new C-suite hire, product launch, office expansion, layoffs, M&A activity
  • Geographic location - Useful when paired with other criteria: Series A fintech in London, enterprise healthcare SaaS in Boston

Real micro-segment examples

Segment DescriptionSizeWhy It Works
Series A fintech companies (50-200 employees) using Stripe who hired Head of Growth in last 90 days 28 prospects All experiencing same pain: scaling growth post-Series A. Stripe signals payment infrastructure maturity. New Head of Growth means budget unlocked
VP Sales at Series B enterprise SaaS (200-500 employees) using Salesforce + Outreach who posted SDR Manager job in last 30 days 42 prospects Hiring SDR Manager signals scaling outbound motion. They need tools, training, processes. Your message addresses exact need at exact moment
Founders of bootstrapped B2B SaaS (10-50 employees, no funding) who hit $1M ARR milestone in last 6 months based on LinkedIn posts 19 prospects Crossing $1M ARR is inflection point. Founders publicly celebrate it on LinkedIn. Timing perfect for operational upgrades
CMOs at consumer brands (500-2000 employees) using Shopify Plus who launched DTC site in last 12 months 36 prospects New DTC motion means experimentation with channels, attribution tools, customer data platforms. They're actively buying MarTech
CROs at private equity-backed B2B services firms (100-500 employees) who completed acquisition in last 6 months 24 prospects Post-acquisition integration means systems consolidation, process standardisation, tech stack rationalisation. They're making buying decisions
Pattern: Notice how each example stacks 3-4 filters. Industry + size + tech/trigger. The intersection creates a tiny addressable market with shared context you can reference directly in your copy.

Building Lists in Clay (Practical Guide)

▼

Clay workflow for micro-segments

  1. Start with broad filter - Apply industry or job title filter first. Example: VP Sales at SaaS companies. This returns 50,000+ results
  2. Layer company stage filter - Add employee count range (50-200) or funding stage (Series A-B). Cuts list to 5,000
  3. Add tech stack filter - Use BuiltWith or Clearbit enrichment to filter by tools used. Example: companies using Salesforce + Outreach. Down to 800
  4. Apply trigger filter - Filter by job postings (using Ashby, Greenhouse, Lever integrations) or funding announcements (Crunchbase). Down to 120
  5. Enrich with additional data - Pull LinkedIn profiles, recent posts, company news. Review list manually. Remove poor fits. Final list: 35-50

Key Clay features for micro-segmentation

  • BuiltWith enrichment - Identifies tech stack: CRM, email tools, analytics platforms, payment processors
  • LinkedIn enrichment - Pulls recent posts, job changes, company updates, shared connections
  • Job board scraping - Monitors job postings from Ashby, Greenhouse, Lever. Filter by role title, department, seniority
  • Crunchbase data - Funding rounds, M&A activity, investor lists, revenue estimates
  • Custom filters - Write formulas to combine criteria: "IF funding_date within 180 days AND employee_count between 50-200"

Manual review step (critical)

After Clay returns your filtered list, manually review each prospect. Remove prospects who do not truly fit the segment. Check:

  • LinkedIn profile - Do they actually have decision-making authority?
  • Company website - Does the company match your ICP?
  • Recent activity - Any signs they're actively in-market for your solution?

Spend 2-3 minutes per prospect on manual review. For a 50-person list, this takes 2 hours but dramatically improves list quality.

Pro tip: Save your Clay filters as templates. Once you build one high-performing micro-segment, clone the filter logic and change one variable to create adjacent segments. Example: same filters but swap industry from fintech to healthcare SaaS.

Writing Hyper-Relevant Copy That Feels 1-to-1

▼

Copy structure for micro-segments

Micro-segment campaigns use a hybrid approach: shared template body for the entire segment, unique personalised first line per prospect.

Email structure

ElementPersonalised or Shared?Example
Subject line Shared (segment-specific) "Scaling your SDR team post-Series B"
First line Personalised (unique per prospect) "Saw you just hired James as SDR Manager - congrats"
Hook (lines 2-3) Shared (references segment characteristics) "Most Series B SaaS companies scaling outbound hit the same bottleneck: training new SDRs fast enough to meet pipeline targets."
Value (lines 4-6) Shared (speaks to segment pain) "We help companies like Acme and Datadog cut SDR ramp time from 90 days to 30 days using a structured playbook approach."
CTA Shared "Worth a quick 15 min to walk through the playbook? Calendar link below."

The power of the personalised first line

The first line is the only part that must be unique per prospect. It proves you researched them individually. Sources for first-line personalisation:

  • Recent LinkedIn post - "Loved your post about SDR coaching frameworks last week"
  • Job posting - "Saw you're hiring 5 SDRs this quarter"
  • Company news - "Congrats on the Series B announcement"
  • Mutual connection - "Sarah mentioned you're scaling your outbound team"
  • Recent hire - "Saw you brought James on as SDR Manager"

Template body: reference shared characteristics

The body should explicitly reference the characteristics all prospects share. This proves relevance without requiring per-prospect customisation.

Bad (generic): "We help sales teams improve pipeline."

Good (segment-specific): "Most Series B SaaS companies using Salesforce + Outreach hit this challenge when scaling from 5 SDRs to 20: training consistency."

The second version signals you understand their exact context: stage, tech stack, team size, pain point.

Copy rule: If your template email could be sent to 500 prospects across different industries and stages, it's not segment-specific enough. The copy should only make sense for the 30-50 people in your micro-segment.

Sending Strategy: Spacing Over Days, Not Hours

▼

Why spacing matters for small lists

Sending 50 emails in one hour from the same domain looks automated. Spreading 50 emails over 3-5 days looks manual. Deliverability improves and engagement increases.

Recommended sending cadence

  • 10-15 emails per day - Spread across business hours (9 AM - 4 PM in recipient timezone)
  • Randomise send times - Not every hour on the hour. Use 9:17 AM, 10:43 AM, 1:22 PM, etc.
  • 3-5 day window - For a 50-person list, send over Monday to Friday of one week
  • No weekend sends - Business email opened on weekends has lower engagement

Follow-up strategy

For micro-segments, use shorter follow-up sequences. Fewer touches, higher quality.

TouchTimingChannelContent
1 Day 0 Email Initial outreach (personalised first line + segment template)
2 Day 3 LinkedIn connection request Custom note: "Sent you an email about scaling SDR teams - worth connecting?"
3 Day 6 Email (reply to original thread) Bump: "Following up - did this land at a good time?"
4 Day 10 LinkedIn voice note (if connected) 30-second voice message referencing original email
5 Day 14 Email (final breakup) "Last note - if timing isn't right, I'll check back in Q3"
Pro tip: For prospects who open multiple emails but don't reply, send a manual breakup email referencing their engagement: "Noticed you opened my last 3 emails - is the timing just off, or should I stop reaching out?"

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Segment too broad (over 100 prospects)

If your list has 300 people, it's not a micro-segment. You cannot do individual research on 300 people. The copy becomes generic.
Stack more filters until list is under 50. Add tech stack filter, trigger filter, or tighter company size range. Ruthlessly cut marginal fits.

2. No unifying characteristic beyond title/industry

"VP Sales at SaaS companies" is not a micro-segment. There's no shared context you can reference. Copy feels like any other cold email.
Add at least 2 more dimensions: company stage, tech stack, trigger event. The intersection creates the segment.

3. Using the same copy as large campaigns

The entire point of micro-segments is hyper-relevant copy. If you use generic template language, you wasted the effort of building a micro-segment.
Rewrite the template to explicitly reference the shared characteristics: "Most Series B companies using Salesforce hit this when scaling from 10 to 30 reps..."

4. Skipping individual prospect research

If your first line is "Hi Sarah" with no personalisation, you signal laziness. She can tell you didn't research her.
Spend 3-5 min per prospect. Visit LinkedIn, read recent posts, check company news. Find one unique detail. Add it to first line.

5. Sending all emails in one burst

50 emails sent in 30 minutes from one domain triggers spam filters and looks automated. Lower deliverability, lower engagement.
Space over 3-5 days. 10-15 emails per day. Randomise send times. Use Instantly's delay settings to simulate manual sending.

6. Not iterating based on results

You built one micro-segment, got low response, and gave up. Maybe the segment was wrong, the timing was bad, or the copy missed.
Run 3-5 micro-segments before drawing conclusions. Track what works: which industries, stages, triggers, and copy angles convert best. Double down on winners.

7. No manual follow-up on engagers

Someone opened 3 emails and clicked the link but didn't reply. You left them in the automated sequence instead of manually following up.
Pull high-engagement prospects out of automation. Manually email or call them: "Saw you clicked the link - worth a quick chat?"

8. Forgetting to exclude existing contacts

Your micro-segment includes prospects already in your CRM as active opportunities or customers. Sending cold outreach to them damages relationships.
Always cross-reference your micro-segment list against CRM before sending. Exclude anyone with an open opportunity, closed-won deal, or active conversation.
