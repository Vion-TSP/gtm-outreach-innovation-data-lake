Tactic 1.3 - Reactive Outbound

Micro-Segmented Outreach (Lists Under 50)

Build tiny lists of fewer than 50 prospects who share hyper-specific characteristics. Deep research each person, write segment-specific copy that feels 1-to-1, and achieve reply rates that make broad cold outreach look broken.

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Small lists enable extreme personalization. When you have 500 prospects, you can't deeply research each one. When you have 30, you can. Micro-segments (under 50 people) let you write copy so specific that it feels like a 1-to-1 email, not a blast. Quality beats quantity at this level.

The psychology behind micro-segmentation

  • Shared context creates instant credibility - When your email references a specific combination of traits they share (e.g., "Series A fintech companies using Stripe who just hired a VP Sales"), they think: "This person gets my exact situation". Credibility is instant
  • Scarcity implies exclusivity - A message written for 30 people feels more valuable than one written for 5000. "I'm reaching out to a handful of VPs in Series A fintechs" signals selectivity, not spam
  • Hyper-relevance = low friction - When every sentence in your email applies directly to them, there's no cognitive load. They don't have to translate generic advice into their context. It just fits
  • Research shows through - You can't fake deep knowledge at small scale. When you mention their tech stack, recent blog post, and hiring plan in one email, they know you invested time. Effort earns replies

Why size matters: under 50 is the magic number

At 100+ prospects, you're forced to generalize. At 50 or below, you can invest 5-10 minutes researching each person without the process becoming unsustainable. That 5 minutes per person is the difference between "sort of relevant" and "wow, this person really understands me".

The math: If you spend 5 min researching each of 30 people, that's 2.5 hours total. If that micro-segment converts at 3x the rate of a 500-person broad list, you book more meetings in less time with better qualification. Small beats big when execution quality matters.

Best Practices

What Makes a Good Micro-Segment

▼

The 3-5 trait rule

A micro-segment is defined by 3-5 shared characteristics that create a hyper-specific group. Too few traits (just industry) and you're still too broad. Too many traits (7+ filters) and your list shrinks to 3 people. The sweet spot is 3-5 traits that result in 20-50 prospects.

High-performing trait combinations

Trait 1Trait 2Trait 3Trait 4Example Segment
Industry Company stage Tech stack Recent trigger Series A fintech companies using Salesforce who just hired a VP Sales
Industry Company size Tech adoption Geography 50-200 employee SaaS companies in UK using HubSpot who adopted Gong in the last 6 months
Job title Company stage Funding status Problem signal Heads of Sales at post-Series B companies who raised in last 12 months and are hiring 3+ AEs
Industry Tech stack Company size Content engagement 100-500 employee ecommerce companies using Shopify who engaged with ABM content in last 30 days

What NOT to do

  • Don't segment on one trait only - "All VPs of Sales" is 50,000 people. That's not a micro-segment, it's a mass market
  • Don't use vague traits - "Interested in growth" or "innovative companies" are subjective and impossible to filter accurately
  • Don't mix unrelated traits - "Series A companies OR using Salesforce OR in fintech" creates a messy Venn diagram. Use AND logic, not OR
Test question: If you can write an email where every single sentence applies to every single person on the list, your micro-segment is well-defined. If you have to add caveats or generic language, go narrower.

Optimal List Size (20-50 People)

▼

Why 20-50 is the sweet spot

Under 20 people, you're spending too much time per prospect for too little volume. Over 50 people, you start cutting corners on research and personalization quality drops. The 20-50 range lets you maintain high research quality while generating enough volume to make the effort worthwhile.

List size by segment quality

List SizeResearch Time Per PersonTotal Research TimeWhen to Use
10-20 people 10-15 min 2-5 hours Tier 1 accounts (enterprise, dream customers, high ACV). Deep research justifies low volume
20-35 people 5-10 min 2-6 hours Ideal balance. High personalization, reasonable volume. Most micro-segments should target this range
35-50 people 3-5 min 2-4 hours When the segment is very tightly defined (traits do most of the work). Light research to add individual touches
50-100 people 1-3 min 1-5 hours Not a true micro-segment anymore. Only use if traits are so specific that minimal individual research is needed

When to split a segment

If your initial filters return 80 people, don't just take the first 50. Split into two micro-segments by adding one more filter (e.g., geography or company size). Two 40-person segments with tighter targeting beat one 80-person segment with looser fit.

Pro tip: Start with 25-30 people for your first micro-segment. This gives you room to exclude bad-fit prospects after research (maybe 5-10 drop out) while still having 20+ solid targets.

Writing Copy That Feels 1-to-1

▼

The two-layer approach

Micro-segment emails use two layers: (1) a segment-specific template that applies to everyone, and (2) a 1-2 sentence personalized intro per person using individual research. The template does the heavy lifting, the intro proves you researched them specifically.

Layer 1: Segment-specific template

Write ONE email that applies to every person in the segment. Reference shared traits explicitly so they recognize themselves immediately.

Example (Series A fintech VPs using Salesforce)Subject: Acme + Salesforce Hi Sarah, Reaching out to a handful of VPs at Series A fintech companies who recently adopted Salesforce. Most teams at your stage hit the same wall 90 days post-SFDC implementation: data quality degrades, reps stop logging activity, and forecasting becomes guesswork. We help Series A fintechs (like Acme, Stripe, Brex) keep Salesforce clean and actionable without hiring a full-time ops person. Happy to share how they set it up. Worth a quick chat? James

Layer 2: Personalized intro (1-2 sentences)

Add a short, research-based intro above the template. This proves you looked at them individually, not just blasted the segment.

Example with personalized introSubject: Acme + Salesforce Hi Sarah, Saw you joined Acme 6 months ago and recently rolled out Salesforce to the sales team. Congrats on the momentum. Reaching out to a handful of VPs at Series A fintech companies who recently adopted Salesforce. Most teams at your stage hit the same wall 90 days post-SFDC implementation: data quality degrades, reps stop logging activity, and forecasting becomes guesswork. We help Series A fintechs (like Acme, Stripe, Brex) keep Salesforce clean and actionable without hiring a full-time ops person. Happy to share how they set it up. Worth a quick chat? James

Why this works

  • Segment template creates instant relevance - "Series A fintech VPs using Salesforce" - they read that and think: "This is for me"
  • Personalized intro proves effort - "Saw you joined 6 months ago and rolled out Salesforce" - can't be automated, must have researched
  • Efficiency at scale - Write the template once, spend 2-3 min per person on the intro. 30 emails in under 2 hours
Pro tip: If you can't write a segment-specific template that applies to everyone, your micro-segment isn't tight enough. Go back and add more filters.

When to Split a Segment Further

▼

Signs your segment is too broad

Even at 30-40 people, a segment can be too broad if the shared traits don't create a tight enough cohort. Here are signs you need to split further:

  • Your template feels generic - If you're writing "companies like yours" instead of "Series A fintech companies using Salesforce", the segment lacks specificity
  • You're adding too many conditional phrases - "If you're focused on X" or "depending on your priorities" - these signal the segment includes people with different needs
  • Research reveals two distinct subgroups - Half the list is early-stage using basic tools, half is scaling fast with complex stacks. Split into two segments
  • Reply rate is mediocre - If your first 10-15 sends get low engagement, the segment may be too broad or poorly defined. Tighten it

When NOT to split: If splitting creates segments under 15 people, you've gone too narrow. At that point, just personalize each email fully rather than using a segment template.

Tracking Per-Segment Performance

▼

Why per-segment tracking matters

Broad outreach metrics (average reply rate across all sends) hide what's working. One high-performing micro-segment and three mediocre ones average out to "okay". You miss the insight: double down on the winner, kill the losers.

Metrics to track per segment

MetricWhat It Tells YouGood Benchmark
Reply rate Is the segment well-targeted and copy relevant? Above 15% = great. 8-15% = good. Below 8% = iterate or kill
Positive reply rate Are replies interested or just "not interested"? Above 50% of replies should be positive or neutral (not hard no)
Meeting booked rate Does engagement convert to pipeline? Above 5% of total sends = excellent. 2-5% = good. Below 2% = rethink
Time to first reply How quickly do they engage? (Fast = strong relevance) Under 24 hours = strong signal. 24-48 hours = good. Over 72 hours = weak

Iterating based on results

After sending to 20-30 people in a micro-segment, analyze results within 7 days. Use this decision tree:

  • High reply rate (above 12%) - Replicate the approach. Build 2-3 similar micro-segments with the same structure and copy style
  • Medium reply rate (6-12%) - Iterate on copy. Tighten the segment slightly or rewrite the value prop. Test again with 20 more
  • Low reply rate (below 6%) - Segment is poorly defined or value prop is off. Go back to definition phase. Don't keep sending
Pro tip: Tag each micro-segment in your sending tool (Instantly, Outreach, etc.) with a unique identifier (e.g., "Seg-01-Series-A-Fintech-SFDC"). This makes it easy to filter and analyze performance per segment in your analytics dashboard.

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Lists too large (over 100 people)

You call it a "micro-segment" but it's 200 people. Research quality drops, copy becomes generic, and you lose the core advantage: hyper-relevance.
Cap at 50 people max. If your filters return 100+, add another filter or split into two distinct micro-segments. Small is the entire point.

2. Surface-level segmentation (just industry)

Segment = "all VPs in SaaS". That's 20,000 people with wildly different needs. Not a micro-segment, just a basic filter.
Use 3-5 traits minimum: industry + company stage + tech stack + trigger event. Narrow until you hit 20-50 people with very similar profiles.

3. Not researching individuals

You build a tight micro-segment but skip individual research. Email feels segment-specific but not personal. Loses the "wow, they really looked at us" effect.
Spend 5-10 min per person. Log 2-3 specific details (LinkedIn activity, recent blog post, tech adoption, hiring). Add 1-2 sentence personalized intro per email.

4. Reusing copy across segments

You nail one micro-segment, then copy-paste the email to a different segment with minor tweaks. Recipients notice. Feels templated, not tailored.
Write fresh copy for each micro-segment. Reference the specific shared traits of THAT segment. Don't recycle. Each segment deserves its own template.

5. Not iterating based on results

You send to 30 people, get mediocre replies, then just move on to the next segment. Never learn what worked or didn't. Repeat the same mistakes.
Analyze results after every 20-30 sends. Track reply rate per segment. If below 8%, iterate on segment definition or copy before moving on. Double down on winners.

6. Mixing unrelated people into one segment

Your "micro-segment" includes 20 VPs of Sales and 10 Heads of Marketing because you needed to hit 30 people. Different roles = different needs = bad fit.
Keep segments homogenous. If you have 20 VPs and 10 Heads of Marketing, build TWO micro-segments. Don't force unrelated people into one list just to hit a number.

7. Sending too fast (no batch pacing)

You send all 40 emails in one hour. Email providers flag you as a spammer. Deliverability tanks. Half the emails never reach the inbox.
Batch sends over 3-5 days (10-15 emails per day max). Space them out 30-60 min apart. Slower sending = better deliverability = more replies.

8. No clear next steps after winning segment

You nail a micro-segment (18% reply rate, 4 meetings booked) but don't document what made it work or build similar segments. Lightning in a bottle, never repeated.
When a segment works, document the winning formula: traits used, copy structure, research depth. Replicate it with 2-3 similar segments. Turn wins into repeatable process.
