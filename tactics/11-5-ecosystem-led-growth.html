Tactic 11.5 - Warm Intro / Network

Ecosystem-Led Growth (Partner Overlap)

Find accounts where you and your partner both have relationships. Partner provides warm intro. Coordinate joint approach.

Effort: Medium Cost: Low ICP: All ICPs
01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: You and your partners sell to overlapping customer bases but complementary needs. Accounts already using your partner's product are pre-qualified (they have budget, they buy technology, they have similar pain points). Partner introductions carry immense trust because they risk their customer relationship.

The psychology of partner-led introductions

  • Trusted advisor status - When your partner introduces you, they're essentially saying "I trust this company with my customer." That's powerful social proof
  • Pre-qualified need - If an account uses your partner's product, they likely have the adjacent need you solve. Reduced discovery phase
  • Ecosystem thinking - Buyers increasingly want integrated solutions, not point products. Partner intros position you as part of a cohesive ecosystem
  • Competitive moat - Accounts introduced through partners are less likely to churn. The combined relationship is stickier than standalone

Why ecosystem-led growth accelerates pipeline

Traditional outbound requires building trust from zero. Partner intros start with inherited trust. Your partner has already proven value to this account. Their endorsement shortcuts months of credibility building. Plus, partners are motivated to help because successful intros strengthen their own customer relationships.

The network effect: As you grow your partner ecosystem and systematically share customer data, the number of overlapping accounts grows exponentially. Ten partners with 100 overlapping accounts each creates 1,000 warm intro opportunities.

Best Practices

▼

Partner Selection (Complementary Not Competitive)

▼

What makes a good ecosystem partner

The best partners sell to the same ICP but solve adjacent problems. You want high customer overlap without product conflict. If you compete directly, neither party will make intros for fear of losing deals.

Ideal partner characteristics

CharacteristicWhy It MattersExample
Same ICP, different problem Maximum overlap, zero competition If you sell sales engagement software, partner with sales intelligence (ZoomInfo) or conversation intelligence (Gong)
Integration or workflow connection Natural reason to co-sell Your product integrates with their product. Joint solution is better than standalone
Similar deal size and sales cycle Alignment on customer type and effort Both selling to mid-market at similar ACV. Easier to coordinate than mixing enterprise and SMB motions
Mature customer base More accounts to overlap with Partner with 500+ customers gives more overlap opportunity than partner with 50 customers
Willing to reciprocate Partnership must be two-way Partner actively makes intros for you, not just receiving intros from you. Balanced exchange

Red flag partners to avoid

  • Direct competitors - Even if you claim different positioning, competing for same budget creates conflict
  • No customer overlap - Partner sells to completely different ICP. Minimal overlap means minimal value from partnership
  • One-sided takers - Partner only asks for intros, never reciprocates. Depletes your goodwill quickly
  • Poor reputation - Partner has quality issues or bad customer experiences. Their intro damages your credibility rather than enhancing it
Vetting tip: Before formalising partnership, run a quick overlap analysis. Connect CRMs via Reveal trial. If you see fewer than 20 overlapping accounts, the partnership probably isn't worth the coordination effort yet.

Data Sharing Agreement (Protect Sensitive Information)

▼

Why formal agreements matter

Sharing CRM data with partners is powerful but risky. Without clear agreements, you risk exposing sensitive deal information, pricing, contacts, or pipeline to companies who might misuse it. Legal and commercial guardrails protect both parties.

Key elements of data sharing agreements

ElementWhat to Cover
Scope of data sharing What data is shared (company names only? Contact names? Deal stages? Revenue?). What is explicitly excluded (pricing, contract terms, internal notes)
Confidentiality obligations Partners must treat shared data as confidential. Cannot share with third parties without permission. Must secure data appropriately
Customer consent How to handle customer data privacy. Some industries (healthcare, finance) require explicit consent before sharing customer information
Usage restrictions Data can only be used for partnership purposes, not general prospecting or competitive intelligence
Termination process What happens to shared data if partnership ends. Must be deleted or returned. No ongoing use

Privacy-preserving approaches

  • Hash-based matching - Tools like Reveal use hashed email addresses so neither party sees raw data until both opt to share specific accounts
  • Tiered disclosure - Start with company names only. Reveal contact details and deal stage only after both parties agree to collaborate on specific account
  • Aggregate reporting only - Share stats ("We have 50 overlapping accounts") without revealing which specific accounts
  • Customer-first approach - Before making intro, ask customer "Would you like to hear about our partner's complementary solution?" Get opt-in
Legal tip: Have your legal team review data sharing agreement before connecting CRMs. Standard Reveal TOS may not cover all your requirements. Add custom clauses for your industry or business model if needed.

Coordination Protocol (Who Leads, Who Supports)

▼

Why coordination matters

When two companies approach the same account, confusion about roles kills deals. Who owns the customer relationship? Who takes the lead in conversations? Who handles contracting? Clear protocols prevent conflicts and improve win rates.

Coordination decision tree

ScenarioLead CompanySupporting Company
Partner is existing customer, you are new Partner (they have relationship) You (partner introduces you, you run sales process)
You are existing customer, partner is new You (you have relationship) Partner (you introduce partner, they run sales process)
Both pursuing as prospects Whoever engaged first or has stronger relationship Other party. Coordinate joint value prop
Neither has relationship Whoever has stronger ICP fit or larger deal size Other party. Joint outreach from start

Communication cadence between partners

  • Weekly sync calls - 30 min standing call to review new overlaps, coordinate on active deals, report on outcomes
  • Slack channel - Dedicated partner Slack channel for quick questions, intro requests, deal updates
  • Quarterly business review - Formal QBR reviewing partnership metrics: intros made, pipeline generated, deals closed, revenue share
  • Immediate escalation protocol - If conflict arises (both reaching out to same contact, pricing confusion), escalation path to resolve quickly

Joint discovery call best practices

  • Pre-call alignment - 15 min before customer call, align on who covers what, key questions, combined value prop
  • Lead company drives agenda - Lead company runs the call, supporting company joins to answer specific questions about their product
  • Integrated narrative - Don't present as two separate products. Present as integrated solution solving connected problems
  • Clear next steps - End call with specific next steps owned by specific party. No ambiguity on follow-up
Rule of thumb: The party with the existing customer relationship always leads. If neither has relationship, whoever has stronger ICP fit leads. Supporting party defers but adds value through expertise and integration story.

Revenue Attribution (Track Partner-Sourced Deals)

▼

Why attribution matters

You can't optimise what you don't measure. Tracking which deals originated from partner intros shows partnership ROI, identifies best-performing partners, and ensures proper compensation (referral fees or rev share) where applicable.

Minimum viable attribution tracking

Data PointPurposeWhere to Track
Partner source Which partner made the intro CRM field: Partner Source (dropdown)
Intro date When intro was made CRM activity date or deal created date
Intro type Warm intro vs joint discovery vs co-marketing lead CRM field: Partner Intro Type (dropdown)
Deal stage progression Track velocity from intro to close Standard CRM deal stages with date stamps
Closed revenue Calculate partner-sourced revenue CRM deal amount + Partner Source tag
Referral fee paid Track compensation if applicable Finance system or CRM custom field

Key metrics to monitor

  • Partner-sourced pipeline - Total $ value of open opportunities introduced by partners
  • Partner intro conversion rate - What percentage of partner intros convert to customers? Should be significantly higher than cold outbound
  • Deal velocity - Days from intro to close. Partner-sourced deals should close faster due to inherited trust
  • Revenue by partner - Which partners generate most closed revenue? Double down on best-performing partnerships
  • Reciprocity balance - Are you making as many intros for partners as they make for you? Track both directions
Reporting tip: Create quarterly partner scorecard showing intros made (by you), intros received (from partner), conversion rates, closed revenue. Share with partners to demonstrate value and maintain accountability.
05

Common Mistakes

▼

1. No formal data sharing agreement

You connect CRMs without legal agreements. Partner misuses data or exposes sensitive information. Legal and commercial risks materialize.
Create formal data sharing agreement before connecting CRMs. Cover confidentiality, usage restrictions, and termination process.

2. One-sided relationship

You constantly ask partner for intros but never reciprocate. Partner feels used. They stop making intros or end partnership.
Track intros in both directions. Aim for balanced exchange. Actively look for opportunities to introduce partner to your customers.

3. No coordination on messaging

You and partner approach same account with conflicting messages or uncoordinated timing. Customer gets confused. Deal dies.
Establish coordination protocol before joint outreach. Weekly sync calls to align on active accounts and messaging.

4. Expecting partner to do all the work

You expect partner to make intro, educate customer on your product, and drive the deal. Partner has their own priorities. Intro doesn't convert.
Partner provides warm intro and context. You own the sales process from there. Don't delegate your job to the partner.

5. Not tracking joint revenue

You don't track which deals came from partner intros. Can't prove partnership ROI. Partnership loses executive support and dies.
Tag all partner-sourced deals in CRM. Track conversion rates and revenue. Report quarterly to demonstrate value.

6. Partnering with competitors

You try to partner with direct competitor. Neither party makes meaningful intros because you're fighting for same budget. Partnership fails.
Partner with complementary companies solving adjacent problems for same ICP. Avoid direct competitors.
