Tactic 1.7 - Reactive Outbound

Trigger Library Approach

Pre-written insight blocks tied to signals. Assemble email from blocks plus personalised opener. Speed plus quality at scale.

01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Quality personalisation does not require starting from scratch every time. The most effective insights can be pre-written, categorised by trigger type, and assembled modularly with a custom opener. You get 80% of the quality in 20% of the time.

The problem with traditional prospecting

  • Fully custom emails take too long - Writing a truly thoughtful, insight-driven email takes 15-25 minutes per prospect. At that pace, SDRs send 15-20 emails per day, not 80-100
  • Fully templated emails convert poorly - Generic template blasts get ignored. Response rates hover around 1-2% because they lack relevance and feel mass-produced
  • The false choice - Teams think they must choose between quality (slow, manual) or quantity (fast, templated). The trigger library gives you both

How the trigger library solves this

The library contains pre-written insight blocks tied to specific signals or triggers. When an SDR spots a relevant signal, they select the matching insight block, write a 1-2 sentence personalised opener, and combine them into a natural-sounding email. The entire process takes 2-3 minutes per email.

The insight blocks are written once by your best writers, tested and refined over time, and reused across hundreds of prospects. The personalised opener ensures the email feels custom and shows you did the research.

The psychology

  • Modular quality scales - Pre-written insight blocks can be higher quality than ad-hoc writing because they are crafted carefully, tested, and refined. You get A-plus writing at scale
  • Context triggers trust - The personalised opener anchors the email in real context (their funding round, job posting, product launch). This creates trust before the recipient even reads the insight
  • Consistency across team - Junior SDRs can send emails that sound like they came from your best writer, because they are literally using your best writer's blocks

Best Practices

▼

Building the Library

▼

How to structure the library

The library is organised by signal type. Each signal has multiple insight blocks and CTA options. Structure it as: Signal → Insight Block → CTA.

Example categories:

  • Funding signals - Seed, Series A, Series B, growth rounds
  • Hiring signals - Sales roles, marketing roles, engineering roles
  • Product signals - Launches, feature releases, pricing changes
  • Competitor signals - Switching from competitor, mentioning competitor pain
  • Expansion signals - New office, new market entry, international expansion
  • Tech stack signals - Adopting new tools, deprecating old tools

Categories of blocks

Industry insights - Broader trends or data points relevant to their space. Example: "SaaS companies that cross $10M ARR typically shift from founder-led sales to structured outbound. The transition is messy without a clear handoff process."

Competitor angles - Insight tied to switching costs, common competitor pain points. Example: "Teams moving off Outreach often cite deliverability issues and lack of signal-based automation. The migration takes 2-4 weeks if done right."

Trigger-specific - Insight directly tied to the signal they exhibited. Example: "Post-Series B teams usually double headcount in 12-18 months. The bottleneck is not hiring, it is onboarding and ramping new reps fast enough."

Pain-point specific - Insight about a common pain point for their persona or industry. Example: "Most sales leaders spend 60% of their time on pipeline reviews and forecast calls. The best teams automate this with signal-based pipeline scoring."

Writing blocks that sound natural when combined

  • Write insight blocks in second person ("you", "your team") so they flow naturally after a personalised opener
  • Avoid jargon or overly formal language - conversational tone wins
  • Keep blocks 2-3 sentences max - longer blocks feel like essays, not emails
  • End insight blocks with a natural transition point - do not end mid-thought
  • Test combinations - paste different openers with different blocks and read aloud to check flow

Version control

Use Notion's version history or Google Docs' revision history to track changes. When you update a block, note the reason and date. Over time, you will refine blocks based on what works. Do not lose the old versions - sometimes they work better for certain ICPs.

The Assembly Process

▼

How an SDR uses the library in practice

The daily workflow for an SDR using the trigger library:

  1. Spot signal - Clay feed or manual research surfaces a prospect with a relevant trigger. Example: James at Acme just posted a job opening for 3 AEs
  2. Find matching blocks - SDR opens Notion library, navigates to Hiring Signals category, reviews the 3-4 insight blocks available, selects the one that fits best
  3. Write 1-2 personalised sentences as opener - SDR writes a custom intro anchored in the real signal: "Saw you are hiring 3 AEs at Acme - looks like you are scaling fast, James."
  4. Combine - Paste the insight block after the opener. Paste a matching CTA block at the end
  5. Review for flow - Read the assembled email aloud. Does it sound natural? Any awkward transitions? Edit if needed (usually takes 10-20 seconds)
  6. Send - Send via Instantly or Gmail. Log in CRM

Total time per email: 2-3 minutes. At this pace, an SDR can send 80-100 personalised emails per day without sacrificing quality.

Maintaining the Library

▼

Weekly review of what is working

Every Friday, review which insight blocks are driving responses and which are not. Pull reply rate data by block (tag each email with the block ID in your CRM or Instantly). Blocks with sub-2% reply rates need to be rewritten or retired.

Retiring stale blocks

Blocks go stale over time. Industry trends change, competitor landscapes shift, buyer priorities evolve. If a block has not been used in 60 days, archive it. If it is getting used but not performing, rewrite it. Do not let dead weight clutter the library - SDRs should only see blocks that work.

Adding new blocks from successful emails

When an SDR writes a fully custom email that gets a great response, extract the insight and turn it into a library block. This is how the library grows organically over time. Best practice: SDRs submit new block suggestions weekly, sales leadership reviews and approves.

A/B testing blocks against each other

For high-volume signals (funding rounds, hiring posts), write 2-3 insight blocks and rotate them. Track reply rates for each. After 50 sends per block, compare performance. Keep the winner, retire the losers. Continuous improvement.

Pro tip: Treat the library as a living asset, not a static document. The best libraries evolve weekly based on real performance data. Stale libraries die because SDRs stop trusting them.

Scaling Across Teams

▼

Onboarding new SDRs with the library

New hires ramp faster with a library. Instead of learning to write from scratch, they learn to assemble from tested blocks. Onboarding process:

  1. Day 1: Review the library structure, understand signal categories
  2. Day 2: Shadow a senior SDR assembling emails using the library
  3. Day 3: Assemble 10 practice emails, get feedback from manager
  4. Day 4: Send first 20 emails using library, manager spot-checks
  5. Week 2: SDR is fully autonomous, contributing new block suggestions

Result: New SDRs hit quota-level email volume and quality within 2 weeks, not 2 months.

Quality control

Weekly spot-checks on assembled emails. Managers review 5-10 sent emails per SDR per week. Look for: awkward transitions, blocks used incorrectly, weak personalised openers. Provide feedback in weekly 1-on-1s. Teams with tight QC loops see 20-30% higher reply rates than teams without.

Shared vs personal blocks

The main library is shared across the team - these are the tested, approved blocks everyone uses. But allow SDRs to maintain a personal library of blocks they have written that work for their specific ICP or style. Best practice: Personal blocks that perform well get promoted to the shared library after manager review.

Pro tip: Run monthly library workshops. Entire SDR team reviews top-performing blocks, workshopping new ideas, and voting on what to add or retire. This keeps the team bought in and engaged with the library as a strategic asset, not just a tool.

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Blocks that sound robotic when combined

Writing blocks in isolation without testing combinations leads to awkward transitions and stilted language. Recipient immediately senses it is templated.
Write blocks in second person, test combinations with different openers, read aloud before adding to library. If it sounds unnatural, rewrite.

2. Not updating stale blocks

Industry trends change. Competitor pain points shift. A block written 6 months ago may no longer resonate. Stale blocks kill reply rates and erode trust in the library.
Weekly review of block performance. Retire blocks with sub-2% reply rates. Rewrite blocks that reference outdated trends or data. Treat library as living asset.

3. SDRs skipping the personalised opener

Some SDRs try to save time by using library blocks without writing a custom opener. Result: Email feels generic and templated, reply rate drops by half.
Make the personalised opener non-negotiable. Manager spot-checks emails weekly. Celebrate examples of great openers in team meetings. Opener is what makes the library work.

4. Library too complex (over 50 blocks)

A library with 80-100 blocks is impossible to navigate. SDRs waste time searching for the right block, or worse, stop using the library altogether.
Cap library at 30-40 blocks total. Ruthlessly retire underperformers. Use clear naming conventions and category filters. Simple beats comprehensive.

5. No quality review process

Without spot-checks, SDRs start using blocks incorrectly, combining blocks that do not flow, or writing weak openers. Quality degrades over time without oversight.
Weekly manager review of 5-10 sent emails per SDR. Provide specific feedback in 1-on-1s. Run monthly library workshops to review what is working and what is not.

6. Not tracking which block combos work best

Teams build the library but never analyse performance. You do not know which insight blocks drive replies and which fall flat. Missed opportunity for optimisation.
Tag each email with block IDs in CRM or Instantly. Pull reply rate data by block weekly. Double down on winners, retire losers. Data-driven library beats gut-driven library.
