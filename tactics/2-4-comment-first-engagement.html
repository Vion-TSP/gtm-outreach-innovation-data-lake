Tactic 2.4 - LinkedIn-Native

Comment-First Engagement Strategy

Engage with 3-5 prospect posts over 2 weeks before outreach. Builds familiarity and credibility by adding value in their feed before asking for their time. Warm approach for senior buyers who ignore cold messages.

Li
LinkedIn (Primary)
Effort: Medium Cost: Low ICP: Founders, C-suite, Sales Leaders
01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Senior buyers ignore cold connection requests. But they notice who engages with their content. By commenting thoughtfully on 3-5 posts over 2 weeks, you build familiarity before asking for anything. When you eventually send a connection request, you're not a stranger - you're the person who added value to their posts.

The psychology behind comment-first engagement

  • Reciprocity trigger - You gave them value (thoughtful engagement, visibility boost) before asking for their time. Reciprocity bias makes them more likely to accept your request
  • Familiarity principle - Humans trust familiar names. After seeing your name 3-5 times in their notifications, your connection request feels less cold
  • Social proof signal - Commenting publicly signals you're part of their professional community, not a random spammer
  • Pattern interruption - Most outreach asks immediately. Giving first (engagement) before asking (connection) breaks the pattern and stands out

Why this works for senior buyers specifically

Founders, C-suite, and Sales Leaders receive 20-50 cold connection requests per week. They ignore most. But they post content hoping for engagement. When you comment thoughtfully, you're giving them something they actually want: validation, visibility, and conversation on their posts. This reverses the power dynamic.

The timing advantage: By engaging over 2 weeks, you avoid the spam signal. A single comment followed by an immediate connection request looks transactional. Five comments over 14 days looks like genuine interest in their ideas.

Best Practices

▼

What Makes a Good Comment

▼

Good comments share these traits

  • Adds insight - Builds on their idea with a new angle, data point, or contrarian perspective. Example: "This mirrors what we saw at James Corp - reducing call scripts actually increased conversion by 18% because reps sounded more natural."
  • Asks a thoughtful question - Shows you engaged deeply with their post. Example: "Curious how you balanced standardisation vs customisation when training your SDR team on this approach?"
  • Shares a related experience - Connects their insight to your own story. Example: "Went through this exact transition last year. The hardest part wasn't the tech - it was getting buy-in from sales ops. How did you approach that?"
  • Disagrees respectfully (when genuine) - Thoughtful disagreement can spark engagement. Example: "Interesting take. I've actually found the opposite works for enterprise deals - longer emails with proof points outperform short ones. Maybe segment-dependent?"

What NOT to comment

  • "Great post!" - Generic praise. Zero thought. Signals you didn't read it
  • "Thanks for sharing" - Adds no value. Filler comment
  • Emoji only - 👏💯🔥 without text. Lazy engagement
  • Self-promotional - "We help with this at Acme, DM me" - Spammy. Save it for later
  • One-word responses - "Agreed." "100%." "Exactly." - Too short to be memorable
Length target: 2-4 sentences (30-60 words). Long enough to add value, short enough that others read it. If your comment is longer than their original post, you're overthinking it.

Engagement Cadence

▼

Optimal commenting frequency per prospect

TimelineNumber of CommentsSpacingWhy This Works
Week 1 2 comments 3-4 days apart Introduces your name to their notifications without overwhelming. Establishes you as someone interested in their content
Week 2 2-3 comments 2-3 days apart Reinforces familiarity. By comment 4-5, your name feels familiar, not random
End of Week 2 Connection request 24-48h after last comment Send while you're still fresh in their mind, but not immediately after a comment (avoids looking transactional)

Managing multiple prospects simultaneously

Don't try to warm 50 prospects at once. Start with 10-15 active prospects in your engagement rotation at any given time. As prospects accept your connection request (or reject it), rotate them out and add new prospects to your warming pool.

Daily engagement habit: Block 10-15 minutes per day (mornings work best - catch posts from previous evening/night). Scroll through recent posts from your warming list. Leave 3-5 quality comments per day total across all prospects. This keeps your engagement consistent without burning out.

Rotation strategy: Maintain a rolling 30-day cycle. Week 1-2: warm new prospects. Week 3: send connection requests to warmed prospects. Week 4: engage with newly connected prospects, add new prospects to warming pool. Repeat.

Mixing engagement types

Your 3-5 engagement touches don't all need to be comments. Ideal mix: 3-4 comments + 1 share with commentary. The share signals you valued their content enough to amplify it, which is more memorable than likes or comments alone.

When to share: Only share posts that are genuinely valuable to your network. Sharing low-quality content just to warm a prospect damages your own credibility. If their content isn't share-worthy, stick to comments and likes.

Transitioning from Commenter to Connector

▼

Crafting the connection request note

After 3-5 quality engagements over 1-2 weeks, send your connection request. The note should reference your engagement without being transactional.

Connection note templates

ScenarioTemplateWhy It Works
General engagement "Sarah - been following your posts on sales hiring. Really resonated with your take on founder-led sales transitions. Would love to connect." References their content theme, not a specific pitch. Shows you've been paying attention
After sharing their post "James - shared your post on SDR metrics with my team yesterday. Sparked a great conversation. Let's connect." Highlights that you amplified their content. Strong reciprocity signal
Shared experience angle "Sarah - your posts on scaling outbound mirror what we went through at Acme last year. Would be great to compare notes." Positions you as a peer with relevant experience, not a vendor

What happens after they accept

Do not immediately pitch. Continue engaging with their content for another 3-5 posts after connecting. Then send a message referencing a recent post: "Your post on X got me thinking - we tackled this at Acme by doing Y. Worth a quick chat?"

Transition timeline: Connection accepted → Engage with 3-5 more posts over 1-2 weeks → Send first message (asking, not pitching) → If they respond, continue conversation → Pitch 3-5 messages in, not in message one.

Patience pays: The prospects who convert from this approach take 3-4 weeks from first engagement to first meeting. That's intentional. Slow burn builds trust. If you rush the pitch, you undo all the goodwill from your engagement.

Tracking Who You're Engaging With

▼

Why tracking matters

Without tracking, you'll forget who you've engaged with, when you last commented, and whether you've sent a connection request. This leads to awkward situations: commenting too frequently, sending duplicate requests, or losing track of warmed prospects entirely.

Minimum viable tracking system

Use a simple spreadsheet or CRM. Minimum fields:

  • Name - Prospect full name
  • LinkedIn URL - Direct link to their profile
  • Date First Commented - Start of warming period
  • Comment Count - Track toward 3-5 target
  • Last Engagement Date - Avoid over-commenting or going silent too long
  • Connection Request Sent - Date sent (or "Not Yet")
  • Status - Warming / Pending / Connected / Rejected

Scaling from 10 to 50+ prospects

Once this becomes part of your daily routine, you can scale from 10-15 active warming prospects to 30-50 by using tools like Taplio or Aware to surface recent posts in a single dashboard. At 50 prospects, you're commenting 10-15x per day, which is sustainable in a 20 min daily block.

Quality over quantity: 10 prospects with 5 thoughtful comments each beats 50 prospects with generic engagement. Start small, master the cadence, then scale.

Scaling Beyond Manual Engagement

▼

When you hit the limits of manual engagement

At 50+ active prospects, manual engagement becomes unsustainable for a solo rep. Two options: hire a dedicated engagement SDR, or layer in semi-automated tools (with extreme caution around LinkedIn ToS).

Team-based approach: engagement SDRs

Some teams hire an SDR specifically for engagement. Their job: comment on 50-100 prospect posts per day on behalf of the AE team. The AE reviews high-engagement prospects weekly and takes over the relationship from there. This works best at scale (5+ AEs feeding prospects to the engagement SDR).

Automation tools (use cautiously)

Tools like Phantombuster or Dux-Soup can automate likes and basic comments, but LinkedIn actively bans accounts for automation. If you go this route: use conservative limits (max 50 actions/day), rotate IP addresses, and avoid generic comments. High risk of account suspension.

TSP recommendation: Stay manual until you hit 50+ prospects. At that point, either rotate prospects monthly (keep 30 active at a time) or invest in Taplio + VA support (VA finds posts, you write comments). Avoid full automation - the quality drop isn't worth the time savings.

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Generic comments that add no value

"Great post!" or "Thanks for sharing" signal you didn't actually read it. Zero memorability. You're just another bot-like engagement.
Write 2-4 sentences that add insight, ask a question, or share a related experience. If you can't think of a valuable comment, skip that post and wait for a better one.

2. Engaging once then immediately pitching

Leaving one comment then sending a connection request with a sales pitch 2 hours later is transparent and transactional. Kills all goodwill.
Minimum 3 engagements spread over 1-2 weeks before connecting. Then continue engaging after connection before pitching. Patience builds trust.

3. Commenting too frequently (stalker vibes)

Commenting on every post they publish (daily or multiple times per day) feels desperate or stalky. They'll notice and it's off-putting.
Max 2-3 comments per week per prospect. Space them out every 2-3 days. Let other posts go by without engagement. You want to be present, not obsessive.

4. Not tracking who you're warming

Without tracking, you lose context: Did I already comment on their posts? Did I send a connection request? When did I last engage? This leads to duplicate requests or ghosting warmed prospects.
Use a simple spreadsheet or CRM to track: Name, Date First Commented, Comment Count, Last Engagement, Connection Status. Update it after every engagement.

5. Pitching in the connection request

Connection note: "Love your content. We help companies like yours with X. Let's chat." = Instant rejection. The connection request isn't a sales opportunity.
Connection note should reference your engagement and express interest in their ideas, not your product. "Been following your posts on X. Really resonated. Let's connect." Save the pitch for message 3-5 after acceptance.

6. Giving up after 1 week

Commenting 2-3 times in week one then moving on because you don't see immediate results. Familiarity takes time to build.
Commit to 2 full weeks minimum (3-5 engagements) before sending connection request. If they don't accept, continue engaging for another week before moving on. Some prospects take 3-4 weeks to warm.
