01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: LinkedIn's algorithm massively favours carousel posts (multi-slide PDFs). They get 3-5x more engagement than single-image or text-only posts. Carousels keep users on LinkedIn longer (swiping through slides), so the platform rewards them with more reach.

The psychology behind carousel posts

  • Pattern interrupt - Carousels stand out in the feed. Most posts are text or single images. The swipeable format catches attention and invites interaction
  • Curiosity loop - Each slide creates curiosity about the next. Slide 1 hooks with a problem. Slide 2 teases the solution. Users keep swiping to get the full story
  • Actionable value in digestible chunks - Long-form posts overwhelm. Carousels break insights into bite-sized slides. Each slide delivers one idea, making complex topics easy to consume
  • Save and share behaviour - LinkedIn shows you how many people saved your post. Carousels get saved more than any other format because they're reference-worthy. People save them to revisit later or share with colleagues

Why carousels outperform other content formats

Carousels combine visual appeal (design draws attention), educational value (each slide teaches something), and algorithmic preference (LinkedIn promotes them). A well-designed carousel delivers a micro-lesson in 2 minutes. This builds authority without requiring prospects to read a 2000-word blog post or watch a 30-minute video.

The engagement flywheel: More swipes signal engagement. LinkedIn interprets engagement as quality. Quality content gets more reach. More reach drives more engagement. Carousels trigger this flywheel better than static posts.

Best Practices

▼

Topic Selection (Solve One Specific Problem per Carousel)

▼

Why narrow topics beat broad topics

Broad carousels ("10 tips for better sales") are forgettable. Narrow carousels ("How to write cold email subject lines that get opened") are actionable. Narrow topics attract the right audience and get saved more because they solve a specific problem.

How to choose a carousel topic

  • One pain point, one carousel - Each carousel should address a single problem your ICP faces. Don't try to cover multiple topics. Depth beats breadth
  • Look for high-friction moments - What causes your ICP to pause and search for help? Those are your topics. Example: "How to get executive buy-in for new tools" or "How to prioritise features with limited resources"
  • Mine customer questions - What do customers ask during sales calls? What confusion appears in support tickets? Turn common questions into carousels
  • Check what competitors are posting - See what carousel topics get high engagement in your space. You can create your own version with better insights or a unique angle

Good vs bad carousel topics

Bad (Too Broad)Good (Specific)
"10 ways to improve your GTM strategy" "How to align sales and marketing on ICP definition"
"Best practices for cold outreach" "The 5-step framework for writing cold emails that get replies"
"How to use data in your business" "3 metrics that predict churn (and how to track them)"
Topic validation: Before creating a carousel, search LinkedIn for similar posts. If you find 5-plus posts on the topic with high engagement, it's validated. If you find zero, either you've discovered a gap or the topic isn't resonant. Test it.

Slide Structure (Hook Slide, Problem, Steps, CTA)

▼

Anatomy of a high-performing carousel

Great carousels follow a predictable structure: hook to grab attention, problem to create relevance, steps or insights to deliver value, and CTA to drive action. This structure guides readers through a clear narrative.

Slide-by-slide breakdown

SlidePurposeExample
Slide 1: Hook Grab attention immediately. Bold statement, question, or surprising stat. Make them want to swipe "Your cold emails are getting ignored. Here's why." or "5 mistakes killing your LinkedIn outreach"
Slide 2: Problem setup Expand on the hook. Explain why this problem matters. Create urgency or pain "Most reps send 100-plus emails per week and get 2 replies. The problem: generic messaging."
Slides 3-10: Core content Deliver the solution. Step-by-step framework, tips, or insights. One idea per slide. Keep it actionable Step 1: Research the prospect. Step 2: Personalise the first line. Step 3: Focus on their problem, not your product. Etc.
Slide 11: Recap Summarise key takeaways. Reinforce the value delivered "Recap: Research, personalise, focus on their pain, keep it short, clear CTA."
Slide 12: CTA Tell readers what to do next. Follow you, comment, DM, share. Make it specific "Found this helpful? Follow me for more GTM insights. Drop a comment with your biggest cold email challenge."

Slide length guidance

  • 8-12 slides is the sweet spot - Fewer than 8 feels incomplete. More than 12 and completion rate drops. Most high-performing carousels are 10 slides
  • One idea per slide - Don't cram multiple points into one slide. Break complex ideas into multiple slides. Makes content easier to digest
  • Text per slide - Aim for 10-20 words per slide. Headlines should be 5-7 words. Body text 1-2 short sentences max. More text reduces readability on mobile
Hook formula: Start with a bold claim, stat, or question. Examples: "90% of sales decks fail. Here's why." or "What if I told you cold calling still works?" or "The biggest mistake in cold outreach (and how to fix it)." Test different hooks to see what resonates.

Design Tips (Bold Text, Minimal Words per Slide, Consistent Brand)

▼

Design principles for high-performing carousels

Great design is invisible. It makes content easy to read and visually appealing without being distracting. Bold text, consistent branding, and minimal words are the foundation.

Typography best practices

  • Use large, bold fonts - Slide headlines should be 60-80pt. Body text 30-40pt. Most viewing happens on mobile; small text is unreadable
  • Limit fonts to 2 max - One for headlines, one for body text. Using 3-plus fonts looks messy and unprofessional
  • High contrast - Dark text on light background or light text on dark background. Avoid low-contrast colour combos (grey on white, yellow on white)
  • Left-align text - Centre alignment works for headlines but makes body text harder to read. Stick to left-aligned for multi-line text

Visual hierarchy

ElementTreatmentWhy
Headline Large, bold, top of slide First thing readers see. Should communicate the slide's main idea
Body text Smaller, below headline, 1-2 sentences max Expands on headline. Keeps slides scannable
Icons or graphics Simple, supporting (not decorative) Visual aids help reinforce the message. Don't use clipart or generic stock photos
Branding Logo in corner, consistent colours Subtle but present. Users should know it's your content without branding being distracting

Colour and branding

  • Stick to your brand colours - Use 2-3 colours consistently across all slides. This builds brand recognition. People should recognise your carousels before seeing your name
  • Use colour intentionally - Highlight key words or phrases with a contrasting colour. Don't make every slide a different colour; it looks chaotic
  • White space matters - Don't fill every pixel. Generous margins and spacing make slides easier to read. Cluttered slides overwhelm
Mobile-first design: 80-plus percent of LinkedIn engagement happens on mobile. Design slides on desktop but view them on mobile before posting. If text is hard to read on a phone screen, increase font size or reduce word count.

Caption Writing (Hook First Line, Context, CTA)

▼

Why the caption matters as much as the carousel

The carousel delivers the content, but the caption sells the click. Without a strong caption, people scroll past. The first line is critical because LinkedIn truncates after one line; users must click "see more" to read the rest.

Caption structure

SectionPurposeExample
First line (hook) Grab attention before "see more." Make it punchy and curiosity-driven "Cold emails landing in spam? Here's the fix." or "Your sales deck is boring. Here's why."
Context (2-3 sentences) Expand on the hook. Explain what the carousel covers and why it matters "Most reps don't realise their emails trigger spam filters. I've analysed 500-plus cold campaigns. Here are the 5 mistakes killing deliverability."
Preview of value Tease what's inside the carousel without giving it all away "In this carousel: SPF/DKIM setup, email warmup strategy, content red flags, volume best practices, and tools to track deliverability."
CTA Tell readers what to do. Follow, comment, share, DM. Make it specific "Found this helpful? Follow me for more GTM insights. Comment with your biggest email challenge and I'll respond."

Caption length

  • Keep it short - 100-150 words max. Long captions get skipped. People want to swipe through the carousel, not read a blog post
  • Use line breaks - Break caption into short paragraphs (1-2 sentences each). Makes it scannable. Blocks of text are hard to read on mobile
  • Avoid hashtags - LinkedIn's algorithm no longer rewards hashtags. They make captions look spammy. Focus on strong copy instead

Hook formulas that work

  • Problem statement - "Your cold emails are getting ignored. Here's why."
  • Bold claim - "Cold calling is dead. Here's what works instead."
  • Question - "Why do most sales decks fail? I analysed 200 to find out."
  • Surprising stat - "90% of LinkedIn messages go unanswered. Here's how to be in the 10%."
Testing hooks: The first line determines click-through rate. Test different hooks on similar carousels. Track which ones drive more engagement. Double down on high-performing patterns.

Posting Cadence (1-2 Per Week)

▼

How often to post carousels

Carousels are high-effort content. Posting daily burns you out and dilutes quality. Posting once a month makes you forgettable. The sweet spot: 1-2 carousels per week, supplemented with regular text or single-image posts on other days.

Weekly posting rhythm

DayContent TypeWhy
Monday Carousel post High engagement day. People catching up after the weekend
Tuesday-Wednesday Text posts, single-image posts, engagement Stay visible without creating new carousels. Comment on others' posts
Thursday Carousel post (optional, if doing 2/week) Mid-week engagement spike. Good for tutorials or frameworks
Friday Light content (insights, quick tips) Engagement drops Friday afternoon. Save heavy content for Mon-Thu

Batching carousel creation

  • Script 4-5 carousels in one session - Set aside 2 hours. Outline topics and write slide scripts for multiple carousels. This is faster than scripting one at a time
  • Design them all at once - Once scripts are done, design all carousels in one session using the same template. Batch work is more efficient
  • Schedule or queue for posting - Now you have 4-5 weeks of content ready. Post on your chosen cadence without scrambling each week

Posting time optimisation

LinkedIn engagement peaks during work hours. Best posting times: 8-10 AM or 12-2 PM in your audience's timezone. Avoid evenings and weekends when engagement drops. Check your LinkedIn analytics to see when your audience is most active and post accordingly.

Consistency beats volume: Posting 1 carousel per week for 12 weeks beats posting 3 in one week then nothing for 2 months. Build a habit. Your audience will expect and look forward to your carousels.

Optimal Slide Count (8-12)

▼

Why 8-12 slides is the optimal range

Too few slides and your carousel feels incomplete or surface-level. Too many slides and completion rate drops as users get bored or overwhelmed. Data shows 8-12 slides maximises engagement and completion rate.

Slide count by content type

Content TypeRecommended SlidesWhy
Step-by-step frameworks 10-12 slides Hook (1), intro (1), 5-7 steps (1 per slide), recap (1), CTA (1). Covers full framework without feeling rushed
Tips or insights 8-10 slides Hook (1), intro (1), 5 tips (1 per slide), recap (1), CTA (1). Short and punchy
Mistakes to avoid 8-10 slides Hook (1), intro (1), 5 mistakes (1 per slide), recap (1), CTA (1). Negative framing hooks attention
Deep-dive tutorials 12-15 slides Only go longer if topic demands it. Example: "Complete guide to email deliverability" justifies 15 slides. But most topics don't need this

What happens when you go too long

  • Completion rate drops - Users swipe through first 5-6 slides, then bail. They don't see your CTA or recap
  • Engagement per slide decreases - LinkedIn analytics show drop-off by slide. Slide 15 gets 30-40% of the views that slide 1 gets
  • Carousel feels like work - If swiping through your carousel feels like a chore, users won't save or share it. Keep it tight

When to go shorter (5-7 slides)

Sometimes less is more. If your topic is simple or you want quick consumption, 5-7 slides works. Example: "3 mistakes killing your LinkedIn profile" only needs 5 slides (hook, intro, 3 mistakes, CTA). Don't artificially pad to hit 10 slides.

Slide count test: After scripting your carousel, read through it. If any slide feels like filler or doesn't add value, cut it. Every slide must earn its place. Tight, valuable carousels perform better than long, padded ones.

Engagement Strategy (Reply to Every Comment in First 2 Hours)

▼

Why the first 2 hours are critical

LinkedIn's algorithm watches initial engagement closely. If your post gets likes and comments in the first 2 hours, LinkedIn interprets it as high-quality and pushes it to more people. If engagement is slow, the post dies in the feed.

Post-publish engagement checklist

  • Reply to every comment within 2 hours - Don't just say "Thanks!" Ask follow-up questions. Keep the conversation going. Each reply signals engagement to the algorithm
  • Engage with commenters' profiles - After replying, visit their profile and engage with their recent posts. This builds relationships and increases likelihood they'll engage with your future content
  • DM high-intent commenters - If someone leaves a thoughtful comment or asks a question related to your product, DM them: "Loved your comment on my carousel. Would you be open to a quick chat about X?"
  • Share the post in relevant communities - Post link in Slack communities, internal channels, or DM to close connections. Initial engagement boost helps algorithmic reach

What to reply to comments

Comment TypeHow to Reply
"Great post!" Thank them and ask a question: "Thanks Sarah! Which of the 5 tips do you think is most underused?"
Question or clarification Answer thoroughly. Then ask a follow-up: "Great question. Here's how I think about it... What's your experience with this?"
Adds their own insight Acknowledge and build on it: "Love this addition, James. Totally agree that X is underrated. Have you found Y works as well?"
Critique or disagreement Stay professional. Acknowledge their perspective: "Fair point. I've seen it work in X scenarios, but you're right it doesn't fit Y. Thanks for adding nuance."

Proactive engagement (before posting)

  • Seed engagement with your network - Before posting, DM 5-10 close connections: "Posting a carousel on X in 10 minutes. Would love your thoughts." When it goes live, they'll be the first to comment
  • Post when your audience is active - Check LinkedIn analytics to see when your followers are online. Post during peak hours for maximum initial engagement
Engagement flywheel: More comments - More replies from you - More total engagement - Algorithm boosts reach - More views - More comments. The first 2 hours set this flywheel in motion. Prioritise active engagement immediately after posting.

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Too much text per slide

You cram 5 sentences onto one slide. Text is tiny and unreadable on mobile. Users skip it immediately.
Limit to 10-20 words per slide. Use large fonts (60-plus pt for headlines). If content doesn't fit on one slide, split it into two slides. Prioritise readability over saying everything.

2. No hook on first slide

Slide 1 says "Introduction" or has a generic title. Users don't swipe because nothing grabbed their attention.
Slide 1 must hook. Use a bold statement, question, or stat. Examples: "Your emails are landing in spam. Here's why." or "5 mistakes killing your outreach." Make them curious enough to swipe.

3. Inconsistent design

Each slide uses different fonts, colours, or layouts. Looks unprofessional and hurts brand recognition.
Use a template. Keep fonts, colours, and layout consistent across all slides. Your carousels should be instantly recognisable as yours. Build a branded template in Canva and reuse it.

4. No CTA on last slide

Carousel ends with no call-to-action. Users swipe through, get value, then scroll away. You miss the conversion opportunity.
Always include a CTA on the last slide. Tell readers what to do next: follow you, comment, DM, share. Make it specific. "Drop a comment with your biggest challenge and I'll respond."

5. Posting without engaging in comments

You post the carousel then disappear. Comments sit unanswered. Algorithm sees low engagement and kills reach.
Block 1-2 hours after posting for active engagement. Reply to every comment. Ask follow-up questions. Keep the conversation going. First 2 hours are critical for algorithmic reach.

6. Making it too promotional

Every slide mentions your product. It reads like a sales pitch disguised as education. Users don't save or share it.
Deliver pure value for 11 slides. Only mention your product on slide 12 if it's genuinely relevant. Or keep it in the caption CTA. Trust that educational content builds more pipeline than direct pitches.

7. Poor readability on mobile

You design on desktop and never check mobile. Text is too small, colours don't contrast well, slides are hard to read on a phone.
Always preview on mobile before posting. 80-plus percent of views happen on mobile. If text is hard to read on your phone screen, increase font size or reduce word count.
