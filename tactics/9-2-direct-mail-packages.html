Tactic 9.2 - Creative / Guerrilla

Creative Direct Mail Packages

Send physical packages with relevant gifts and personalised notes to high-value prospects. Bypasses digital filters entirely and creates memorable brand moments that drive conversations with C-suite and VP-level buyers.

Effort: Medium Cost: Medium-High ICP: C-suite, VP-level
01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Physical packages bypass every digital filter. Spam filters, LinkedIn connection limits, email blocklists - none of them matter when a branded package lands on someone's desk. The tactile experience creates memory formation that digital outreach never achieves.

The psychology behind direct mail packages

  • Reciprocity trigger - Receiving an unexpected gift creates psychological debt. Most people feel obligated to acknowledge or reciprocate in some way
  • Pattern interrupt at physical level - Opening a package is a multi-sensory experience (touch, sight, sometimes smell). This creates stronger memory encoding than clicking an email
  • Social proof and status - Receiving packages signals importance. Executives often display gifts in their office or mention them to colleagues, extending your reach
  • Endowment effect - Once someone owns something (even a small gift), they value it more highly and feel more connected to the giver

Why this works specifically for enterprise

Enterprise buyers are insulated from cold outreach by layers of gatekeepers, assistants, and spam filters. A physical package sidesteps all of this. Mail rooms deliver packages directly to recipients. Assistants hand over packages. The physical nature demands attention in a way digital never can.

The format advantage: Unlike emails that can be deleted in seconds, packages require physical interaction. Even if the recipient doesn't engage immediately, the package sits on their desk as a visual reminder of your outreach.

Best Practices

▼

Gift Selection (Relevant to ICP, Not Generic Swag)

▼

Why generic gifts fail

Branded pens, t-shirts, and cheap gadgets scream "mass marketing." They get thrown away or stuffed in a drawer. The gift must feel chosen specifically for the recipient to trigger reciprocity.

Good vs bad gift choices

Bad (Generic)Good (Relevant)
Company-branded water bottle Premium coffee subscription (for sales leaders who work long hours)
Logo t-shirt Noise-cancelling headphones (for remote workers in noisy environments)
Cheap Bluetooth speaker Industry-specific book with personalised note about why this chapter is relevant
Random snack box Local specialty food from their city (shows you researched where they're based)

Gift selection framework

  • Role-based - What does this person's job involve? Sales leaders: coffee, whisky, sales books. Engineers: mechanical keyboards, coding books, desk toys. Marketers: design books, creative tools
  • Company-based - What's relevant to their business? SaaS company: book on scaling software teams. Retail: industry trend report. Manufacturing: innovation case studies
  • Pain point-based - What problem do they face that your solution addresses? If you solve remote work collaboration issues, send high-quality webcam or desk setup items
  • Personal interest-based - Check LinkedIn for hobbies or interests. Runner? Running gear. Coffee enthusiast? Specialty beans. Book lover? Recent bestseller in their field
Rule of thumb: If you could send the exact same gift to 100 different people without changing anything, it's too generic. The best gifts feel chosen for one specific person.

Personalisation at Scale (Research Each Recipient)

▼

Why personalisation is non-negotiable

Direct mail packages are expensive. If you're spending $50-150 per package, the personalisation must justify the cost. A generic package with a generic note gets the same results as a $5 handwritten card - which means you wasted your budget.

Research checklist per recipient

SourceWhat to FindHow to Use It
LinkedIn profile Recent posts, interests, job changes, shared connections Reference in note: "Saw your post on AI in sales. Totally agree on X." Or: "Congrats on the new role as VP Sales"
Company news Funding, product launches, expansions, awards "Congrats on Series B. Scaling post-raise is tough - thought this book on hypergrowth would be timely"
Tech stack Tools they use (from job postings, LinkedIn, BuiltWith) If they use Salesforce and you integrate with it: "Noticed you're on Salesforce. We work with a lot of SF teams to solve X"
Social profiles Hobbies, interests, personal brand If they post about running: include running-related gift with note about "fuelling your next marathon"

Note personalisation examples

Generic (bad): "Hi Sarah, I thought you might enjoy this coffee. Let me know if you'd like to chat about how we can help."

Personalised (good): "Hi Sarah, Saw Acme just hit 50 employees. Congrats! Scaling sales ops at this stage is brutal - I've seen teams struggle with X. Thought you might appreciate the caffeine boost while building out your new team. Happy to share what's worked for other VP Sales in similar growth stages if useful."

Time investment: Spend 10-15 min researching each recipient before choosing gift and writing note. This research makes the difference between a package that gets ignored and one that starts a conversation.

Timing and Coordination (Email on Delivery, Call 2 Days Later)

▼

Why timing matters

A package alone is interesting but not actionable. The follow-up email and call are what convert interest into meetings. Poor timing kills conversion - too early and they haven't received it yet (awkward), too late and they've forgotten about it.

Optimal follow-up sequence

TimelineActionMessage Template
Day 0 Package ships No message yet. Track delivery via platform
Day 3-5 (delivery day) Email same day it's delivered "Hi Sarah, a package should have arrived for you today. I sent you some coffee because I know scaling a sales team at your stage means a lot of late nights. No strings attached, but I'd love to share some insights on X if relevant. Let me know if you'd like to chat."
Day 5-7 (2 days post-delivery) Follow-up call or LinkedIn message Call: "Hi Sarah, it's James from Acme. I sent you a package earlier this week - just following up to see if you had a chance to check it out?" LinkedIn: "Following up on the package. Hope you enjoyed the coffee. Would love to chat about X when you have 15 min."
Day 10-12 (if no response) Final email touchpoint "Sarah, following up one last time on the package I sent. If now's not a good time, no worries - but wanted to make sure you received it. Happy to reconnect down the road when timing's better."

Delivery day coordination tips

  • Set delivery alerts - Most platforms (Sendoso, Reachdesk, Postal) notify you when package is delivered. Set these up and respond immediately
  • Mid-week delivery - Schedule delivery for Tuesday-Thursday. Avoid Monday (too hectic) and Friday (people leave early or work remote)
  • Confirm address beforehand - For high-value packages, send a quick email first: "I have something to send you. Are you still based at [office address]?" This prevents wasted sends
Pro tip: If you can, call the front desk or reception the day after delivery to confirm the package was delivered to the right person. This also primes the recipient - when their assistant mentions "a package arrived for you," they're curious before they even open it.

Budget Management (Match Gift Value to Account Potential)

▼

The ROI calculation

Direct mail is expensive. A $100 package only makes sense if the account is worth $10,000+ in potential revenue. You need clear criteria for who gets packages and how much to spend per send.

Spend tiers by account value

Account ValuePackage BudgetGift Examples
$5,000-10,000 ACV $25-40 Book + handwritten note, specialty coffee bag, desk plant
$10,000-50,000 ACV $50-75 Noise-cancelling headphones, whisky bottle, premium coffee subscription (1 month)
$50,000-100,000 ACV $100-150 High-end tech gadgets, multi-month subscriptions, curated gift boxes
$100,000+ ACV $200+ Premium experiences (wine tasting kit with virtual sommelier), luxury items, custom-built gifts

Budget control best practices

  • Set monthly caps - Limit direct mail spend to 10-15% of total marketing budget. Track per-send ROI monthly
  • Approval workflows - Require manager approval for packages over $100. Prevents wasteful spending on low-quality accounts
  • Test small first - Send 10-15 packages in month one. Measure response rate and meeting conversion before scaling up
  • Reuse successful gifts - Once you find a gift that works for your ICP, buy in bulk for discount and reuse across similar prospects
Rule of thumb: Package cost should be no more than 1% of expected account value. A $10,000 opportunity justifies a $100 package. A $100,000 enterprise deal can justify a $500-1,000 package if it's strategic.
05

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Irrelevant gift (generic swag no one wants)

You send branded pens, t-shirts, or cheap gadgets. Recipients see it as junk mail and throw it away immediately.
Choose gifts relevant to their role, company stage, or personal interests. Spend 10-15 min researching each recipient before selecting the gift. Avoid anything with your logo on it.

2. No personal note included

You send a gift with no context or explanation. Recipient has no idea who you are or why you sent it. Package feels like random spam.
Always include a handwritten or robotic-pen note explaining why you chose this gift for them specifically. Reference something personal (company news, role, shared connection). Make it about them, not about you.

3. No follow-up plan (send package and wait passively)

You send the package and hope they reach out. Most recipients appreciate the gift but never take action unless prompted.
Email the day of delivery referencing the package. Call or LinkedIn message 2 days later. Use the package as a conversation starter, not a standalone tactic. Follow-up is what converts.

4. Sending to office when they work remotely

You send to company HQ but the recipient works from home full-time or in a different office. Package sits in a mailroom or gets returned.
Check LinkedIn for remote work mentions. Send a quick email before shipping: "I have something to send you. Still based at [address]?" For high-value targets, consider home address (available via data enrichment) or ask directly.

5. Package too expensive for account value

You send $200 gifts to accounts worth $5,000 annually. The ROI doesn't justify the spend and you burn through budget fast.
Match gift value to account potential. Use spending tiers: $25-40 for small accounts, $50-75 for mid-market, $100-150 for enterprise, $200+ for strategic mega-deals. Require approval for high-cost packages.

6. No tracking (don't know if package was delivered or opened)

You send packages with no delivery confirmation. You follow up before it arrives (awkward) or don't know if it got lost in the mail.
Use platforms with delivery tracking (Sendoso, Reachdesk, Postal all offer this). Set up notifications for delivery. Only follow up after confirmed delivery. For critical packages, call reception to confirm receipt.
