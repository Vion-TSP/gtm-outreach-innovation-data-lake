Tactic 1.6 — Cold Outbound

Multi-Channel Orchestrated Sequences

Pair cold email with LinkedIn nurturing, phone, video, and direct mail to create persistent, non-spammy outreach that converts.

Effort: Medium Cost: Low–Medium ICP: All ICPs
01

Why It Works

▼
6–8
Touchpoints needed to convert
The core insight: Spreading 6–8 touchpoints across multiple channels feels helpful rather than spammy. A prospect who gets one email, one LinkedIn message, and one call feels engaged — not harassed. The same 3 touchpoints via a single channel feels like badgering.

The psychology behind multi-channel

  • Mere exposure effect — Seeing your name across channels builds familiarity and trust before a conversation happens
  • Channel preference — Different buyers prefer different channels. Multi-channel ensures you reach them where they actually pay attention
  • Context switching — A LinkedIn message after an email creates a "surround sound" effect that increases perceived importance
  • Reduced spam fatigue — 3 emails in a week = annoying. 1 email + 1 LinkedIn + 1 call in a week = thorough

The Channels

▼

Email

Primary channel - scalable and trackable

When to use: Primary channel for first touch, follow-ups, and value delivery. The backbone of every sequence.

  • Scales easily (hundreds/day with automation)
  • Highly trackable (opens, clicks, replies)
  • Low cost per touch
  • Crowded — avg exec gets 120+ emails/day
  • Deliverability challenges (spam filters, warmup)
  • Easy to ignore or auto-archive

LinkedIn

High credibility - professional context builds trust

When to use: Pre-warming before email, parallel nurturing, sharing content, voice notes for high-value targets.

  • Professional context (buyers expect outreach)
  • Profile acts as a landing page (social proof)
  • Voice notes drive 30% higher engagement
  • Daily limits (100 connections/week safely)
  • InMail credits are finite
  • Automation risks account restrictions

Phone

Highest conversion per live conversation

When to use: Mid-sequence to break through digital noise, after engagement signals, for high-value accounts.

  • Highest conversion per interaction
  • Real-time objection handling
  • Demonstrates commitment and seriousness
  • Low connect rates (most go to voicemail)
  • Time-intensive (can't scale easily)
  • Requires skilled reps (talk tracks matter)

Video (Personalised)

Extreme differentiation - few competitors do this

When to use: High-value accounts, executive outreach, to stand out after email/LinkedIn touches have been ignored.

  • Extreme differentiation (few competitors do it)
  • Builds personal connection fast
  • AI tools now enable scaling
  • Time-intensive if truly personalised
  • Requires decent production quality
  • Low view rate if thumbnail/subject line weak

Direct Mail

Cuts through digital noise - physical is memorable

When to use: Executive ABM, stalled deals, high-value re-engagement. Physical items bypass all digital filters.

  • Near-zero competition in mailbox
  • Tangible = memorable (sits on desk)
  • Works on unreachable execs
  • Highest cost per touch
  • Slower (shipping time)
  • Requires accurate mailing addresses

SMS / WhatsApp

Highest immediacy - read almost instantly

When to use: Post-meeting reminders, time-sensitive offers, warm leads only. Never for cold first-touch (compliance risk).

  • Highest open/read rates of any channel
  • Feels personal and immediate
  • Great for confirmations and follow-ups
  • Compliance-heavy (GDPR, TCPA, opt-in required)
  • Feels invasive if unsolicited
  • Limited message length and formatting

Channel Best Practices

▼

Email Best Practices

▼

Subject Lines

  • Under 7 words — short subjects perform significantly better
  • Lowercase, no punctuation (feels like a colleague, not marketing)
  • Include their company name or a specific reference
  • Avoid spam triggers: "free", "guarantee", "act now", ALL CAPS

Examples: Acme + your company | quick question about their initiative | saw your post on the topic

Body Copy

  • Under 125 words — shorter emails perform significantly better
  • Plain text (no HTML, images, or logos)
  • One CTA only — "Would a 15-min call this week make sense?"
  • First line must earn the second line — never start with "My name is..."

Personalisation Hierarchy

LevelElementImpactEffort
1 (Basic)First name + company nameLow impactAutomated
2 (Good)Role-specific pain pointModerate impactSegmented
3 (Great)Company-specific insight (news, tech, hiring)High impactClay/research
4 (Elite)Personal reference (post, podcast, talk)Highest impactManual/AI

Send Times

  • Best days: Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday
  • Best windows: 8:00–10:00 AM or 1:00–3:00 PM (recipient's timezone)
  • Monday AM = inbox overload. Friday PM = weekend mode
  • Test early morning (6–7 AM) for executives who check before meetings

LinkedIn Best Practices

▼

Connection Requests

  • Personalised notes significantly increase acceptance than blank requests
  • Keep notes under 40 words (the visible preview is short)
  • Reference mutual connections, shared groups, or their content
  • Never pitch in the connection request

Example: "Hi Sarah — enjoyed your post on the topic. We're both in the the same industry space and I'd love to connect."

InMail

  • Under 100 words — shorter InMails get 22% higher response
  • Open with something about them, not about you
  • Ask a question (questions get 50% more replies than statements)
  • Don't duplicate your email — use a different angle

Voice Notes

  • 30% higher engagement than text messages
  • 30–60 seconds maximum
  • Mention their name and something specific (proves it's not mass-produced)
  • Casual, conversational tone — not a script read

▼

Voicemail Formula (20 sec max)

  • Structure: Name + company + one specific reason + callback number
  • "Hi their name, your name from Acme. Calling because [one specific reason tied to them]. My number is 07700 900123. I'll also send a quick email."
  • Always follow up with email — voicemail alone rarely converts
  • Vary voicemail messages across attempts (don't repeat the same script)

Best Times to Call

  • Highest connect rate: 8:00–9:00 AM and 4:00–5:00 PM
  • Best days: Wednesday and Thursday
  • Avoid lunch (12–1 PM) and Monday/Friday extremes
  • Call within 5 minutes of any engagement signal (email open, website visit) for 8x higher connect rate

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Same message across channels

Prospects see you copy-pasting and immediately disengage. It signals laziness and mass outreach.
Tailor the message and angle per channel. Email = formal insight. LinkedIn = conversational. Phone = direct ask.

2. Too many touches too fast

5 touches in 3 days across channels feels like stalking, not selling. Triggers blocks and spam reports.
Space touches 1–3 days apart. Never hit the same channel twice in one day. Let each touch breathe.

3. No sequence logic or triggers

Time-based sequences ignore engagement. You email someone who already replied, or skip someone who clicked 4x.
Build conditional branches: if opened 3x → call. If replied → exit sequence. If no engagement by day 7 → new angle.

4. Pitching in the LinkedIn connect

Kills acceptance rates instantly. People connect to network, not to get sold to in the request.
Connection note = context only (mutual interest, their content, shared group). Pitch comes after they accept, if at all.

5. Skipping the breakup email

Missing the highest-converting email in the sequence. Breakup emails get 2–3x the reply rate of other follow-ups.
Always end with a breakup. "Closing your file" or "Not going to keep bugging you" works because it removes pressure.

6. Generic subject lines

"Quick question" and "Touching base" are invisible in a crowded inbox. 0% differentiation.
Use their company name, a trigger event, or a curiosity hook. Under 7 words. Lowercase.

7. Not tracking per-channel metrics

Without channel-specific data, you can't optimise. You might be investing in channels that aren't converting.
Track open/reply/connect rates per channel per sequence. Review weekly. Kill what doesn't work, double down on what does.

8. Sending long emails

Emails over 125 words see a steep drop in reply rates. Nobody reads a 300-word cold email from a stranger.
125 words max. One pain point, one proof point, one CTA. If you can't say it in 5 sentences, you haven't refined the message enough.

9. Ignoring timezone and send times

Sending at 2 AM in their timezone means your email is buried by morning. Timing is 20% of deliverability.
Always send in the prospect's timezone. Tue–Thu, 8–10 AM or 1–3 PM. Test early AM (6–7 AM) for execs.

10. No A/B testing

Running the same sequence for months without testing means you're leaving 30–50% improvement on the table.
Test one variable at a time: subject line, CTA, send time, channel order. Minimum 100 sends per variant before drawing conclusions.
