Tactic 4.1 - Content-Led Outreach

Podcast Guesting Strategy

Get booked as a guest on podcasts your ICP listens to. Builds authority, generates inbound leads, and creates evergreen content assets. AI search tools make it easy to find podcasts that frequently book guests in your domain.

Co
Content (Primary)

01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Decision-makers consume podcasts during commutes, workouts, and downtime. Unlike cold emails or LinkedIn posts they scroll past, they actively listen to full podcast episodes. A 45-minute appearance gives you more attention than months of standard outreach. Plus, the host's endorsement transfers trust instantly.

The psychology of podcast authority

  • Borrowed credibility - When a respected host interviews you, their audience assumes you're worth listening to. You bypass cold skepticism
  • Passive lead generation - Listeners hear you share insights for 30-60 minutes. Those who resonate seek you out. No hard sell required
  • Long-tail asset - Episodes stay online forever. People discover them months or years later via search. One great episode generates leads for years
  • Multi-touchpoint effect - Prospects might hear your episode, then see your LinkedIn post, then get your cold email. Multi-channel presence compounds credibility

Why podcast guesting beats other content strategies

Creating your own podcast requires consistent production effort. Writing blog posts reaches only people who actively search for content. Podcast guesting gives you distribution to an existing engaged audience without the overhead of building your own. The host does the promotion. You just show up and share value.

The compounding effect: Each podcast appearance expands your network. Hosts introduce you to their connections. Other podcasters discover your episodes and invite you on their shows. One appearance leads to three more invitations.

04

Best Practices

▼

Finding the Right Podcasts (Audience Alignment Over Size)

▼

Audience fit matters more than download numbers

A podcast with 500 highly targeted listeners (all in your ICP) generates more leads than a show with 50,000 general business listeners. Prioritise shows where your ideal customers are concentrated, not vanity metrics.

How to evaluate podcast fit

  • Listen to 2-3 recent episodes - Does the host interview people in your space? Do guests share tactical insights or just high-level fluff? Is the tone conversational or overly produced?
  • Check guest backgrounds - Who else appears on the show? If competitors or complementary companies have been guests, that's a green flag for ICP alignment
  • Review audience signals - Read Apple Podcasts reviews, LinkedIn comments on episode announcements, or YouTube comments. What do listeners care about? Do they match your ICP?
  • Analyse episode topics - Does the show cover pain points your product solves? If yes, listeners are pre-qualified prospects

Target podcast tiers

TierCharacteristicsStrategy
Tier 1: Niche expert shows Small audience but highly targeted. Host is a practitioner, not a professional podcaster. Informal format Easiest to get booked. Start here to build reps and collect testimonials from hosts
Tier 2: Established industry shows Medium audience. Recognised in your industry. Regular publishing cadence. Mix of tactical and strategic content Target once you have 3-5 smaller podcast appearances. Reference those in your pitch
Tier 3: High-profile shows Large audience. Professional production. Well-known host. Competitive guest booking Requires warm intro or significant authority in your space. Use appearances on Tier 1 and 2 shows to build credibility for pitching Tier 3
Pro tip: Search for podcasts by frequent guest, not just by topic. If someone in your space appears on multiple podcasts, research all of those shows. They clearly book guests in your domain and have audiences interested in your expertise.

Crafting Your Pitch (What Value You Bring, Not What You Sell)

▼

What hosts care about

Podcast hosts want great content for their audience. They don't care about your product, your company, or your personal goals. Lead with the value you provide to their listeners. Make it easy for them to say yes.

Pitch structure that works

  • Line 1: Personalisation - Reference a specific recent episode you listened to. Mention what you found valuable. Proves you're not mass emailing
  • Line 2: Your credibility - One sentence stating your expertise and why you're qualified to speak on this topic. Include a notable credential or result
  • Line 3-4: Value to their audience - Suggest 2-3 specific topics you could cover that align with recent themes on their show. Frame as listener benefits, not your talking points
  • Line 5: Social proof - Mention 1-2 other podcasts you've appeared on (if applicable). Link to a previous episode or written content demonstrating your communication style
  • Line 6: Easy next step - Offer to send more info or jump on a quick call. Include your calendar link or ask when works for them

Pitch examples

Good pitch example Subject: Guest idea for The GTM Show Hi Sarah, Just listened to your episode with James on scaling outbound teams. Loved the discussion on balancing automation with personalisation. I lead GTM operations at Acme and spend most of my time helping Series A/B companies fix broken sales processes. Previously scaled outbound at two companies from zero to consistent pipeline. A few topics I think would resonate with your audience: - Common mistakes that kill cold email deliverability (and how to fix them) - Building repeatable outbound motions without burning out your SDR team - How to prioritise which parts of GTM to automate first Happy to send over talking points or links to previous podcast appearances. Here's my calendar if you'd like to chat: [link] Best, James
Common mistake: Pitching with "I'd love to come on your show to talk about..." instead of "Your audience would benefit from learning about..." The first is about you. The second is about them. Always frame value for the host's audience.

Preparation (Stories Over Stats, Specific Over Generic)

▼

Why preparation matters

Great podcast guests make hosts look good. Boring guests with generic answers make hosts work harder to extract value. The more you prepare, the easier you make the host's job, and the better the episode turns out.

Preparation checklist

  • Listen to 3-5 recent episodes - Understand the host's style (conversational vs formal, tactical vs strategic, short vs long answers). Match their energy and format
  • Prepare 5-7 stories - Real examples from your experience. Specific situations with stakes, actions, and outcomes. Stories engage listeners far more than abstract concepts
  • Create a frameworks document - Visual or mental frameworks you use in your work. Hosts love when guests share repeatable models listeners can apply. Example: a 3-step process for qualifying outbound leads
  • Anticipate common questions - What do people always ask you about your expertise? Prepare concise answers in advance so you're not rambling on the spot
  • Research the host's background - Look at their LinkedIn, company, or previous work. Find connection points you can reference during the conversation to build rapport

During the recording

Do ThisNot This
Share specific stories with names, numbers, and context Give generic advice like "you should focus on quality over quantity"
Admit what you don't know or what hasn't worked for you Present yourself as having all the answers. Listeners smell BS
Build on the host's questions and energy Ignore their question to talk about what you want to talk about
Provide actionable takeaways listeners can use immediately Speak in high-level platitudes without tactical details
Pro tip: Prepare one surprising contrarian take or unpopular opinion in your space. Hosts love when guests challenge conventional wisdom. It creates memorable moments and clips for promotion. Example: "Most companies waste money on intent data. Here's why..."

Being a Great Guest (Energy, Stories, Actionable Insights)

▼

What separates great guests from forgettable ones

Great guests leave the host thinking "I need to have them back" and listeners thinking "I need to learn more from this person." It's not about being the smartest person in the room. It's about being engaging, helpful, and memorable.

Energy and presence

  • Speak like you're talking to a friend - Conversational tone beats formal presentation. Pretend you're explaining this to a colleague over coffee
  • Vary your pace and tone - Monotone voices lose listeners. Speed up during exciting parts, slow down for important points, pause for emphasis
  • Show enthusiasm - If you sound bored talking about your expertise, why would anyone else care? Genuine excitement is contagious
  • React to the host - Laugh at their jokes. Build on their insights. Treat it like a two-way conversation, not a one-way lecture

Content quality

  • Stories over stats - "We increased reply rates" is boring. "Here's what happened when we tried X and it completely backfired..." is engaging. Stories stick. Stats don't
  • Be specific - Instead of "segment your outbound list," say "we split our list into three tiers based on company revenue, tech stack, and recent funding. Here's why that mattered..."
  • Give away your best stuff - Don't hold back insights to protect your competitive advantage. The more value you provide, the more people want to work with you
  • Make it actionable - End answers with "here's what you can do starting today..." Listeners want practical next steps, not just concepts
Pro tip: After the recording, send the host a thank-you message highlighting your favourite part of the conversation. Most guests disappear after recording. Standing out with gratitude increases your chances of being invited back or recommended to other hosts.

Repurposing Content (Audiograms, Quotes, Blog Posts, LinkedIn)

▼

Why repurposing matters

Only a small fraction of the podcast's audience listens to every episode. Repurposing surfaces your insights to people who missed the episode, don't have time to listen, or prefer different content formats. One 45-minute episode can fuel weeks of content.

Repurposing formats

  • Short video clips (60-90 sec) - Pull the best moments from the episode. Add captions. Share on LinkedIn, Twitter, and your website. These clips often get more views than the full episode
  • Audiograms - Animated waveform video with a short audio snippet. Great for Instagram Stories and LinkedIn. Tools like Headliner or Descript make these easy
  • Quote graphics - Pull memorable lines from the transcript. Design simple quote cards with your photo and the podcast logo. Share on social
  • Blog post recap - Turn the transcript into a written article. "10 Insights from My Appearance on The GTM Show." Embed the episode audio. Drives SEO and gives non-podcast listeners access
  • LinkedIn carousel - Extract 5-7 key takeaways. Create a carousel post walking through each point. Link to full episode in comments
  • Email newsletter feature - Share the episode with your email list. Include 2-3 key points discussed and why they matter. Easy content for your newsletter

Building Relationships with Hosts for Repeat Appearances

▼

Why relationships matter

Podcast hosts are connectors. They know other hosts, potential customers, investors, and industry leaders. A strong relationship with one host can open doors to dozens of other opportunities. Treat every podcast appearance as the start of a long-term relationship, not a one-off transaction.

How to stay on hosts' radar

  • Promote their show genuinely - Share other episodes you enjoyed, not just yours. Tag them when you see relevant content. Show you care about their success, not just your appearance
  • Introduce them to potential guests - When you meet someone who'd be great for their show, make the intro. Hosts remember people who help them find great content
  • Engage with their content - Comment on their LinkedIn posts, share their articles, respond to their emails. Stay visible without being pushy
  • Offer to return for updates - Six months after your first appearance, reach out with new insights or developments in your space. "Hey Sarah, remember we talked about X? Here's what's changed since then. Would love to do a follow-up episode."

Building a guest network

Connect with other guests who've appeared on the same shows. These are people in adjacent or complementary spaces to yours. Comment on their episodes, DM them with thoughts, explore collaboration opportunities. Guest networks compound your podcast opportunities.

Pro tip: After a great episode, ask the host: "Who else should I talk to in this space?" Hosts know dozens of other podcasters and are usually happy to make intros if you were a good guest. One warm intro beats 20 cold pitches.

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Pitching podcasts that don't match your ICP

You appear on shows with large audiences, but none of the listeners are in your target market. Generates awareness but zero leads.
Research who actually listens to the show. Check guest backgrounds and episode topics. Prioritise tight ICP alignment over download numbers.

2. Generic pitch emails that could be sent to anyone

Hosts get dozens of guest pitches per week. Generic emails ("I'd love to share my expertise on your show") get ignored immediately.
Reference specific episodes. Suggest topics aligned with recent themes. Show you've done your homework. Make it personal.

3. Talking about your product instead of sharing insights

Listeners tune out the moment they feel sold to. Podcast audiences expect education, not pitches. Product talk kills credibility.
Focus 100% on providing value. Share frameworks, stories, and lessons. Mention your company only in bio. Let insights sell for you.

4. No preparation before recording

You show up without listening to previous episodes or preparing stories. Give rambling answers. Make the host work hard to extract value.
Listen to 3-5 recent episodes. Prepare 5-7 stories and frameworks. Anticipate common questions. Make the host's job easy.

5. Not promoting the episode after it goes live

The episode launches and you post once on LinkedIn then forget about it. Miss the opportunity to drive traffic and extract maximum value.
Create clips, quote graphics, blog recap. Share multiple times across channels over 2-3 weeks. Repurpose into newsletter content.

6. Treating it as a one-off transaction

You do the episode, say thanks, then disappear. Waste the relationship opportunity with the host and their network.
Stay in touch. Promote other episodes. Introduce potential guests. Offer to return for follow-up episode. Build long-term relationship.
