Tactic 2.7 - LinkedIn-Native

LinkedIn Collaborative Articles

Contribute to AI-generated LinkedIn articles on topics in your skill area. Earn Top Voice badges. Build authority and search ranking. Free, low-effort, high-visibility thought leadership.

Li
LinkedIn (Primary)
Effort: Low Cost: Free ICP: Technical Buyers, Founders
01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: LinkedIn Collaborative Articles are AI-generated content hubs that invite expert contributions. They rank highly in Google search. Contributing earns you a Top Voice badge, which displays prominently on your profile. Low effort, high visibility, and LinkedIn actively promotes these articles.

Why LinkedIn pushes Collaborative Articles

  • Content generation at scale - LinkedIn uses AI to generate thousands of articles on professional topics. They need human contributors to add credibility and depth. This creates a win-win: you get visibility, LinkedIn gets expert content
  • SEO advantage - Collaborative Articles rank on the first page of Google for many professional queries. Your contribution appears in search results, driving profile visits from people who've never heard of you
  • Top Voice badge system - LinkedIn rewards consistent contributors with Top Voice badges. These badges appear on your profile under your headline, signaling authority. Prospects see the badge before they read your bio
  • Algorithm boost for contributors - LinkedIn's algorithm gives slight visibility boosts to Top Voice badge holders. Your regular posts get marginally more reach

The psychology of social proof

Badges and credentials act as shortcuts for trust. When a prospect sees "Top Voice in Sales" under your name, they subconsciously categorise you as an authority before engaging with your content. It's the same psychology as verified checkmarks, certifications, or university logos on a resume. You're not just saying you're an expert - LinkedIn is endorsing you as one.

Low barrier to entry: Unlike publishing a book, speaking at a conference, or building a large following, earning a Top Voice badge requires only consistent thoughtful contributions over 3-4 weeks. It's the most accessible credibility signal in B2B marketing.

04

Best Practices

▼

Finding Articles (Where to Contribute)

▼

LinkedIn suggests articles based on your profile skills

LinkedIn's algorithm surfaces Collaborative Articles in your feed based on the skills listed on your profile. If you have "Sales Strategy" and "Cold Calling" as skills, you'll see articles on those topics. Make sure your profile skills are up to date and aligned with your ICP's pain points.

Search for articles manually

If LinkedIn isn't suggesting relevant articles, search manually. Use the search bar: "collaborative articles" + your topic. Example: "collaborative articles revenue operations" or "collaborative articles B2B sales." This surfaces active articles where you can contribute.

Focus on articles with moderate activity

Avoid articles with zero contributions (no one will see yours). Avoid articles with 500+ contributions (yours will get buried). Target articles with 20-100 contributions. This is the sweet spot for visibility and engagement.

Skill selection strategy: Choose 2-3 skill areas aligned with your ICP's pain points. Don't scatter contributions across 10 different topics. Depth beats breadth. Earn Top Voice in 2-3 areas that matter to your prospects.

Contribution Quality (What Makes a Good Contribution)

▼

Personal experience over generic advice

Generic advice is forgettable. "Handling objections requires active listening and empathy." True, but boring. Personal experience sticks. "At Acme, we faced constant pricing objections. We started leading with ROI calculators before even mentioning price. Objections dropped by 40%." Specific examples signal real expertise.

Specific examples over vague insights

Vague: "Sales onboarding should include training and mentorship." Specific: "Our onboarding includes 2 weeks of shadowing top reps, followed by 10 recorded cold calls with feedback from managers. New hires hit quota 30% faster." Specificity demonstrates depth of knowledge.

Contrarian takes get more reactions

If everyone is saying the same thing, your contribution blends in. Contrarian takes spark debate. Example: "Most people say cold calling is dead. I disagree. We generate 60% of our pipeline from cold calls because we target the right buyers at the right time." Controversy drives engagement.

Quality test: Before submitting, ask: "Could this contribution apply to any company in any industry?" If yes, it's too generic. Add specificity. Your contribution should be impossible to write without your actual experience.

Frequency (How Often to Contribute)

▼

3-5 contributions per week to earn the badge

LinkedIn's Top Voice algorithm rewards consistency over volume. Contributing once doesn't earn the badge. Contributing 50 times in one day doesn't either. Aim for 3-5 thoughtful contributions per week for 3-4 weeks. This signals consistent expertise.

Batch your contributions

Set aside 30-45 minutes once or twice per week. Find 3-5 relevant articles. Write all contributions in one session. This is more efficient than writing one at a time throughout the week.

Don't stop after earning the badge

The badge requires ongoing contributions to maintain. If you stop contributing for months, LinkedIn may remove the badge. Aim for 2-3 contributions per week after earning the badge to keep it active.

Scheduling tip: Block 30 min every Monday and Thursday for Collaborative Article contributions. Consistent calendar blocking makes this a habit, not a sporadic activity.

Leveraging the Badge (How to Use It)

▼

Badge shows on your profile automatically

Once earned, the Top Voice badge displays under your headline on your LinkedIn profile. Example: "Sarah Johnson - Revenue Operations Leader | Top Voice in Sales Strategy." Prospects see this before reading your summary or experience. It's instant credibility.

Mention it in outreach

Cold email subject line: "Quick question from a LinkedIn Top Voice in RevOps." LinkedIn message intro: "Hey James, I write about sales automation as a LinkedIn Top Voice. Noticed you're hiring SDRs..." The badge gives you permission to position yourself as an expert.

Reference it in content

Use it in your email signature, LinkedIn posts, or bio on other platforms. "LinkedIn Top Voice in Sales | Helping B2B teams scale outbound." It's a lightweight credibility marker that requires no explanation.

Positioning tip: Don't overuse it. Mention it once in outreach or profile, not repeatedly. Overuse feels insecure. One mention is enough to signal authority.

Which Topics to Target (Strategic Skill Selection)

▼

Pick 2-3 skill areas aligned with your ICP's pain points

Don't chase badges in random topics. Focus on skills that matter to your target buyers. If you sell to sales leaders, target "Sales Strategy," "Revenue Operations," or "Pipeline Management." If you sell to founders, target "Go-to-Market Strategy" or "Sales Playbooks."

Avoid overly broad or irrelevant topics

Earning Top Voice in "Leadership" or "Communication" is fine, but it's not differentiated. Everyone claims to be a good leader. Earn badges in niche, specific topics that align with your expertise and your ICP's challenges.

Check if LinkedIn offers articles in your target topics

Not all skills have active Collaborative Articles. Search "collaborative articles" + your topic before committing. If there are no articles or very few, pick a related topic with more activity.

Alignment test: Ask: "If a prospect saw this Top Voice badge, would it make them more likely to engage with me?" If the answer is yes, target that skill. If no, skip it.
05

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Generic contributions that could apply to anything

Generic advice is forgettable. "You should listen to your customers" or "Build strong relationships" adds no value. It blends into the noise.
Include specific examples from your experience. Name the company, tactic, or result. "At Acme, we did X and saw Y result." Specificity signals real expertise.

2. Writing too short (under 50 words)

LinkedIn's algorithm prioritises thoughtful, substantive contributions. A 2-sentence reply doesn't signal expertise. It looks like a quick comment, not a real insight.
Aim for 150-300 words. This is long enough to provide depth but short enough to hold attention. Structure: intro, insight, example, takeaway.

3. Not engaging with other contributors' responses

Contributing once and disappearing limits visibility. LinkedIn's algorithm tracks engagement within the article. If you don't react to others, fewer people see your contribution.
After contributing, read 3-5 other responses. Like or comment on thoughtful ones. This increases your visibility and signals active participation.

4. Targeting irrelevant skill areas

Earning Top Voice in "Public Speaking" doesn't help if you sell sales automation software. Your badge needs to align with your ICP's pain points.
Focus on 2-3 skills directly relevant to your ICP. If you sell to sales leaders, target "Sales Strategy" or "Revenue Operations." Align your badge with your buyer.

5. Contributing once and stopping

Earning a badge requires consistency. One contribution doesn't signal expertise. LinkedIn's algorithm needs to see repeated thoughtful contributions over weeks.
Commit to 3-5 contributions per week for 4 weeks. Block time on your calendar. Treat it like a recurring task, not a one-off activity.

6. Treating it as a checkbox instead of genuine thought leadership

If your contributions feel like you're gaming the system, LinkedIn's algorithm and human readers will notice. Low-quality contributions don't earn badges or respect.
Write contributions you'd genuinely be proud to share with a prospect. If you wouldn't send your contribution to a client, don't post it. Quality over quantity.
