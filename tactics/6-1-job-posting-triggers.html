Tactic 6.1 - Signal-Based / Intent

Job Posting Signal Triggers

Monitor target accounts for relevant job postings that signal pain points, growth initiatives, or tech stack gaps. Reach out with contextual messaging tied directly to hiring needs they have publicly advertised.

Effort: Low-Medium Cost: Low ICP: Founders, Sales Leaders, Operations Teams
01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Job postings reveal strategic priorities and current pain points with perfect timing. When a company posts for a Revenue Operations Manager, they are publicly admitting they have operational gaps. Your outreach arrives exactly when they are thinking about solving that problem.

The psychology behind job posting triggers

  • Public admission of need - Job postings are companies explicitly stating they lack capability in an area. This removes the objection that they do not need your category
  • Budget already allocated - If they are hiring for a role, budget has been approved. Decision makers are in spending mode for that function, making them more receptive to tools that support it
  • Timing advantage - Catching them during active hiring means your solution can potentially reduce headcount needs or accelerate new hire productivity. Both are compelling value propositions
  • Contextual relevance - Referencing their specific job posting in outreach demonstrates you understand their exact situation. It is personalisation based on their own words

Why job postings specifically

Unlike vague signals like website visits, job postings are concrete public declarations of strategic direction. The job description itself provides incredible detail about tech stack, processes, and pain points. It is free market research handed to you.

The compounding effect: Companies hiring in one area often have related needs. A sales hire suggests potential need for sales tools. An engineering hire might signal product expansion that requires additional infrastructure. One signal can reveal multiple opportunities.

04

Best Practices

▼

Role Selection - Choosing High-Signal Job Titles

▼

Identifying relevant roles for your category

Not all job postings signal buying intent for your solution. Focus on roles where the job description naturally includes responsibilities that your product supports or pain points it solves.

Role categories to monitor

  • Roles your product replaces or reduces - If your automation tool reduces need for manual work, monitor for operations coordinator or data entry roles. Their hiring need is your sales opportunity
  • Roles your product empowers - If you sell sales enablement software, track sales development rep, account executive, and sales operations postings. These roles will use your product daily
  • Roles that influence buying decisions - For B2B software, track revenue operations, sales ops, marketing ops, and chief of staff roles. These people evaluate and recommend tools
  • Executive roles signalling growth - New VP or C-level hires often bring budget and mandate to change tech stack. Monitor for CRO, CMO, VP Sales, VP Ops appointments

Signal quality by role type

Role TypeSignal QualityWhy
VP or C-level Very High Executives bring budget and authority to change stack. Often hired specifically to transform function
Operations roles High Ops people are tool evaluators by nature. Hiring ops signals process gaps your solution may address
IC contributor roles Medium Individual contributors signal team growth but less direct buying power. Good secondary signal
Support or admin roles Low Unless your product specifically targets these functions, they are weak signal for software buying intent
Relevance test: Before adding a job title to monitoring list, ask: "If I saw this role posted, could I craft a credible message about how our product helps that person succeed?" If the answer is not immediately obvious, it is probably not a strong signal.

Job Description Analysis - Mining for Insights

▼

What to extract from job descriptions

Job descriptions are goldmines of information about company priorities, tech stack, processes, and pain points. Spend time analysing them rather than just noting the title.

Key elements to identify

  • Pain points and challenges - Look for phrases like "streamline processes," "improve efficiency," or "reduce manual work." These reveal problems they are trying to solve
  • Tech stack mentions - JDs often list required experience with specific tools. This tells you their current stack and potential integration needs
  • Responsibilities that map to your product - If JD lists tasks your product automates or supports, you have perfect talking points for outreach
  • Growth indicators - Mentions of scaling, expansion, or building new teams signal company trajectory and receptiveness to growth-enabling tools

Turning JD language into outreach hooks

Copy their words: Use exact phrases from the job description in your outreach. If they say "streamline operations," use "streamline operations" in your message. This creates subconscious familiarity and relevance.

Outreach Messaging - Tying to Job Posting

▼

Message structure for job posting triggers

Your outreach must explicitly reference the job posting to establish context and relevance. Generic messages waste the timing advantage this signal provides.

Effective message framework

  • Subject line references the posting - "Re: Your Sales Ops Manager posting" or "Saw you are hiring for Revenue Operations"
  • Opening acknowledges the hire - "Noticed Acme is bringing on a Sales Operations Manager. Congrats on the growth"
  • Connect posting to product value - "Many companies hiring for this role find that tool X helps new ops hires ramp 3x faster by..."
  • Specific JD reference - Quote or paraphrase responsibility from job description and tie to your solution
  • Low-friction CTA - Offer resource, template, or quick call rather than full demo. Meet them where they are in the journey

Positioning angles based on hiring context

ScenarioAngleValue Prop
First hire in function Help new hire succeed from day one Provide frameworks, templates, and automation so they can deliver quick wins and prove value fast
Backfill for departed employee Reduce transition disruption Your tool provides continuity and processes so new hire does not start from scratch. Minimise knowledge loss

Timing variation: If you reach out within 1-2 weeks of posting, focus on "considering alternatives to headcount." If you reach out 4-6 weeks later or after hire is made, shift to "helping new hire ramp quickly." Adjust message to their current stage.

Follow-Up Strategy - Staying Relevant Through Hiring Cycle

▼

Multi-touch approach across hiring timeline

One message is rarely enough. Most hiring cycles take 4-8 weeks. Stay relevant with multiple touches tied to likely stages of their process.

Follow-up cadence and messaging

  • Touch 1 - Week of posting - Initial outreach referencing JD and offering resource
  • Touch 2 - Two weeks later - Share relevant case study or customer story from similar company hiring for same role
  • Touch 3 - Four weeks later - Assume they may have made hire. Offer to help new hire ramp with templates or onboarding resources
  • Touch 4 - Six to eight weeks later - If hire is confirmed via LinkedIn, reach out directly to new person in role offering to support their first 90 days

Targeting both hiring manager and new hire

TargetTimingMessage Focus
Hiring manager or exec During active hiring process Tool can reduce headcount needs, accelerate new hire impact, or provide frameworks for success
New hire in role 30-60 days after start date Help them deliver quick wins, ramp faster, and prove value in new role. Often have mandate to evaluate tools
LinkedIn stalking pays off: Monitor target company's LinkedIn page for announcement of new hire. When they post "Please welcome Sarah as our new Head of Sales Ops," that is your signal to reach out to both the hiring exec and Sarah directly with relevant positioning for each.
05

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Generic messaging that ignores the job posting

Sending standard pitch without referencing the specific role wastes the contextual trigger. Recipient does not see connection between your outreach and their current need.
Explicitly mention the job title and quote or reference specific responsibilities from the JD. Make it obvious you are reaching out because of this specific posting, not blind outreach.

2. Tracking too many irrelevant job titles

Monitoring every role creates noise and false signals. You waste time on postings that do not actually indicate need for your solution.
Limit to 3-5 high-signal job titles where connection to your product is obvious. Quality over quantity. Better to catch 100% of VP Sales Ops postings than 20% of all sales-related roles.

3. Reaching out too late after posting goes live

Job posting is freshest signal in first 1-2 weeks. Reaching out 6 weeks later means they have likely already made hiring decision or committed to headcount plan.
Set up alerts to notify you within 24 hours of posting. Reach out within first week while they are still considering all options including tools versus headcount.

4. Only reaching out to HR instead of hiring manager

HR posts the role but is rarely the budget owner or decision maker for software purchases. You are pitching to wrong person.
Use LinkedIn to identify likely hiring manager based on function. For sales roles, reach out to VP Sales or CRO. For ops roles, find COO or Head of Operations. Target decision makers.

5. No follow-up after initial outreach

Hiring cycles take weeks. One message gets lost in their inbox during busy hiring process. You lose the opportunity.
Plan 3-4 touch sequence over 6-8 weeks tied to likely hiring stages. Stay relevant without being annoying. Provide value at each touch.

6. Forgetting to reach out to new hire after they start

New hires are highly motivated to prove value quickly and often have mandate to evaluate tools. Missing this window wastes the signal.
Monitor LinkedIn for hire announcements. 30-60 days after start date, reach out offering templates, best practices, or quick win resources. Help them succeed in new role.
