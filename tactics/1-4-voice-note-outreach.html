Tactic 1.4 - Reactive Outbound

Voice Note Cold Outreach

Send 30-60 second voice notes via LinkedIn or email. The human voice builds trust and connection faster than text, cutting through the noise of written messages.

01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Voice carries emotion, energy, and authenticity that text cannot replicate. A 45-second voice note feels personal and human in a way that even the best-written cold email never will. It signals effort and breaks the pattern of templated outreach.

Why voice outperforms text

  • Pattern interruption - Decision-makers receive hundreds of text-based LinkedIn DMs and emails per week. They receive fewer than 5 voice messages per month. Voice stands out immediately
  • Emotional bandwidth - Tone of voice conveys warmth, confidence, and sincerity. You can sound excited about their company in a way that text cannot capture
  • Efficiency signal - A 45-second voice note takes less time to listen to than reading a 200-word email. You are respecting their time by being concise
  • Authenticity proof - Voice is harder to fake or template at scale. Receiving a voice note signals you cared enough to record something specifically for them

When voice notes work best

Voice outreach is most effective for high-value targets where personalisation matters. It works especially well after a warm trigger event like a recent LinkedIn post, funding announcement, or job change. Voice also works as a follow-up after ignored emails - it re-opens the conversation with a fresh medium.

The friction factor: Voice notes have slightly higher friction than text because the recipient must play audio. This means they work best when you have earned attention through research and relevance. Use voice for tier-1 prospects where you have a specific hook, not for spray-and-pray cold outreach.

Recording Tips (Sound Professional, Not Scripted)

▼

Tone and delivery

  • Smile while talking - It genuinely changes your tone. You sound warmer and more approachable. Record standing up for better energy
  • Vary your pace - Speak slightly faster than normal conversation (1.1-1.2x speed). Slow speakers lose attention. Fast speakers sound energetic
  • Use their name early and often - Say their name in the first 5 seconds and again in the CTA. "Hey Sarah" + "Sarah, would love to chat" = personalised
  • Avoid filler words - "Um", "uh", "like" undermine credibility. Practice your script once before recording. Do not read from a script during the recording

Audio quality

  • Quiet environment - No background noise (traffic, office chatter, keyboard typing). Use a closet or car if your workspace is noisy
  • Phone vs microphone - Built-in phone audio is usually fine for voice notes under 60 seconds. If recording via Loom on a laptop, use headphones with a mic for better quality
  • Test first - Record a 10-second test, play it back. Does it sound clear? Is the volume consistent? Fix before recording 50 voice notes
Common mistake: Reading from a script word-for-word. It sounds robotic. Instead, outline 3 bullet points (hook, value, CTA) and speak naturally. Retake if needed, but do not script every word.

Script Structure (30-60 Seconds)

▼
SectionDurationContentExample
Intro + Hook 10 sec Their name + specific reason you are reaching out. Reference something recent and relevant "Hey Sarah, I saw your post last week about scaling your SDR team from 5 to 15 reps - that caught my attention"
Value / Insight 20 sec One specific insight, pattern, or idea relevant to their situation. Reference a similar company or outcome "We helped a company in a similar stage - they were burning through SDRs with low conversion. We rebuilt their qualifying framework and their meeting set rate doubled in 6 weeks"
CTA 10 sec Clear, low-friction call to action. Offer a short call or send a resource "Happy to share what we did - would 15 min next week work? I will drop my calendar link in the message"

Tone guidelines

  • Conversational, not salesy - Imagine you are leaving a voicemail for a friend. Casual but professional
  • Confident but humble - "We have seen this work for similar teams" not "We are the best in the industry"
  • End on a question - "Does that sound relevant?" or "Worth a quick chat?" invites a reply
Length matters: 30-45 seconds is ideal. 60 seconds is the upper limit. Anything over 60 seconds and completion rate drops by 50%. Shorter is better.

Follow-Up Timing and Strategy

▼

When to follow up

ScenarioFollow-Up TimingMessage Type
No response after voice note 24-48 hours Short text message: "Hey Sarah - did you get a chance to listen? Happy to send a calendar link if it resonates"
They listened (if tracked via Loom) Same day or next morning "Saw you checked out the voice note - worth a quick chat this week?"
No response after first follow-up 4-5 days later Send a resource or article relevant to their challenge. Re-engage with value, not another ask
Still no response 7-10 days later Final breakup message: "Guessing this is not a priority right now - let me know if you want to revisit in Q3"

Follow-up medium

  • First follow-up: Text message (LinkedIn DM or email). Lower friction than another voice note
  • Second follow-up: Share a resource (article, case study, video). Provide value without asking
  • Final follow-up: Breakup email. Polite and non-pushy. Opens the door for them to re-engage later
Do not send multiple voice notes in a row. One voice note is a pattern interrupt. Two back-to-back voice notes feels aggressive. Follow up with text after the first voice note.

LinkedIn Voice Message Limits and Workarounds

▼

LinkedIn native voice message constraints

  • 1st connections only - You can only send voice messages to people you are already connected with. Cannot send to 2nd or 3rd degree connections
  • No analytics - LinkedIn does not show whether they played the voice note, how much they listened, or when
  • 60 second limit - Voice messages are capped at 1 minute. Cannot exceed this
  • No bulk sending - Must manually send each voice note. No automation via Sales Navigator or third-party tools

Workarounds for scale

  • For 2nd/3rd degree connections: Send a connection request with a short note. Once accepted, send the voice note immediately
  • For tracking: Use Loom or Otter to record the voice note, then send the trackable link via LinkedIn DM instead of using native voice
  • For bulk outreach: Record voice notes in batches (10-20 at a time), save as files, then manually attach to DMs. Tedious but scalable
Hybrid approach: Use LinkedIn native voice for your top 20 prospects (fully personalised, 1-on-1). Use Loom links via LinkedIn DM for the next 50-100 prospects (still personal but trackable).

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Reading from a script (sounds robotic)

When you read word-for-word, your tone becomes flat and unnatural. Prospects can tell you are reading, which kills authenticity
Outline 3 bullet points (hook, value, CTA) and speak naturally. Practice once, then record. Retake if needed, but do not read verbatim

2. Too long (over 60 seconds)

Completion rate drops by 50% after 60 seconds. Long voice notes signal you do not respect their time
Cap at 45-60 seconds. If you cannot say it in 60 seconds, your message is not clear enough. Edit ruthlessly

3. No personalisation (could be sent to anyone)

Generic voice notes are just templated emails in audio form. No hook = no attention = delete
Mention their name and a specific detail (recent post, company news, job change) in the first 10 seconds. Personalisation is the entire point of voice

4. Sending to cold prospects who have not connected

Voice notes require trust. Sending a voice note to someone who does not know you feels invasive, not personal
Connect first, then send voice. Or use voice as a follow-up after 1-2 text-based emails to warm prospects

5. No follow-up text message

They listen to your voice note, think it is interesting, but then forget. No text reminder = no action
Follow up 24-48 hours later with a short text: "Did you get a chance to listen? Happy to send a calendar link." Make it easy to reply

6. Bad audio quality (noisy background, unclear voice)

Poor audio quality signals low effort or unprofessionalism. If they cannot hear you clearly, they stop listening
Record in a quiet room. Test your audio first. Use headphones with a mic if needed. Clear audio is non-negotiable
