Tactic 5.2 - Event-Based Plays

Virtual Roundtable

Host 60-90 minute facilitated peer discussion sessions with 8-15 executives under Chatham House rules. Create structured conversation around specific challenge or opportunity where attendees learn from each other's approaches and experiences without attribution or pitching.

Effort: Medium Cost: Low-Medium ICP: C-suite, VP-level
01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Senior leaders struggle to find peer groups where they can discuss real challenges candidly. Virtual roundtables offer the peer learning value of an executive dinner with dramatically lower barriers - no travel, shorter time commitment, and Chatham House rules enable radical candor. Position yourself as trusted convener and you become part of their trusted circle.

The psychology behind virtual roundtables

  • Chatham House rules unlock honesty - Knowing insights can be shared but never attributed creates safety to discuss real challenges including mistakes and failures. This level of candor rarely happens in public forums
  • Structured peer learning - Facilitated format ensures everyone contributes and conversation stays productive. Not a webinar with Q&A but true peer exchange with expert moderation
  • Low commitment equals high attendance - 60-90 minutes from their desk is far easier commitment than half-day conference or evening dinner. Removes travel friction while preserving intimacy
  • Valuable network access - Attendees gain connections to peers facing similar challenges. Virtual format means geographic diversity - connect UK exec with US exec with Australian exec all in one session

Why virtual specifically

Virtual format democratises access. An executive in Scotland can join roundtable with London-based peers without train journey. Parent with childcare constraints can participate from home. Junior staff can observe and learn without intimidation of room presence.

The compounding effect: Virtual roundtables can become recurring series. First session builds foundation, subsequent sessions deepen relationships. Attendees start referencing each other between sessions. You become hub of valuable professional network.

04

Best Practices

▼

Topic Selection - Specific Beats Broad

▼

Why specificity matters

Broad topics attract everyone but engage no one. "Sales leadership" could mean anything. "Building sales playbooks that new AEs actually follow" speaks to specific pain and attracts people dealing with that exact challenge.

Topic selection criteria

  • Narrow enough to be actionable - Attendees should leave with specific ideas they can implement, not vague inspiration. Test: can you create 3-4 specific discussion questions from the topic?
  • Common but unsolved problem - If there is definitive answer everyone knows, no need for roundtable. Best topics are where smart people try different approaches and compare notes
  • Relevant to your solution without requiring it - Topic should relate to what you sell but conversation should not need your product. You want to seem knowledgeable not necessary
  • Timely and current - Tie to recent developments or emerging challenges. "Adapting sales comp plans in hybrid work era" beats generic "sales compensation design"

Topic ideas by persona

Target PersonaStrong Topic ExampleWhy It Works
Sales Leaders Transitioning AE teams from transactional to consultative selling Specific transformation many are attempting. Multiple valid approaches. Clear pain point
Marketing Leaders Building attribution models that sales actually trusts Cross-functional tension everyone faces. Technical enough to be interesting, political enough to need peer advice
Rev Ops Leaders Tech stack consolidation - what to keep vs cut in efficiency era Timely challenge. Tactical decisions with strategic implications. Everyone has war stories
Validation test: Before committing to topic, message 3-5 people in target audience asking "Would you attend 60-min peer discussion on X topic?" If less than 2 say yes immediately, pick different topic.

Participant Management - Size and Mix Matter

▼

Optimal size

8-15 participants is sweet spot. Fewer than 8 and conversation feels sparse if someone drops last minute. More than 15 and quiet people disappear. Aim for 10-12 confirmed registrations expecting 8-10 to actually attend.

Invitation strategy

  • Over-invite strategically - Send 25-30 invites to get 12-15 registrations. No-show rate typically 20-30% even with confirmations. Build waitlist for future sessions
  • Personal outreach beats mass email - First 10 invites should be personal LinkedIn messages or emails. Once you have core committed, can expand to broader invite
  • Name-drop confirmed attendees - After first few commit, mention to others "James from Acme and Sarah from another fintech have confirmed" to build FOMO
  • Set clear expectations upfront - Explain Chatham House rules, expected participation level, and no-pitch policy in invite. Self-selection reduces problem attendees

Participant mix considerations

DimensionBest PracticeWhy
Prospect vs Customer ratio 70% prospects, 30% happy customers Customers provide authentic perspective without feeling like sales plants. Majority should still be target prospects
Company stage Mix similar stages - all early stage or all scale-ups Series A founder and public company exec face different challenges. Conversation fragments if stages too diverse
Industry diversity Balance familiarity and fresh perspective All same industry risks echo chamber. Too diverse risks loss of relevance. 60% similar, 40% adjacent works well
Emergency backup plan: If registrations drop below 6 people two days before event, consider rescheduling. Better to move date than host sparse roundtable that disappoints attendees. Blame "scheduling conflicts" not low registration.

Facilitation Techniques - Drive Engagement Not Monologues

▼

Opening the session strong

First 5 minutes set tone for entire roundtable. Establish energy, safety, and expectations immediately. Boring intro kills momentum you will never recover.

Strong opening sequence

  • Start exactly on time - Waiting for stragglers punishes people who arrived on time. Start at stated time, latecomers catch up
  • Set ground rules clearly - Explain Chatham House rules explicitly: "Share insights freely but never attribute quotes to individuals or companies". Emphasise cameras on for engagement
  • Quick round of intros - Name, role, company, and answer one opening question related to topic. Keep to 30 seconds each. This gives everyone voice early
  • Dive into content fast - After 5-7 minutes of setup, launch into first real discussion question. Do not spend 20 minutes on logistics and intros

Facilitation moves during session

ChallengeFacilitation ResponseExample Language
Someone dominating Politely redirect to others "James, really valuable point. Before we go further, Sarah, how does your team handle this differently?"
Conversation going off-topic Acknowledge and redirect "Interesting tangent - let's park that and come back if time. On the original question about X..."
Awkward silence after question Call on someone specific "Sarah, I know you have dealt with this challenge. What approach did your team take?"
Too much agreement Surface dissent "We are hearing common themes. Anyone taking contrarian approach or seeing different results?"

Breakout room strategy

For 60-min roundtable, stay in main room. For 90-min session, consider 10-min breakout discussion in pairs or trios on specific question, then reconvene to share insights. Breakouts energise by changing format and ensuring quiet people contribute.

Energy management: Virtual attention spans are shorter than in-person. Every 20 minutes, shift format slightly - use poll, change discussion question angle, go to breakouts. Variety maintains engagement.

Follow-Up and Conversion - From Attendee to Opportunity

▼

Immediate post-event follow-up

Strike while memory is fresh. Attendees should receive something valuable within 48 hours that reinforces the quality of what they just experienced.

48-hour follow-up checklist

  • Summary of key insights - Synthesise 3-5 main themes discussed without attributing to specific people. Shows you were listening and provides shareable takeaway
  • Resources mentioned - Compile any articles, tools, or frameworks referenced during discussion. Add your own related content if genuinely relevant
  • Offer to connect participants - If two attendees had obvious synergy, offer to intro them. Facilitating valuable connections builds social capital
  • Feedback request - Ask what worked and what could improve. Shows you care about quality. Insights help improve next roundtable

Converting engagement to pipeline

TimingOutreach ApproachContext
3-5 days after Personalised value offer Reference their specific comments: "You mentioned struggling with X. We have helped similar companies with that. Would brief analysis be useful?"
1-2 weeks after Relevant content share Send case study or content piece directly addressing challenge they raised. Not sales pitch, just value-add with soft CTA
3-4 weeks after Next roundtable invite If running series, invite to next session. Recurring attendance builds relationship depth. Easier path than cold sales ask

When to hand to sales

If prospect responds positively to follow-up, explicitly asks questions about your solution, or requests introduction to someone who can help them, hand-off to sales team with context. Warm intros convert far better than cold prospecting.

Long game mindset: Not every attendee converts immediately. Some take 6-12 months. Keep them on nurture track with invites to future roundtables, relevant content, and occasional check-ins. Compound relationship-building over time.
05

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Turning roundtable into product demo

Attendees feel bait-and-switched when peer discussion becomes thinly veiled sales pitch. Trust evaporates immediately and they mentally check out even if they stay on call.
Strictly separate content from selling. Save all product discussion for follow-up conversations days later. Your restraint builds more credibility than any pitch. If attendees ask about your product, answer briefly and redirect to peer conversation.

2. Choosing too broad a topic

Generic topics like "sales enablement" or "customer success strategy" attract mixed audience with different needs. Conversation stays superficial because examples are not relevant to everyone.
Pick specific narrow topic that attracts right subset of audience. "Onboarding enterprise AEs in technical products" beats "sales enablement". Specificity drives relevance and depth.

3. Letting one person dominate conversation

Dominant talker makes it their personal soapbox. Other attendees tune out because they are not getting peer learning promised. Value proposition collapses.
Actively facilitate and redirect. "James, great point. Before we continue, let's hear from others who have not spoken yet." Direct questions to specific quiet people. Your job is to orchestrate balanced discussion.

4. Poor time management and running over

Running past stated end time disrespects attendees' schedules. People have hard stops and will drop off. Final 15 minutes of discussion wasted on dwindling audience.
Start exactly on time and end exactly on time. Build 5-min buffer in your plan. Give 10-min warning before end. Better to finish 2 min early leaving people wanting more than drag 15 min over.

5. No follow-up or generic mass follow-up

Attendees gave you 90 minutes and engaged authentically. Generic "thanks for attending" email wastes opportunity. Or worse, no follow-up means they forget you immediately.
Personalised follow-up within 48 hours referencing their specific contributions. Share promised resources. Offer to continue conversation on topics they raised. Show you were paying attention to them specifically.

6. Not enforcing cameras-on policy

Cameras-off reduces engagement dramatically. Cannot read body language. People multitask. Conversation loses energy and intimacy. Feels like bad webinar not peer roundtable.
Set expectation of cameras on in invite and again at start. If someone cannot, acknowledge briefly and move on. Model engagement yourself - be visible and present. Energy is contagious on video.
