Tactic 7.3 - ABM Plays

Multi-Threading (Buying Group)

Map the full buying committee at enterprise accounts and run coordinated parallel outreach with role-specific messaging. Enterprise purchases average 11 stakeholders, multi-threading ensures you reach all key decision makers simultaneously rather than relying on a single champion.

Effort: High Cost: Medium ICP: Enterprise Accounts
01

Why It Works

▼
The core insight: Enterprise deals die not from objections but from single-threading. When you rely on one champion to sell internally for you, the deal stalls the moment that person gets busy, changes role, or loses political capital. Multi-threading creates redundancy and direct relationships across the buying committee, dramatically improving close rates.

The psychology behind multi-threading

  • Social proof within organisation - When multiple stakeholders receive tailored outreach simultaneously, they start comparing notes internally. "Did you get that email from James too?" This internal buzz builds momentum
  • FOMO among decision makers - If the VP of Sales is engaging with you but the CRO has not been contacted, the CRO feels left out of important evaluation. Multi-threading ensures nobody feels excluded from strategic decisions
  • Political insurance - Enterprise politics are complex. Your champion might have detractors. By building relationships across the committee, you insulate the deal from internal political dynamics
  • Genuine consensus building - Different stakeholders have different concerns. Finance cares about ROI, Operations cares about implementation, End Users care about usability. Addressing each directly builds true buy-in not just champion enthusiasm

Why buying committees specifically

B2B enterprise purchases involve an average of 11 stakeholders according to Gartner research. Each stakeholder has veto power but nobody has unilateral approval authority. Your champion can love your solution, but if they cannot get Finance, IT, and Operations aligned, the deal does not happen. Multi-threading gives you direct line to each voice that matters.

The amplification effect: When you engage multiple stakeholders with tailored messaging, you effectively run multiple parallel sales processes that reinforce each other. The VP of Engineering hears about technical benefits while the CFO hears about ROI. Both conclude independently that this makes sense, strengthening overall deal momentum.

Best Practices

Committee Mapping - Identify All Key Roles

▼

Typical enterprise buying committee roles

Enterprise software purchases typically involve 6-12 stakeholders across multiple functions. Understanding standard buying committee roles helps you systematically identify who needs to be included rather than accidentally missing critical stakeholders.

Standard buying committee personas

RoleTypical TitlePrimary ConcernInfluence Type
Economic Buyer CFO, VP Finance, Budget Owner ROI, budget impact, contract terms Final approval authority on spend. Can kill deal but rarely champions alone
Executive Sponsor CXO level, VP reporting to C-suite Strategic alignment, organisational impact Political air cover. Protects deal from internal objections. Essential for large strategic purchases
Technical Buyer CTO, VP Engineering, IT Director Technical feasibility, security, integration Veto power on technical grounds. Must approve architecture and security. Can block if not satisfied
Champion/Coach Manager, Director, Department Head Solving their specific pain, looking good internally Internal advocate who sells for you. Navigates politics and builds consensus. Most deals have a champion
End User Representative Team Lead, Power User, Department Rep Usability, day-to-day workflow impact User adoption risk. If end users push back, implementation fails even if deal closes
Operations/Implementation RevOps, IT Operations, Change Management Implementation complexity, change management, support burden Can slow or block deal based on implementation concerns. Critical for successful deployment

Research tactics to map committee

  • LinkedIn org search - Search company name plus job title keywords like "finance director," "engineering vp," "operations lead" to identify stakeholders
  • Company website - Leadership page and team pages often list key decision makers with bios and contact information
  • Ask your champion - Once you have one engaged contact, ask directly: "Who else should be involved in evaluating this?" Most champions will map the committee for you
  • Past deal patterns - Look at closed deals at similar companies. Which roles were involved? Use past patterns to predict current committee composition
Completeness check: If your mapped buying committee has fewer than 5 people for an enterprise deal, you probably have not found everyone. Keep researching. Missing stakeholders appear late in the process and derail deals.

Role-Specific Messaging - Different Roles Different Value Props

▼

Why one message to all stakeholders fails

The CFO does not care about API architecture. The CTO does not care about finance team approval workflows. The Operations lead does not care about board-level strategy. Generic messaging that tries to appeal to everyone ends up resonating with nobody. Role-specific messaging acknowledges each stakeholder's unique priorities.

Messaging frameworks by role

Stakeholder RoleMessage FocusProof Points to Include
CFO / Finance ROI, payback period, budget efficiency, cost savings vs current approach Customer ROI data, TCO analysis, payback timeline, efficiency gains from similar companies
CTO / Technical Architecture, security, scalability, integration complexity, technical risk Technical architecture docs, security certifications, integration examples, uptime SLAs
Executive Sponsor Strategic alignment, competitive advantage, transformation impact, organisational change Executive case studies, industry analyst reports, strategic outcomes from peer companies
Operations Implementation timeline, change management, training requirements, support model Implementation case studies, typical timelines, training programmes, support SLAs
End Users Ease of use, daily workflow improvements, time savings, reduced manual work Product demos, user testimonials, before-after workflow comparisons, training resources

Example message variations

To CFO: "Sarah, companies like Acme typically see 3-to-1 ROI within first year by reducing manual finance reconciliation time. Happy to walk through the business case."

To CTO: "James, wanted to share how Acme could leverage your existing Salesforce and Snowflake investments through our native integrations. No rip-and-replace required."

To Operations Lead: "Noticed Acme is scaling quickly. Our typical implementation takes 6 weeks with minimal disruption. Happy to share how similar companies managed the change."

Consistency requirement: While messaging must be tailored by role, core positioning and benefits should remain consistent. Stakeholders will compare notes. If you tell Finance one thing and Engineering something contradictory, you lose credibility across the committee.

Timing Coordination - Synchronise Touchpoints

▼

Why timing matters for multi-threading

If you contact the CFO in January and the CTO in March, they have no reason to connect your outreach internally. When multiple stakeholders receive tailored outreach within the same week, they naturally discuss it with each other, creating organic internal momentum and making your company a topic of conversation in executive meetings.

Coordination strategies

  • Launch window approach - Send initial outreach to all 3-5 key stakeholders within 48-hour window. Tight enough timing that they compare notes, loose enough that it does not feel like spam
  • Wave sequencing - Launch Wave 1 to primary stakeholders on Monday. If no response by Wednesday, launch Wave 2 to secondary stakeholders. Creates multiple chances for internal conversation
  • Event-triggered coordination - When one stakeholder engages such as replying or taking a meeting, use that as trigger to reach back out to other stakeholders with updated message referencing the engagement
  • Campaign themes - Use consistent theme or hook across messages to different stakeholders so when they compare, the coordination is obvious and intentional not accidental

Transparency in timing

Be explicit about your multi-stakeholder approach. In your messages, mention that you are also reaching out to other relevant stakeholders. This transparency prevents concerns about going behind anyone's back and actually accelerates internal alignment.

Example transparent language: "Sarah, given this impacts both Finance and Operations, I am also reaching out to James in your Ops team to ensure we address all relevant considerations. Happy to coordinate a joint conversation if that would be more efficient."

Calendar coordination tip: When multiple stakeholders respond positively, immediately suggest group discovery call rather than separate 1-on-1 meetings. "Would it make sense to get you, James, and Sarah together for 30 minutes rather than three separate calls?" This consolidates your selling effort and ensures alignment from the start.

Champion vs Committee - Do Not Ignore Your Champion

▼

The champion relationship dynamic

Multi-threading does not mean abandoning your champion or going around them. Your champion is still critical for navigating internal politics and driving consensus. Multi-threading supplements and strengthens the champion relationship by building supporting relationships that reduce pressure on your champion to sell alone.

How to multi-thread without alienating champion

  • Inform before action - Tell your champion before reaching out to other stakeholders: "I would like to connect with Sarah in Finance to address any budget questions. Does that make sense? Anyone else I should include?"
  • Position as support - Frame multi-threading as helping your champion succeed: "I want to make sure you have all the internal support you need. Let me handle the technical conversation with James so you do not have to."
  • Share insights back - When other stakeholders engage, loop your champion in on what you learned: "Spoke with James. He is concerned about implementation timeline. Thought you should know so we can address it together."
  • Champion stays primary - Your champion should still be your main point of contact and strategic advisor. Other relationships are important but supplementary

When champion resists multi-threading

Some champions resist your attempts to engage other stakeholders, either from insecurity or desire to control the process. This is actually a red flag. True champions with political capital are confident having you engage other stakeholders. Resistance often signals the champion lacks influence to drive the deal.

Champion ResponseWhat It SignalsHow to Handle
"Let me handle internal coordination" Possible insecurity or limited influence Probe gently: "Who else will need to approve this decision? Happy to support those conversations however helpful."
"You should not contact anyone without my approval" Control issues or fear of exposure Respect boundaries but qualify deal risk: "Help me understand the approval process. Who has veto power?"
"Sure, let me introduce you to X and Y" Healthy champion with real influence Ideal scenario. Leverage champion introductions to build additional relationships
Risk mitigation: If your champion blocks all attempts at multi-threading and you cannot get access to other stakeholders, the deal is at high risk. Champion-only deals have much lower close rates and longer sales cycles. Consider whether to invest further or prioritise other opportunities.

Common Mistakes

▼

1. Same message to all stakeholders

Sending identical generic message to CFO, CTO, and Operations Lead shows you have not thought about their individual priorities. Everyone ignores it.
Create role-specific messaging addressing each stakeholder's unique concerns. CFO gets ROI message, CTO gets technical architecture message, Operations gets implementation message. Tailor every outreach.

2. Not mapping the full committee upfront

You engage one or two people then discover late in sales cycle that there are five other stakeholders with veto power you never contacted. Deal stalls or dies.
Invest time upfront mapping full buying committee before first outreach. Identify 6-10 potential stakeholders across functions. Prioritise 3-5 for initial outreach but know who else exists.

3. Reaching only one person - single threading

You find a champion and stop there. Champion gets busy, leaves company, or loses political capital. Deal dies with them because you have no other relationships.
Always build relationships with at least 3 stakeholders minimum at enterprise accounts. Champion plus two others. Creates redundancy and insulates deal from single point of failure.

4. Not coordinating timing of touches

You contact CFO in January, CTO in March, Operations in May. Random uncoordinated timing means stakeholders never discuss your outreach internally. No momentum builds.
Synchronise initial outreach to all key stakeholders within 48-hour window. Creates natural internal conversation. Follow-up touches should also be coordinated in waves.

5. Ignoring champions at mid-level

You only target C-suite and VP-level stakeholders, ignoring Director or Manager-level champions who often drive deals from within. Miss key advocates.
Include mid-level stakeholders who will be daily users or implementation leads. They often have strong influence on decision and can champion effectively despite not being C-suite.

6. Going around champion without communication

You reach out to other stakeholders without telling your champion. Champion feels blindsided and loses trust in you. Relationship damaged.
Always inform champion before multi-threading. Position it as supporting them not going around them. Get their input on who to contact and how. Make them ally in multi-threading not victim.
